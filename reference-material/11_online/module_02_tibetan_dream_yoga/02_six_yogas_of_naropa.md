# Module 2, Lesson 2 — The Six Yogas of Naropa as a System

*The most institutionally stable Tibetan dream-yoga lineage runs through the Six Yogas of Naropa — Tibetan* Nā-ro chos drug *— "the six dharmas of Naropa."*

---

## The standard list

The standard list comprises *tummo* (inner heat), *gyulü* (illusory body), *milam* (dream), *ösel* (clear light), *bardo* (intermediate state), and *phowa* (transference of consciousness).

The system, Glenn Mullin observes in *The Six Yogas of Naropa*, was not originally referred to as "six" anything. In the early Tibetan period there were "traditions of speaking of two, three, four, six, eight and ten yogas," and the title was probably applied retroactively, most likely by Gampopa, "whose work produced the Dakpo Kargyu school" (Mullin, *Six Yogas of Naropa*).

What the system actually does is more important than what it is called.

## The architecture

Mullin, paraphrasing Tsongkhapa's *A Book of Three Inspirations*, describes the architecture this way.

*Tummo* — the inner-heat practice — is the foundation, "the foundation stone," because it is the technology by which the practitioner first learns to "draw the subtle life-sustaining energies into the central channel." Once those energies enter, abide, and dissolve in the central channel, "the four blisses are induced," and on the basis of that altered energetic state every subsequent yoga becomes possible.

*Gyulü* — illusory body — and *ösel* — clear light — are then the two principal practices for inducing the experience of enlightenment.

*Milam* — dream yoga — sits as a kind of nighttime extension of illusory body. *Bardo* and *phowa* are the practices that take the work into the death-process if liberation has not been attained in life.

**Tsongkhapa puts the relation crisply, in Mullin's translation:**

> "*Only when progress in dream yoga has been made firm can one effectively work with the bardo yogas.* Thus here again [with the bardo yogas] the foundation stone is the power achieved through the inner heat" (Glenn H. Mullin, trans., *The Six Yogas of Naropa*, Snow Lion; Tsongkhapa's *A Book of Three Inspirations*).

The system has, in other words, a logic. The energetic foundation enables the contemplative recognitions; the recognitions worked out in the daytime body become available in the nighttime body; the recognitions worked out in the nighttime body become available in the bardo of dying.

**Each yoga is a stage of training for what comes next. None is freestanding.**

## The lineage in traceable persons

The lineage Mullin follows runs through traceable persons.

**Tilopa** (988–1069) — the Indian *mahāsiddha* who, according to the traditional account, received the teachings from Vajradhara in direct visionary transmission and codified them into the system that bears Naropa's name.

**Naropa** (956–1041) — Tilopa's principal disciple, abbot of the great Indian Buddhist university of Nalanda before he left it to follow Tilopa, and the figure under whose name the consolidated system reached Tibet.

**Marpa Lotsawa** (1012–1097) — the great Tibetan translator who made three trips to India to receive the teachings from Naropa, brought the system back into Tibetan, and established the Kagyu lineage there.

**Milarepa** (c. 1040–1123) — Marpa's most celebrated disciple, the cotton-clad yogi who completed the transmission of *tummo* through years of solitary mountain retreat, and whose songs of realization remain among the most beloved texts in the Tibetan tradition.

**Gampopa** (1079–1153) — Milarepa's principal monastic disciple, the figure whose work consolidated the lineage into the institutional form known as the Dakpo Kagyu.

From there the transmission ran through Pal Pakmo Drupa and Drikung Jigten Sumgon into the four older and eight younger Kagyu schools, with subsequent transmission to the Sakya, the Zhalu, and finally to **Tsongkhapa** (1357–1419), founder of the Geluk school, "fourteenth generation in the Tibetan line of transmission" — the first generation, by Naropa's own instruction, "free to write extensively on the system."

Until that point, Naropa had stipulated in his *Vajra Verses of the Whispered Tradition* that the teachings be passed only by ear, "for thirteen generations in Tibet," with secrecy enforced (Mullin, paraphrasing Naropa's colophon).

## The tantric sources

The original sources, Tsongkhapa explains and Mullin translates, are tantric scriptures attributed to the Buddha himself:

> "The instruction on the inner heat yoga comes to us through Tilopa, who commented that this was the transmission of the mahasiddha Krishnacharya... which fuses [the teachings on inner heat yoga found in] the Hevajra Tantra and the Heruka Chakrasamvara Tantra. *The illusory body and the clear light doctrines are derived from the oral tradition teachings of the Guhyasamaja Tantra.*" (Mullin trans., *The Six Yogas of Naropa*, Snow Lion)

Dream yoga, treated as a stage of illusory body in Tsongkhapa's structure, draws on these same sources together with what Tsongkhapa calls *The Five Stages* and *The Four Seats*.

## The parallel Niguma lineage

A parallel transmission of related material reached Tibet through **Khyungpo Naljor** (c. 990–1140), who studied with **Niguma** — variously described in the sources as Naropa's sister or his consort — and brought back the Six Yogas of Niguma.

The Naropa and Niguma traditions of the Six Yogas, Mullin notes, diverge on certain technical details but cover the same basic architecture. The Shangpa lineage that descends from Khyungpo Naljor has survived into the present principally through Kalu Rinpoche (1905–1989) and his successors, and is the subsidiary stream within which the Sukhasiddhi yogas are also preserved.

For the modern Western reader, the Niguma lineage is less visible than the Naropa one, but it is part of the same Indian inheritance and the same broad practice field.

## What the lineage establishes

The figures behind these lineages are the Indian *mahāsiddhas* — "the great accomplished ones" who flourished between roughly the seventh and twelfth centuries CE. Their hagiographies, preserved in Tibetan, are not modern history; they encode the cultural understanding that dream was a legitimate channel for the transmission of teaching. That understanding, more than the specific dates and biographies, is what migrated into Tibet.

**What the lineage establishes, then, is that dream yoga is one of six interlocking practices, with prior energetic foundations and subsequent extensions into the death-process, transmitted through traceable persons, drawn from named tantric scriptures, and stabilized over a thousand years of monastic and yogic transmission.**
