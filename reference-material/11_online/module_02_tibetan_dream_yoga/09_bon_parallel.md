# Module 2, Lesson 9 — The Bön Parallel

*A separate origin, a parallel arrival. The Bön Dzogchen tradition and the Buddhist Nyingma Dzogchen tradition arrived independently at a remarkably close set of practice instructions for dream and sleep.*

---

## A separate institutional lineage

The Bön tradition is institutionally distinct from Tibetan Buddhism. It traces itself to **Tonpa Shenrab Miwoche**, "the founder figure of Bön, traditionally said to have lived thousands of years before the Buddha Shakyamuni," and to a homeland west of Tibet that the texts call Olmo Lung Ring, traditionally identified with Zhang Zhung.

In Tenzin Wangyal's biography, given in the preface to *Tibetan Yogas of Dream and Sleep*, the Bön transmission of dream practice runs through the *Zhang Zhung Nyan Gyud* — the Oral Transmission of Zhang Zhung — together with the *Mother Tantra* and the writings of the nineteenth-century Bön master Shardza Rinpoche.

## The dream that admitted Tenzin Wangyal

Tenzin Wangyal's own pedagogical lineage is direct. He was ordained at the main Bön monastery in Dolanji, India, recognized as the reincarnation of Khyungtul Rinpoche, and at age thirteen received the *Zhang Zhung Nyan Gyud* teachings from Lopon Sangye Tenzin.

The dream that admitted him to those teachings — handed in by every prospective student the night before instruction began, "so that he might determine our readiness" — turned on a Tibetan syllable A, whose significance as a Dzogchen symbol the young Tenzin Wangyal did not yet understand.

Fifteen years later, teaching in the West, he was passing out small pieces of paper marked with the same syllable when "*the dream from fifteen years before, in which I passed out the same paper to people getting on the bus, came back to me. It was as if it hit me on the head*" (Tenzin Wangyal, *Tibetan Yogas of Dream and Sleep*).

The story does the historical work of establishing that the Bön tradition's relation to dream is operational, not merely doctrinal. Dreams are taken by Bön teachers as data — about a student's readiness, about practice obstacles, about appropriate next steps.

Tenzin Wangyal's mother, his teacher Lopon Tenzin Namdak, and the older monks at Dolanji all worked with their dreams as a normal feature of the contemplative life. The text's preface returns repeatedly to this point: in Tibetan Bön culture, "*information from the unconscious is often of greater value than the information the conscious mind can provide*."

## How close the two traditions actually are

For the practices the broader Svapna curriculum will eventually teach, the differences between Bön and Buddhist Tibetan dream yoga are smaller than the surface differences in vocabulary suggest.

**The companion notes to *Tibetan Yogas of Dream and Sleep* state the relation clearly:**

> "to a non-specialist eye they can look very similar — both have lineages of masters, formal monastic institutions, tantric practices, and Dzogchen teachings. *The differences are real but are mostly differences of historical pedigree and ritual specifics rather than philosophical content for the practices we are studying*" (companion notes, project archive).

Where Buddhist practitioners visualize Padmasambhava or Vajrayoginī, Bön practitioners visualize Tapihritsa or Shenla Odker, or use the dakini Salgye Du Dalma in the sleep-yoga visualization.

The function — the establishment of a stable form for awakened awareness, into which the practitioner's own awareness can eventually be recognized — is the same.

## The Mother Tantra's image-pedagogy

What is *distinctive* about the Bön presentation, and worth flagging here, is the extent to which the *Mother Tantra* foregrounds the metaphors that the Buddhist Six Yogas treatments tend to use only in passing.

The opening Part One of Tenzin Wangyal's book is structured around five images from the Mother Tantra — the lamp in the dark room, the reflection in the mirror, the lightning illuminating mountains, the rainbow, the moon reflected in many waters — given as pedagogical instruments rather than illustrations.

The point of the metaphors is operational: "*We must go beyond the image, but it can point us in the right direction.*" The practitioner is asked to *let the image work* on the senses and the body, rather than to extract a doctrine from it.

**The reading instruction for the Mother Tantra material is given in Tenzin Wangyal's own voice, and is unusually explicit about the kind of attention the practice asks for:**

> When we enter a house illuminated by a lamp we do not examine the lamp, the wick, and the oil. We just experience the luminosity of the room. Try to do the same with teaching metaphors. Our minds, trained to work with abstractions and logic, seize a metaphor and analyze it. *We ask too much of the metaphor.*

This pedagogical mode — direct, image-anchored, body-trusting — is one of the things that has made Tenzin Wangyal's book the most-read English-language entry point to Tibetan dream and sleep practice.

It is also, the lineage notes are careful to add, ecumenical within the broader Tibetan world. Tenzin Wangyal uses Bön terminology where it differs (Tapihritsa instead of Padmasambhava, Shenla Odker instead of various Buddhist deities) and explains that practitioners with Buddhist connections should substitute their own equivalents.

## What the parallel arrival demonstrates

The structural fact this lesson is built around is worth holding in mind.

**Two contemplative lineages, with separate origin myths, different founding figures, different deity sets, different ritual calendars, different institutional histories, arrived at a remarkably close set of practice instructions for dream and sleep.**

The convergence is not because one tradition borrowed from the other — the historical record does not support that, and both traditions resist the framing — but because the underlying phenomenology of the practice converges.

Practitioners working seriously and independently on what happens at the threshold of sleep, in the dream-state, in the deep-sleep state, in the dissolution into clear light, end up describing what they encounter in compatible ways.

This is one of the strongest pieces of evidence in the contemporary practice landscape that the practices are pointing at something real. Two lineages, separately developed, with different vocabularies and different ritual frames, agree on the structure of the territory.

The structural rhyme between them is what gives the contemporary practitioner permission to read them together as evidence about the same phenomenon.

A practitioner trained in either, encountering the other, would recognize most of what they found.
