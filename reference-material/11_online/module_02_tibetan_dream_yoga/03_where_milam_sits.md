# Module 2, Lesson 3 — Where Milam Sits Among the Six Yogas

*It matters that milam is not a stand-alone discipline in the early Kagyu and Geluk treatments. The point on which the contemporary lucid-dreaming reader most often goes wrong.*

---

## Dream yoga as illusory body

**Mullin makes the structural point explicit:**

> Most Kagyu commentators, he writes, "drop out the fifth yoga — that of forceful projection into another body — because the Naropa lineage of this yoga allegedly was lost with the death of Marpa's son Darma Dodey. They make up the list of six by separating dream yoga from the illusory body yogas and treat it by itself. *Tsongkhapa, however, like Pal Pakmo Drupa, taught dream yoga as one of the three stages of the illusory body training*" (Mullin, *Six Yogas of Naropa*).

This is the structural fact worth holding. In the older systematization, dream yoga is an *illusory body* practice — a way of recognizing experientially, in a state where the dream-body and dream-environment are visibly mind-made, that all experience has the same character.

The waking world is no less a projection than the night's dream; it is only that the karmic traces stabilizing it are denser, and the projection runs at a slower frame rate.

## Lucidity as entry, not prize

**Tsongkhapa, again paraphrasing through Mullin:**

> "*merely recognizing that the horses, elephants and so forth that are seen in dreams are illusions will not give rise to the wisdom of emptiness*, in the same way that realizing something to be an illusion in the waking state, such as one's reflection in a mirror, does not give rise to realization of emptiness. One must combine the technique of dream yoga with the instructions on the methods of ascertaining the meaning of emptiness." (Mullin trans., *The Six Yogas of Naropa*, Snow Lion)

The recognition that one is dreaming — what the Western lucid-dream literature calls *lucidity*, the basic precondition of the practice — is not, by itself, the practice. It is the entry.

Tsongkhapa is explicit. Recognizing that the horses and elephants of the dream are illusions does not, on its own, give rise to the wisdom of emptiness. The recognition is necessary but not sufficient.

Without the instruction on emptiness — without, that is, the philosophical and contemplative training that lets the practitioner read the recognition for what it is evidence of — the lucid moment is just an interesting nighttime experience.

**Lucidity, in this older frame, is the entry condition, not the prize.**

## Where the contemporary reader misreads

This is the place where the contemporary practitioner approaching the material from the Western lucid-dreaming literature most often misreads the tradition. The Western literature has, by historical accident, treated lucidity as the destination.

State checks, dream-signs, MILD, WBTB — the entire technical apparatus is organized around the production of the lucid moment. Once the moment is produced, the literature turns to applications: dream-creativity, dream-sex, dream-flying, dream-rehearsal, dream-therapy.

Each of those applications is locally defensible. None of them is what dream yoga is for.

**Dream yoga is for the recognition that the apparent solidity of phenomena, dream and waking alike, is a function of consciousness rather than the other way around.**

The lucid moment is the entry. What the practitioner does *inside* the lucid moment is the practice. And what the practitioner does inside the lucid moment, in the Tibetan tradition, is the work of illusory-body recognition: deliberate, repeated demonstration, in a state where the demonstration is unanswerable, that dream phenomena have no inherent existence from their own side.

This is the move that the contemporary Western teachers — Wallace, Holecek, Wangyal — work to keep visible to a readership that has come to dream yoga through the Western lucid-dream on-ramp. The on-ramp is real. The road it leads to is not the same road as the on-ramp itself.

## The three stages of illusory-body practice

There is a parallel point worth marking. In the Geluk treatment that Mullin and Tsongkhapa give, dream yoga sits not just as one of the six but as the second of three illusory-body practices.

The first is the daytime illusory-body practice — what Padmasambhava calls "powerfully imagine that your environment, city, house, companions, conversation, and all activities are a dream; and even say out loud, '*This is a dream*.'"

The dream-yoga practice proper is the second stage, in which the practitioner takes the illusory-body recognition into actual dreams. The third stage is the bardo illusory-body practice, in which the recognition is brought into the death-process bardos.

The three stages move the same recognition through three increasingly difficult conditions.

In daytime, the practitioner has the most stable conditions but the most habitual conviction in the solidity of the world. In dream, the world is less solid (because more obviously mind-made) but the practitioner is normally less aware. In the bardo, the world is most fluid (no body to anchor perception) but the conditions are the hardest the practitioner has ever faced.

**Training in the milder conditions is what makes the recognition available in the harder ones.**

This is the architecture. Dream yoga is the middle term, connected by design to what precedes and follows it. The practice that immediately precedes it (daytime illusory-body) and the practice that immediately follows it (bardo illusory-body) determine what the dream yoga is doing. Without the daytime practice, the lucid moment in dream has no prior preparation; without the bardo orientation, the lucid moment in dream has nowhere it is going.

## Two framings, one conviction

The Dzogchen treatment, transmitted through the Nyingma stream and the Bön — which the next lesson begins to introduce, and which Lesson 5 takes up in detail — frames the architecture differently.

Dzogchen does not center the illusory-body sequence in the same way. It centers, instead, the recognition of *rigpa* — pristine awareness, the awareness running through all states — and treats dream yoga as one application of that prior recognition rather than as a stage in a sequence.

The two framings are compatible (most contemporary teachers move freely between them), but they are not identical, and the practitioner who goes through the Geluk treatment of the Six Yogas and the Dzogchen treatment of natural light should expect to encounter the same practice in two different conceptual registers.

**What both share — and what every Tibetan source on dream practice shares — is the conviction that lucidity is not the prize. Lucidity is the door.**
