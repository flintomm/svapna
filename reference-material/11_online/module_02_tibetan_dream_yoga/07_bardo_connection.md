# Module 2, Lesson 7 — Dream Practice and the Bardo

*Every Tibetan source on dream yoga places the practice inside the framework of the bardos. The reasoning is structural rather than metaphorical.*

---

## The transitions are analogous

The Tibetan tradition treats the transitions of consciousness as analogous. Falling asleep parallels dying. The dream-state parallels the after-death *bardo of dharmatā* and *bardo of becoming*. Waking parallels rebirth.

**What is trained in the milder transition (sleep) becomes available in the harder one (death).**

Tsongkhapa puts the principle directly, in Mullin's translation:

> "*if one is able to recognize the clear light of sleep during one's lifetime practice, then at the time of death one will be able to recognize the clear light of the moment of death.* Moreover, if one is able to control the subtle energies in the dream state, one will be able to control them in the bardo." (Mullin trans., *The Six Yogas of Naropa*, Snow Lion)

Khenchen Thrangu, in *Journey of the Mind*, develops the same point in plainer Mahāmudrā register. What happens in the dream-state and the bardo-state, on the testimony of the mahasiddhas, runs primarily on two factors: the momentum of accumulated habit and the strength of the practitioner's resolve. The everyday illustration Thrangu reaches for is the resolution to wake at a particular hour — the intention, sincerely held before sleep, carries through the dissolution of consciousness and surfaces the body at the appointed time. The same mechanism applies to the resolution to recognize a dream as a dream while it is being dreamt. Thrangu draws the bardo conclusion plainly: "*the preparation for the bardo of becoming is the same*" (Thrangu, *Journey of the Mind*).

The practice and the bardo are tied by the simple fact that both run on momentum of habit. What the practitioner trains in the bardo of dreams is a strengthened tendency that becomes available in the bardo of dying.

The connection rests on a simple recognition: **the mind on the threshold of sleep and the mind on the threshold of death share a common architecture.**

## The Bardo Thödol on the bardo of dreams

The *Bardo Thödol* — the *Liberation Through Hearing in the Bardo*, in the West known as the *Tibetan Book of the Dead* — encodes this view in liturgical form.

In Francesca Fremantle and Chögyam Trungpa's translation, the text enumerates "six bardos: the bardo of birth, the bardo of dreams, the bardo of samādhi-meditation, the bardo of the moment before death, the bardo of dharmatā and the bardo of becoming."

The bardo of dreams is one stop on a list whose other stops are the death-process bardos. They are, structurally, the same kind of state.

**The famous opening verse of the bardo of dreams in Fremantle and Trungpa is the most direct evidence available of how the tradition itself wanted dream practice held in mind:**

> Now when the bardo of dreams is dawning upon me,
> I will abandon the corpse-like sleep of careless ignorance,
> and let my thoughts enter their natural state without distraction;
> *controlling and transforming dreams in luminosity*,
> I will not sleep like any animal
> but unify completely sleep and practice. (trans. Francesca Fremantle and Chögyam Trungpa, *The Tibetan Book of the Dead*; same edition for the bardo-of-dharmatā verse and the prose passage that follow.)

The verse for the bardo of dharmatā that follows uses, deliberately, the same orientation:

> Now when the bardo of dharmatā dawns upon me,
> I will abandon all thoughts of fear and terror,
> *I will recognize whatever appears as my projection*
> and know it to be a vision of the bardo;
> now that I have reached this crucial point,
> I will not fear the peaceful and wrathful ones, my own
>      projections.

The instruction to *recognize whatever appears as my projection* is, structurally, the same instruction the dream-yogi gives himself nightly.

Fremantle and Trungpa's prose passage at the moment of dharmatā makes the equivalence explicit, addressed to the dying person:

> "whatever sounds, colors and rays of light occur, they cannot hurt you and you cannot die. *It is enough simply to recognize them as your projections.* Know this to be the bardo state." (Fremantle and Trungpa, *The Tibetan Book of the Dead*)

The dream-yoga practitioner, in life, is rehearsing exactly this recognition.

**Tenzin Wangyal collapses the structural relation into a single line: *We say that dream is a test for the bardo*.**

This is what is at stake when the Tibetan tradition speaks of dream yoga as a preparation for the death-process. The training is the same training. Only the conditions under which it will eventually be performed differ.

## The contemporary practitioner's question

A practitioner approaching the material from a contemporary Western frame may find this the part of the tradition that is hardest to inhabit. The bardo doctrine — that consciousness continues after the dissolution of the brain, that there is an intermediate state, that the conditions of that state are determined in part by the practitioner's prior training — is not a claim that contemporary Western science can adjudicate.

Wallace, in the bridge text Module 6 returns to, is direct about this. The standard scientific apparatus has no procedure for adjudicating the claim that dream practice prepares for the bardo. The most a careful scientist of good will can do is hold the claim as a contemplative report — registering it as such, and refraining from either inflating or deflating it.

The contemporary practitioner therefore has to decide how to hold the question. Three positions are visible in the contemporary teaching landscape, and each is defensible.

### The full traditional position

**The full traditional position** holds that the bardo is real, that consciousness continues, that rebirth is the standard outcome of the death process for unenlightened beings, and that dream yoga is preparation for the actual after-death state.

This is the position Wallace argues for, with some force, in the polemical chapters of *Dreaming Yourself Awake*. He holds that the motivation that sustains the practice over decades requires this framing.

### The metaphorical position

**The metaphorical position** holds that the bardo language is a powerful contemplative tool whose claim to literal truth is bracketed.

The practitioner uses the language because it works on the practice — it gives the dream-state its weight, it organizes the practitioner's relation to dying, it stabilizes the daily orientation — without committing to its metaphysical literalism.

This is the position many contemporary Western teachers, including Holecek to some degree, occupy in practice.

### The agnostic position

**The agnostic position** holds that the question is genuinely open, that contemporary evidence does not settle it, that the practice is worth doing on either reading of its metaphysical commitments, and that the practitioner can let the bardo question remain unresolved while doing the practice fully.

### Where the three positions land

Wallace would say the agnostic position is more costly than its holders realize — that the motivation that sustains the practice over decades attenuates without the framing. The agnostic would say the practice is worth doing on its own terms regardless. The metaphorical position would say the bardo language is doing real work in the practice even when its literal status is bracketed.

The work's framing tends toward the middle position — holding the bardo framing seriously while leaving full doctrinal commitment as an open matter. The question is genuinely open, and is part of what each contemporary practitioner has to work out for themselves.

## The structural claim is unanimous

**What is not in question, on any reading, is the *structural* claim.**

The mind on the threshold of sleep and the mind on the threshold of death share a common architecture. Whatever the death-state turns out to be, the practitioner who has trained awareness through the dissolutions of every night for years is better prepared for it than the practitioner who has not.

This much the tradition is saying with force, and on this much the contemporary teacher community has reached something like consensus.
