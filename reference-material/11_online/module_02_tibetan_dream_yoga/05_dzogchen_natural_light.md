# Module 2, Lesson 5 — The Dzogchen Alternative: The Practice of Natural Light

*The Dzogchen treatment, transmitted in the Nyingma school and in Bön, frames the practice differently — and for the modern Western reader, perhaps more accessibly. Lucid dream is a secondary practice. The principal practice is the practice of natural light.*

---

## Lucid dream as secondary practice

**Chögyal Namkhai Norbu, in *Dream Yoga and the Practice of Natural Light*, makes the distinction explicit at the start of his second chapter:**

> When someone says "practice of the night" we usually think of the practice of lucid dreaming. There are many explanations of lucid dreaming. *But in the Dzogchen teaching, the practice of dream work, and development of lucidity, is not fundamental. It is a secondary practice.* In the case of dream practice, secondary means that this practice can arise spontaneously or automatically from doing the principal practice, which is called the "practice of natural light."

The "practice of natural light" — Tibetan *ösel* — operates earlier than the dream. It works the *transition* into sleep itself.

**Namkhai Norbu's account is precise:**

> This practice, the practice of the natural light, actually has to do with the state prior to dream. For example, a person falls asleep; fall asleep means that all of his senses vanish into him, and thus he is sleeping. From that point on there is a passage, a period of transition, until dreams begin. That period may be long or it may be short.... *That which is called the state of natural light is not a moment or a state in which the mind is functioning. It is the period beginning when you fell asleep and ending when the mind begins to function again.*

The Dzogchen claim is that if the practitioner can carry awareness through that transition — meet the moment when the senses dissolve and the mind has not yet reactivated — then lucidity in the dream that follows arises by itself, "spontaneously."

The practitioner does not need to chase the dream; the awareness is already present in the dream because it never lapsed.

**Namkhai Norbu's formulation:**

> When one has an awareness of the presence of this state of natural light, then even if afterwards the state of dreams arises, *one spontaneously becomes lucidly aware that one is dreaming while dreaming, and automatically one achieves mastery of one's dreams.* This means that the dream does not condition the person, but the person governs his or her dream. For this reason, the practice of dreams is secondary, and I cannot overemphasize how extremely important it is to do the practice of the natural light.

## How the Dzogchen framing differs from the Geluk

This is a genuinely different framing from the Geluk treatment of the previous lesson.

The Geluk treatment teaches the practitioner to enter the dream consciously by a sequence of techniques (resolution, breath, throat-chakra visualization), to work within the dream through transformation and illusory-body practices, and to rest in the suchness that emerges as the dream dissolves into clear light.

The Dzogchen treatment teaches the practitioner to maintain awareness through the descent into sleep itself, with everything else following from that.

The technique Namkhai Norbu teaches is correspondingly different in character from the Tsongkhapa version. Where the Geluk texts emphasize energy control, vase breathing, and visualization at the throat or genital chakras, the Dzogchen instruction is the simple visualization of a luminous white *A* at the center of the body, held lightly through the descent into sleep:

> The most important point is that when you fall asleep, *you try to have this 'A' present*. Initially, it should be accurate and sharp; afterwards you relax. Relaxing does not mean you drop the 'A' or that you give it up. You retain a sense of its presence, and you relax, and thus you fall asleep.

The continuity of awareness through the dissolution of attention is the practice. The white *A* is a tether — something for the attention to rest on as the body's perceptual apparatus shuts down — but the work being done is the simpler and harder work of holding awareness through the moment of falling asleep.

## The verse of the bardo of dreams

The classical Tibetan prayer that frames the night, given in Namkhai Norbu's book, makes the orientation visible:

> When the state of dreaming has dawned,
> Do not lie in ignorance like a corpse.
> *Enter the natural sphere of unwavering attentiveness.*
> Recognize your dreams and transform illusion into luminosity.
> Do not sleep like an animal. Do the practice which mixes sleep and reality.

The same prayer appears in Fremantle and Trungpa's *Tibetan Book of the Dead* in nearly identical wording, as one of the "main verses of the six bardos":

> "Now when the bardo of dreams is dawning upon me, / I will abandon the corpse-like sleep of careless ignorance, / and let my thoughts enter their natural state without distraction; / controlling and transforming dreams in luminosity, / *I will not sleep like any animal / but unify completely sleep and practice*" (Fremantle and Trungpa, *Tibetan Book of the Dead*).

The persistence of this verse across Bardo Thödol, Six Yogas, and Dzogchen treatments is itself a kind of evidence: the same practice-orientation, in the same language, runs through every Tibetan stream that handles the night.

## Accessibility under contemporary conditions

What the Dzogchen framing offers a contemporary practitioner that the Geluk framing does not is, on Namkhai Norbu's account, *accessibility under contemporary conditions*.

The classical Geluk treatment presupposes that the practitioner has done substantial *tummo* and *gyulü* preparation — the inner-heat foundations and the daytime illusory-body work — before approaching dream yoga. Few contemporary practitioners have done this.

The Dzogchen treatment requires a stable shamatha foundation and the willingness to attend, night after night, to the moment of falling asleep without letting attention dissipate.

This is also a demanding requirement. Falling asleep is, for most people, the place where attention most reliably collapses. Holding awareness through the dissolution of the senses is, in the practitioner reports available, work of a particular kind — slower than meditation, more sustained than ordinary attention, requiring patience over weeks and months.

But it is work that does not require monastic structure or years of preliminary tantric training. A lay practitioner, with a stable shamatha base, can begin.

## Two compatible framings

The two framings — Geluk and Dzogchen — sit alongside each other rather than against. Most contemporary teachers move between them.

Wallace's *Dreaming Yourself Awake* draws on Padmasambhava's *Natural Liberation* terma cycle (a Nyingma source) but presents the four classical stages (a treatment that aligns more with the Geluk reading) as the practice's working architecture. Tenzin Wangyal's Bön treatment, which the next lessons take up, runs both threads in parallel.

**What the Dzogchen treatment adds to the practice landscape, and what is worth holding against the Geluk framing, is the prior question: *what is the awareness doing, in the moment between waking and dream?***

If the answer is *nothing* — if the awareness lapses at the threshold of sleep — then the dream that follows will be, as it normally is, an unwitnessed sequence. If the answer is *something* — if the awareness can be carried, by training, through the threshold — then the dream that follows arises within an awareness that did not stop.

This is the Dzogchen claim, and the Dzogchen practice is the training in which the claim becomes the practitioner's own first-person experience.
