# Module 2, Lesson 6 — Three Kinds of Dreams; Three Kinds of Sleep

*A typology that recurs across the sources and is most clearly developed in Tenzin Wangyal's Bön presentation. Useful for the practitioner because it gives a working language for what is happening on any given night.*

---

## Three kinds of dreams

The Tibetan tradition distinguishes three kinds of dreams. The distinction is phenomenological: it names what is going on at the level of mind and energy when each kind of dream arises, and it gives the practitioner a way to recognize where in the practice they are.

### Samsaric dreams

**Samsaric dreams** arise from karmic traces. They project the dreamer's conditioned tendencies — the habits, fears, preoccupations, attachments, aversions of the day's mind — onto the night's screen.

Most dreams, for most people, most of the time, are samsaric in this sense. The dream takes the materials of the day and remakes them through the lens of unresolved patterning.

The Bṛhadāraṇyaka's bricoleur (Module 1, Lesson 3) is, in Tibetan terms, working entirely in the samsaric register.

### Dreams of clarity

**Dreams of clarity** arise "when the mind and the prana are balanced and the dreamer has developed the capacity to remain in non-personal presence."

Tenzin Wangyal's framing, in *Tibetan Yogas of Dream and Sleep*, is that these are "*based less on personal karmic traces and instead present knowledge available directly from consciousness below the level of the conventional self*" (Tenzin Wangyal Rinpoche, *Tibetan Yogas of Dream and Sleep*).

This is a category the Western literature has yet to find a clean term for. A dream of clarity is a dream whose mode of arising is different. The conditioned habits of the day's mind are not driving it. Something quieter is.

The practitioner who has stabilized in shamatha and has a working dream practice will, eventually, begin to have dreams of this kind — not by trying for them, but as a side-effect of the prior work.

### Clear-light dreams

**Clear-light dreams** arise "from the primordial prana in the central channel" and contain, in their fullest form, "*awareness, but no subject-object dichotomy*."

**Tenzin Wangyal is unusually candid about how rare these are:**

> "Gyalshen Milu Samleg, the author of important commentaries on the Mother Tantra, wrote that *he practiced consistently for nine years before he began to have clear light dreams*."

This is a working teacher's honest statement about the timeline of advanced practice. Nine years of consistent practice. Not a weekend retreat, not a six-week module, not a year of dabbling.

Nine years of consistent practice for a practitioner with full lineage support, doing the practice in something like the conditions the practice was designed for.

### What the typology is for

The point of the typology is to give the practitioner an honest working sense of where they are.

Most dreams are samsaric, and that is fine; the work is to recognize samsaric dreams rather than to abolish them. Some dreams are dreams of clarity, and the practitioner learns to recognize the difference in feeling-tone, presence, and content. A few dreams, after long practice, are clear-light dreams, and these arise rather than being produced.

## Three kinds of sleep

A parallel typology applies to sleep itself.

**The sleep of ignorance** is the ordinary blank deep-sleep state. Tenzin Wangyal's phrase: "*a darkness thousands of years old*."

This is the deep sleep of someone who has done no practice. The person sleeps; consciousness lapses; the body restores itself; the next morning the person wakes; nothing has been registered.

**Samsaric sleep** is the dreaming state — "a limitless realm of mental activity generated by the karmic traces of past actions."

Most of what is ordinarily called dream falls in this register. The mind is active, but the activity is conditioned by the same karmic patterning that drives samsaric dreams.

**Clear-light sleep** is the state realized through sleep yoga, in which "*the body is sleeping but the practitioner is neither lost in darkness nor in dreams, but instead abides in pure awareness*."

## Mother and son: clear light defined

**Tenzin Wangyal's definition of clear light is quoted nearly word-for-word from the older sources:**

> "*Clear light is defined in most texts as the unity of emptiness and clarity.* It is the pure, empty awareness that is the base of the individual. 'Clear' refers to emptiness, the mother, the base, *kunzhi*. 'Light' refers to clarity, the son, *rigpa*, pure innate awareness."

The mother–son distinction here is the standard Tibetan framing, and it will return in later lessons. The mother is the empty base; the son is the awareness that recognizes itself in that base.

They are two aspects, conceptually distinguishable but ontologically inseparable, of the same recognition.

## Dream yoga and sleep yoga distinguished

**The distinction between dream yoga and sleep yoga is then as Tenzin Wangyal frames it:**

> Dream yoga develops lucidity in relationship to the dream images. But in sleep yoga there is no film and no projection. *Sleep yoga is imageless. The practice is the direct recognition of awareness by awareness, light illuminating itself.*

This is a different difficulty from dream-lucidity, and a deeper one. Dream yoga has dreams to work with. Sleep yoga has nothing. That is precisely the point.

## What the science can and cannot reach

The contemporary lucid-dream science, after seven decades of careful work, has produced detailed accounts of REM sleep and lucid dreaming. It has produced almost nothing on lucid dreamless sleep.

The reason is that the Western scientific apparatus is set up to record activity, and the deep-sleep state Tibetan sleep yoga points at is, by definition, a state in which the activity that the apparatus would record is mostly absent.

What the practitioner attests to in clear-light sleep is the presence of awareness *without* content — awareness that has no object, no image, no thought, no felt-sense of having a body. It is the most demanding contemplative state the tradition recognizes.

**A practitioner who works only with lucid dreams is doing something real, but doing it in the shallowest of the night's available states. A practitioner who extends the work to sleep yoga has access — at least in principle — to a deeper one.**

## The proportion gradually shifts

The typologies are useful because they give the practitioner names for what they are encountering. Most nights are samsaric in dream, and most sleep is sleep of ignorance. This is normal, this is the working baseline, and the practice gradually shifts the proportion.

As shamatha stabilizes, dreams of clarity begin to appear among the samsaric ones. As the dream practice deepens, the night's sleep itself becomes a more conscious medium. As the work continues — over years, in the tradition's expectation — clear-light dreams and clear-light sleep become accessible, and the recognition of *rigpa* in those deepest states is the practice's stated aim.
