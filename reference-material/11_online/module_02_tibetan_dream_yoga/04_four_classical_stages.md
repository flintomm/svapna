# Module 2, Lesson 4 — The Four Classical Stages of Dream-Yoga Practice

*Comprehension, transformation, illusion, suchness. A progression, not a menu.*

---

The classical four-stage structure of dream-yoga practice is laid out clearly in Tsongkhapa via Mullin and, from a different angle, in the Evans-Wentz / Kazi Dawa-Samdup translation of the older "Doctrine of the Dream-State."

The same four stages recur, with minor variations in vocabulary, in nearly every Tibetan source on the practice — Geluk, Kagyu, Nyingma, Bön. They are the practice's working architecture.

## Stage 1 — Comprehension

The first stage is what Western readers would call learning to become lucid.

In Mullin's translation: "*The first step in dream yoga is to learn to retain conscious presence during dreams.*"

Tsongkhapa lists three classical methods.

**The first** is comprehension by *the power of resolution* — the daytime intention, repeated through the day and at sleep onset, that dreams be recognized as dreams.

**The second** is comprehension by *the power of breath* — postural and breathing adjustments at sleep that condition the body to enter sleep with the attention intact.

**The third** is comprehension by *the power of visualization*.

The visualization most often taught is the throat-chakra method. In the Evans-Wentz / Kazi Dawa-Samdup rendering:

> "*visualize in the throat psychic-centre the syllable AH, red of colour and vividly radiant*, as being the real embodiment of Divine Speech" (Evans-Wentz, *Tibetan Yoga and Secret Doctrines*).

Mullin's later translation describes the same syllabic visualization, sometimes with the five-letter cluster OM AH NU TA RA arranged on a four-petalled lotus, drawn from the *Advaya Vijaya Tantra*.

The practitioner holds the visualization at the throat as the descent into sleep begins. The held attention, in those who have done the prior shamatha work, can carry through the threshold of sleep, and the lucidity that arises in the dream that follows is the result of attention that did not lapse.

This is the classical Tibetan version of what the Western lucid-dream literature would much later call WILD — wake-initiated lucid dreaming.

## Stage 2 — Transformation

The second stage is using the lucid dream to scramble the dream's appearances. The instruction in the Evans-Wentz material is unusually direct:

> "*if the dream be of fire, transform the fire into water, the antidote of fire.* And if the dream be of minute objects, transform them into large objects; or if the dream be of large objects, transform them into small objects: thereby one comprehendeth the nature of dimensions." (Evans-Wentz, *Tibetan Yoga and Secret Doctrines*, trans. Kazi Dawa-Samdup)

Mullin's Tsongkhapa version expands on this. The practitioner trains in flying, in projection to "celestial realms" and *buddhafields* such as Sukhāvatī, in multiplying the objects of the dream "from one to two, from two to four, and so on, until hundreds and even thousands."

**The point is demonstration: to demonstrate to the practitioner, in a state where the demonstration is unanswerable, that perception is plastic — that the apparent solidity of the dream-world is a function of mind, and that this fact, once felt, will eventually transfer.**

Set against the Western lucid-dream literature, the comparison is instructive. The Western literature also teaches dream control — flying, transformation, anchoring — and many of the techniques are nearly identical.

The difference is the *aim*. The Western practitioner deploys the techniques for the experience. The dream-yoga practitioner deploys them as evidence — evidence the practitioner produces for himself, that the apparent objectivity of phenomena is a function of consciousness rather than the other way around.

## Stage 3 — Realization that the Dream is Māyā

The third stage goes one step deeper. The Evans-Wentz text states it baldly: the practitioner is to "abandon all feeling of fear" toward dream phenomena, "trample upon the fire," and through repeated demonstration come to understand "*that nothing within the Sangsara is or can be other than unreal like dreams*."

**Mullin's Tsongkhapa frames this as overcoming fear in dreams and training in the illusory nature of dreams:**

> "whenever anything of a threatening or traumatic nature occurs in a dream, such as drowning in water or being burned by fire, recognize the dream as a dream and ask yourself, '*How can dream water or dream fire possibly harm me?*' Make yourself jump or fall into the water or fire in the dream." (Mullin trans., *The Six Yogas of Naropa*, Snow Lion)

The instruction sounds dramatic until what it is doing comes into focus. The dream-fear is a microcosm of waking-fear.

The practitioner who has trained, through repeated lucid encounters with dream-fire, that dream-fire cannot harm him — *because dream-fire is not separate from his own mind* — has done a piece of work that becomes available, in attenuated but real form, in the larger fears of waking life.

This is the third stage's specifically Mahāyāna content. The dream-as-māyā teaching is the inheritance from Śāntideva's *Bodhicaryāvatāra* (Module 1, Lesson 6), now operationalized in a state where the recognition can be tested by direct demonstration.

*Both thoughts are equally deceptive*, Śāntideva had written of the dream-thoughts that the child is dead and that the child is living. The third stage is the practical training in which that recognition becomes available not as an intellectual claim but as a felt fact.

## Stage 4 — Meditating Upon the Suchness of Dreams

The fourth stage is abiding in the empty, luminous nature of the dream-state itself. The Evans-Wentz translation:

> "By concentrating the mind upon the forms of the deities seen in the dream-state, and by keeping the mind free of thoughts, in the quiescent condition, the forms of the deities are attuned to the non-thought condition of mind; *and thereby dawneth the Clear Light, of which the essence is of the Voidness*." (Evans-Wentz, *Tibetan Yoga and Secret Doctrines*, trans. Kazi Dawa-Samdup)

**Mullin gives the same teaching in a more practical Geluk register:**

> "meditate upon yourself as the manifest radiant form of the mandala deity. The mantric syllable HUM shines with a great light from your heart. This light melts the animate and inanimate dream objects into light, which is absorbed into yourself. Your body then also melts into light... The HUM then melts into unapprehendable clear light. *Rest the mind unwaveringly within this light.*" (Mullin trans., *The Six Yogas of Naropa*, Snow Lion)

**This is the actual aim of the practice — the dissolution of the dream into clear light, and through it the recognition of the awareness that has been dreaming all along.**

## What the four stages add up to

This is what every Tibetan source on dream yoga is finally about. The first three stages are the ramp; the fourth stage is the destination.

The destination, in the end, is unspectacular — no particular vision, no particular content, no particular acquisition. The destination is the recognition of an awareness that is not new, that has been there all along, and that the dream-state has been the most accessible site for recognizing.

The lucid moment lets the practitioner enter the dream consciously. The transformations let the practitioner demonstrate that dream-phenomena are mind-made. The fearlessness training lets the practitioner stop reifying dream-content as if it had power over him. The dissolution lets him recognize the awareness that has been doing all of this.

That awareness — what later lessons will follow under the name *clear light*, *ösel*, or, in the Dzogchen vocabulary that the next lesson takes up, *rigpa* — is what the practice was always pointing at. The four stages are how the practitioner is taught to see it.
