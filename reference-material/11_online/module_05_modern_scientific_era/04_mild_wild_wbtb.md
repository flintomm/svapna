# Module 5, Lesson 4 — MILD, WILD, WBTB

*Three induction techniques that, in motivated subjects, reliably increase the rate of lucid dreaming. The Tholey reflection technique that operates upstream of all of them.*

---

The eye-signal demonstration of 1980 established that lucid dreaming was real. It did not, on its own, establish that lucid dreaming was reliably trainable.

The early experimental subjects — LaBerge himself, Worsley, a small handful of other naturally adept lucid dreamers — were already capable of becoming lucid in the laboratory.

**The next question, and the one that would define LaBerge's career through the 1980s and 1990s, was whether the rest of the population could be taught to do the same thing.**

The development of induction techniques came out of this period of work. Three techniques are described in *Exploring the World of Lucid Dreaming* in enough detail to be quoted directly.

## MILD: Mnemonic Induction of Lucid Dreams

The first and most thoroughly developed of the techniques is LaBerge's own. The method emerged from his dissertation work.

He had been using a vague autosuggestion method ("Tonight, I will have a lucid dream") with modest success — about five lucid dreams a month — but he could not reliably produce a lucid dream on a chosen night, and so he could not reliably book the laboratory for a confirming session.

**The MILD technique was developed by reflecting on what was actually happening when the autosuggestion worked:**

> Nevertheless, *I gradually observed a psychological factor that correlated with the occurrence of my lucid dreams: the presleep intention to remember to recognize I was dreaming.* Once I knew how I was trying to induce lucid dreams, it became much easier to focus my efforts. This clarification of intention was followed by an immediate increase in the monthly frequency of my lucid dreams. Further practice and refinements led to a method whereby I could reliably induce lucid dreams. With this new method, I had as many as four lucid dreams in one night and as many as twenty-six in one month. I now could have a lucid dream on any night I chose and had accomplished my goal of showing that *it is possible to bring access to the lucid dream state under volitional control*. (LaBerge and Rheingold)

### The conceptual reframing

The conceptual reframing is the substance of the technique.

Instead of trying to produce a lucid dream directly, the practitioner trains *prospective memory* — the capacity to remember, at a future time, to perform a specific action.

In ordinary waking life, prospective memory is what allows a person to remember to buy tacks at the store, take a medication at noon, or call someone after a meeting. It runs on two factors: motivation (genuine intent fixes a target in memory) and association (a cue tied to the circumstances in which the action will need to be done).

**LaBerge's claim is that the dream-task — recognizing, while dreaming, that one is dreaming — has the same structure as any other prospective-memory task, and can be trained the same way.**

### The five-step protocol

The technique itself, as given in the book, is a five-step procedure to be followed on awakening from a dream during the night:

> 1. *Set up dream recall.* Before going to bed resolve to wake up and recall dreams during each dream period throughout the night.
> 2. *Recall your dream.* When you awaken from a dream period, no matter what time it is, try to recall as many details as possible from your dream. If you find yourself so drowsy that you are drifting back to sleep, do something to arouse yourself.
> 3. *Focus your intent.* While returning to sleep, concentrate single-mindedly on your intention to remember to recognize that you're dreaming. Tell yourself: "Next time I'm dreaming, I want to remember I'm dreaming." Really try to feel that you mean it. Narrow your thoughts to this idea alone.
> 4. *See yourself becoming lucid.* At the same time, imagine that you are back in the dream from which you have just awakened, but this time you recognize that it is a dream. Find a dreamsign in the experience; when you see it say to yourself: "I'm dreaming!" and continue your fantasy.
> 5. *Repeat.* Repeat Steps 3 and 4 until your intention is set, then let yourself fall asleep. (LaBerge and Rheingold)

The original 1980 dissertation established that MILD was, in LaBerge's own self-experimentation, "*approximately 800% more effective than autosuggestion for inducing signal-verified lucid dreams in the sleep laboratory*."

With autosuggestion, he had had a lucid dream "on only one out of six nights in the lab"; with MILD, he had "one or more lucid dreams on twenty out of twenty-one nights spent in the sleep laboratory."

Subsequent studies, including the Aspy, Delfabbro, Proeve and Mohr 2017 trial, have confirmed that MILD increases lucid-dream frequency in motivated populations.

**The mechanism is identical to what Mary Arnold-Forster had described in 1921 — pre-sleep intention, embedded in memorable language, internalized through patient repetition.**

LaBerge's contribution is the structured five-step protocol and the conceptual reframing as prospective memory. The underlying mechanism is the one the empirical-introspective tradition had been working with for sixty years.

## WILD: Wake-Initiated Lucid Dream

A second class of induction technique runs in the opposite direction. Where MILD aims to *carry an intention from the waking state into a dream that will arise spontaneously*, WILD aims to *carry awareness directly across the threshold of sleep* — the practitioner falls asleep without losing consciousness, and the awareness is therefore present in the resulting dream by continuity.

**LaBerge gives the operational distinction crisply:**

> This chapter presents a completely different set of approaches to the world of lucid dreaming based on the idea of falling asleep consciously. *This involves retaining consciousness while wakefulness is lost and allows direct entry into the lucid dream state without any loss of reflective consciousness.* The basic idea has many variations. While falling asleep, you can focus on hypnagogic (sleep onset) imagery, deliberate visualizations, your breath or heartbeat, the sensations in your body, your sense of self, and so on. *If you keep the mind sufficiently active while the tendency to enter REM sleep is strong, you feel your body fall asleep, but you, that is to say, your consciousness, remains awake.* The next thing you know, you will find yourself in the dream world, fully lucid. (LaBerge and Rheingold)

### Continuity with the Tibetan tradition

The terminology of "WILD" versus "DILD" (dream-initiated lucid dream) is LaBerge's own. The underlying distinction had been observed in the contemplative literature for centuries.

The Tibetan dream-yoga tradition has long emphasized the threshold of sleep as the operative training site. Module 2's Lesson 5 covered Namkhai Norbu's "practice of natural light" — which is exactly what WILD asks for, in different vocabulary. Module 2's Lesson 4 covered the throat-chakra AH visualization, which is structurally a WILD technique.

**LaBerge's WILD methods are explicitly continuous with the Tibetan tradition, and he says so.**

### The empirical record on WILD

The empirical record on WILD is more equivocal than on MILD.

WILDs are less common than DILDs in laboratory data — about 28 percent of laboratory lucid dreams in the studies LaBerge and Rheingold cite, against 72 percent DILDs — and considerably less common in home settings.

The technique requires more concentrated effort than MILD and is, as LaBerge puts it, "much favored by the Tibetan tradition" precisely because it depends on and develops a continuous quality of attention through the descent into sleep.

Its rewards, when it works, are substantial: WILDs are typically more vivid and longer-lasting than DILDs, and the practitioner enters the dream already aware.

**As LaBerge writes:**

> "while techniques for direct entry to the dream state require considerable practice in the beginning, they offer correspondingly great rewards. *When mastered, these techniques (like MILD) can confer the capacity to induce lucid dreams virtually at will.*"

## WBTB: Wake Back to Bed

The third technique is the most prosaic and, in motivated subjects, the most reliable single intervention in the literature.

The reasoning behind WBTB is straightforward. Two facts about sleep architecture matter.

**First**, the REM episodes get longer through the night: ninety minutes between REM periods at the start of the night, perhaps twenty or thirty minutes between them by the morning, and the late-night REM periods themselves are correspondingly longer.

**Second**, lucidity is more likely in the long late-night REM periods than in the short early-night ones — partly because the brain in late-night REM is closer in activation profile to the waking state, and partly because the morning sleeper is closer to natural waking and so more easily holds attention through sleep onset.

The WBTB protocol takes advantage of both facts. The practitioner sleeps for several hours, wakes, stays awake for thirty to ninety minutes (engaged in some quiet activity related to lucid dreaming — reading, journaling, MILD rehearsal), then returns to bed.

The next REM episode is typically reached within a few minutes of falling asleep again, and that REM episode is unusually likely to contain a lucid dream.

### The five-step exercise

LaBerge gives the technique as a five-step exercise:

> 1. *Set your alarm.* Before going to bed, set your alarm to awaken you two to three hours earlier than usual, and go to sleep at your normal time.
> 2. *Get out of bed promptly in the morning.* When your alarm goes off, get out of bed immediately. You are going to stay awake for two or three hours.
> 3. *Focus on your intentions for your lucid dreams.* For the half hour before you return to sleep think about what you want to accomplish in your lucid dream.
> 4. *Return to bed and practice an induction technique.*
> 5. *Give yourself at least two hours to sleep.* (LaBerge and Rheingold)

Subsequent experimental work has confirmed the value of the protocol. The 2018 PLOS ONE paper notes that LaBerge, Phillips and Levitan's 1994 study had already established that "*an hour of wakefulness before morning naps makes lucidity more likely*."

Subsequent work showed that "combining MILD with a longer period of wakefulness of either 30 to 60 minutes late in the sleep cycle was associated with even higher increases in lucid dream frequency in the ensuing sleep period."

## The integrated protocol, and Tholey upstream

**The current consensus is that the three techniques are not alternatives but components of a single integrated protocol.**

WBTB sets up the sleep state in which lucidity is more likely. MILD provides the cognitive set during the wake period. The dream that follows is then either a DILD (lucidity arises within the dream, triggered by a dreamsign) or, less commonly, a WILD (the practitioner returns to sleep without losing awareness).

The Tholey reflection technique — the day-long practice of asking *Am I dreaming?* combined with a reality test, developed in Frankfurt by **Paul Tholey** beginning in the late 1950s — operates upstream of all three.

It builds the meta-cognitive habit on which MILD's prospective-memory rehearsal and the dream-state recognition both depend.

LaBerge introduces Tholey early — "the German psychologist Paul Tholey, who has been studying lucid dreams for the past twenty years" — and treats him with consistent respect throughout the book. The reflection technique is described in detail in chapter 3. Tholey's data on hostile dream figures is cited in the nightmare chapter. Tholey's induction techniques for direct dream entry are credited explicitly in the WILD chapter.

**LaBerge on Tholey's "critical-reflective attitude":**

> "Paul Tholey has recently derived several techniques for inducing lucid dreams from over a decade of research involving more than two hundred subjects. Tholey claims that an effective method for achieving lucidity (especially for beginners) is to develop a '*critical-reflective attitude*' toward your state of consciousness. This is done by asking yourself whether or not you are dreaming while you are awake. He stresses the importance of asking the 'critical question' ('*Am I dreaming or not?*') as frequently as possible, at least five to ten times a day, and in every situation that seems dreamlike."

The reflection technique as currently taught is essentially Tholey's, with adjustments.

What the contemporary practitioner inherits, between Tholey, MILD, WILD, and WBTB, is the Western technical apparatus that the integration with the contemplative tradition (Module 6) will work with. The Tibetan tradition has its own techniques. The two sets are largely complementary.
