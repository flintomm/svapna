# Module 5, Lesson 2 — The Discovery of REM (1953)

*A two-page paper in* Science, *by a graduate student and his mentor. The founding document of the modern science of dreaming.*

---

## Two pages, twenty subjects, three findings

The modern scientific study of dreaming begins with a paper of fewer than two thousand words on two facing pages of *Science*.

The paper is titled "Regularly Occurring Periods of Eye Motility, and Concomitant Phenomena, During Sleep." Its authors are **Eugene Aserinsky**, then a graduate student in physiology, and **Nathaniel Kleitman**, the older man's mentor and head of the sleep laboratory at the University of Chicago. Its date is September 4, 1953.

The paper is short because the finding is simple and the case is largely visible in the figure. It describes a phenomenon — bursts of rapid, jerky eye movements during sleep — and reports three things about it: how it was recorded, when in the night it occurred, and what the sleeper was experiencing at the time.

## The technique

Slow rolling eye movements during sleep had been noted before, the paper concedes at the opening, citing Pietrusky, De Toni, Fuchs and Wu, Andreev, and Burford. "*However, this report deals with a different type of eye movement — rapid, jerky, and binocularly symmetrical.*"

The new technique was electrooculography. One pair of leads was placed on the superior and inferior orbital ridges of one eye to detect vertical-plane changes in the corneo-retinal potential; another pair, on the inner and outer canthi of the same eye, to pick up "mainly the horizontal component of eye movement."

The recordings were complicated by superfluous potentials from brain waves, lid and jaw muscle activity, and electrode movement.

To distinguish the eye signal from this noise the experimenters added a simultaneous monopolar recording from the frontal area, gain-adjusted so that "an equal excursion of both pens signified that the bipolar potential was actually 4 times greater than the monopolar potential." The criterion was further confirmed "by direct observation of several subjects under both weak and gradually intensified illumination," and "motion pictures were taken of 2 subjects without awakening them."

## The pattern

What the recordings showed, in twenty normal adult subjects across several series of experiments, was that this rapid eye motility was not a random or rare event. It occurred in regular periods through the night, separated by long stretches of "ocular quiescence."

The mean delay from going to bed to the first appearance of the rapid pattern was three hours fourteen minutes, after which the pattern recurred at intervals of around two hours, with a third or fourth period clustering closer to the end of the night. Each period varied in duration but typically lasted about twenty minutes.

The brain wave records during these eye-movement periods were of low amplitude and irregular frequency — a different EEG pattern from the one seen during quiescent sleep — and the bursts of eye motility were accompanied by mild elevation in respiratory rate (16.9 per minute during eye motility versus 13.4 during ocular quiescence, "*statistically significant with a probability of less than 0.001*"), and by peaks of overt body movement.

## The dream correlation

What gives the paper its weight, beyond the phenomenology of the eye movement itself, is the third experiment.

To confirm what the authors call "the conjecture that this particular eye activity was associated with dreaming," ten subjects in fourteen experiments were awakened during eye motility and during periods of "at least 30 min to 3 hr of ocular quiescence."

The quiescence intervals were chosen on the basis of the EEG to represent "as closely as possible, a depth of sleep comparable to that present during ocular motility" — a control condition.

**The result is reported in two sentences:**

> Of 27 interrogations during ocular motility, *20 revealed detailed dreams usually involving visual imagery*; the replies to the remaining 7 queries included complete failure of recall, or else, 'the feeling of having dreamed,' but with inability to recollect any detail of the dream. Of 23 interrogations during ocular inactivity, *19 disclosed complete failure of recall*, while the remaining 4 were evenly divided into the other 2 categories. (Aserinsky and Kleitman 1953)

The authors' inference is cautious — they note "the inadequacies of employing a χ² test for the independence of the 2 groups of interrogations" — but the chi-squared test gave a probability "less than 0.01" that the association was due to chance.

## The founding sentence

**The paper concludes:**

> "the fact that these eye movements, EEG pattern, and autonomic nervous system activity are significantly related and do not occur randomly suggests that *these physiological phenomena, and probably dreaming, are very likely all manifestations of a particular level of cortical activity which is encountered normally during sleep*."

That sentence is the founding sentence of the modern science of dreaming.

Until it was written, sleep had been treated, as LaBerge and Rheingold put it some forty years later, as "a uniform state of passive withdrawal from the world."

**After it, sleep was a structured cycle of distinct states with distinct neurophysiological signatures, and dreaming had a physiological correlate that could be detected from outside the sleeper's skull.**

The paper is, considered as scientific writing, modest. It does not propose a model of dream formation. It does not speculate on function. It reports a phenomenon, gives a method, runs a control, and stops.

What it leaves on the table is the implication: if dreaming is concentrated in a particular phase of sleep with a particular neural signature, then dreaming is now, in principle, a phenomenon accessible to laboratory study.

## What followed within a decade

The shape of the field that grew up after 1953 is not in the paper itself but is implicit in its closing sentence about "this method furnishes the means of determining the incidence and duration of periods of dreaming."

Within two years of the publication, **William Dement** — then a graduate student in the same Chicago laboratory — had used the method to establish (1955, 1957) that subjects woken during these eye-movement periods reliably recalled vivid dream content, while those woken at other points in the night did not.

By the late 1960s, REM sleep had been worked out in considerable physiological detail. **Michel Jouvet** at Lyon had demonstrated the descending muscle atonia that paralyzes the body during REM. The cycling pattern through the night had been mapped. The role of the brainstem in initiating and terminating REM episodes had been outlined.

By the late 1970s, when the first laboratory work on lucid dreaming was conducted, the framework was ready. There was a known phase of sleep in which dreaming was concentrated, accessible to electrophysiological recording.

What was missing was a way to study the dreamer *as the dream was happening*. Subjective reports collected after waking were the only available source of information about what the dreamer had experienced. The reports were known to be unreliable in particular ways: forgetting, post-hoc reconstruction, the difficulty of putting dream-experience into the categories of waking language. There was no obvious way to address the problem.

And then there was. The next lesson takes up the eye-signal demonstration that closed the gap.

## A note on the paper's tone

A note worth marking before the next lesson. The Aserinsky-Kleitman paper is the kind of scientific writing the contemporary practitioner can productively read.

It is short — under two thousand words. It is concrete — eye electrodes, EEG pens, twenty subjects, the chi-squared test. It is modest — it reports what it found, runs a control, and stops without overinterpreting.

The first peer-reviewed paper in any tradition that established the physiological correlate of dreaming is the kind of paper that any careful reader, with a small dictionary of technical terms, can follow.

This is not a coincidence. The dream science that started in 1953 set its standards from this paper outward. The papers that built on it — Dement on dream-recall during REM, Jouvet on muscle atonia, the long catalog of papers in *Sleep* and *Psychophysiology* through the 1960s and 1970s — share its register.

**The first thing the modern scientific era of dream research established was its tone.**
