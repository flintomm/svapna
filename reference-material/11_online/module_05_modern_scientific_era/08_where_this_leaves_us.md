# Module 5, Lesson 8 — Where the Modern Era Leaves Off

*What the modern scientific era has accomplished, in seven decades. What it has not. The hand-off to Module 6.*

---

## What the modern scientific era has accomplished

What the modern scientific era has accomplished, in the seven decades from Aserinsky and Kleitman to the 2018 PLOS ONE paper, is the following.

It has shown that **dreaming has a physiological signature**, concentrated in the rapid-eye-movement phase of sleep, and that the signature is detectable from outside the sleeper's body.

It has shown that **lucid dreaming** — the contemplative tradition's core empirical claim — is real, in the sense that a person can deliberately and repeatedly produce eye-movement signals from inside a dream that arrive on a polygraph trace at a known location during a known REM episode.

It has produced **a small set of induction techniques**, of which MILD, WILD, WBTB, and the Tholey reflection technique are the most thoroughly studied, and shown that these techniques have measurable effects on the rate of lucid dreaming in motivated subjects.

It has produced **a pharmacological intervention** — galantamine in a WBTB + MILD protocol — with a substantial effect size and a manageable side-effect profile.

It has begun, through the contemporary neuroscience of the 2010s, to **map the neural correlates of the lucid state** with increasing precision, although the present module's primary-source coverage of that work is limited.

And it has begun, through the IRT literature and its predecessors, to **translate the laboratory findings into clinical interventions** for nightmare disorder and post-traumatic dreams — with results that are real and that the contemporary clinical consensus increasingly supports.

This is a substantial body of work. It establishes, on the science's own terms, almost everything the contemplative tradition had claimed about the empirical character of lucid dreaming.

**The lucid state is real, trainable, neurally distinctive, behaviorally observable, and clinically useful.**

## What it has not done

What the modern scientific era has *not* done is settle the contemplative-tradition's questions about what the lucid state is *for*.

Western lucid dreaming, as LaBerge himself acknowledges in chapter 12 of *Exploring the World*, is a methodology without a built-in telos. It can be used for entertainment, problem-solving, performance enhancement, fantasy, sex, creative inspiration.

Each of those uses is locally defensible. **None of them is the practice as the contemplative tradition understood it.**

## LaBerge naming the limit of his own discipline

LaBerge himself is unusually candid about the limit. The book's final chapter quotes Tarthang Tulku at length on the Tibetan understanding of dream as "a vehicle for exploring reality"; reproduces the four stages of the Doctrine of the Dream State (dreams can be changed by will; dreams are unstable; waking-state perception is as unreal as dreams; the Great Realization that nothing within the conditioned world is or can be other than unreal like dreams); and then defers, in a remarkably modest closing move:

> *Plainly, this is not the sort of knowledge that is subject to public verification and scientific testing.* However, this qualification is in no way intended to deny the possible value of mystical experiences, since *there is no reason to believe that the limits of science are the limits of knowledge*. Nor do we intend to imply that you should follow the ways of the Tibetan yogis in seeking your own knowledge of "Divine Wisdom." The methods and symbology of the Tibetan mystical schools were designed to function within the cultural context of Tibetan culture. *If you are serious about pursuing your highest potential, we recommend that you find a guide or teacher who can speak to you in a language that you can understand.* (LaBerge and Rheingold, chapter 12)

**The leading scientist of the lucid-dream field, in the closing chapter of his most-read book, names the limit of his own discipline.**

*Plainly, this is not the sort of knowledge that is subject to public verification and scientific testing.*

The science has done its part. It has put lucid dreaming on the table as a real and trainable phenomenon, given the contemporary practitioner a small but reliable set of induction methods, and produced a steadily improving picture of what the lucid brain is doing.

**What it cannot do is supply the orientation that holds the practice inside a developed contemplative life.**

## The science has met the tradition halfway

The integration of the two streams — the laboratory findings and the contemplative frame — is the subject of Module 6.

What this module needs to establish is that by the close of the 2010s, the empirical scaffold for that integration is fully built. The Asian and European contemplative claims about the trainability and reality of lucid dreaming have been independently confirmed. The techniques have been refined and tested. The neural correlates have been mapped. The therapeutic uses are entering routine clinical practice.

**The science has met the tradition halfway.**

The other half is contemplative work, and is the territory of the next module.

## A short editorial note before the next module

The work's framing — established in the welcome lesson and reaffirmed in each module's opening — is that the science material in this module deserves to be taken seriously *on its own terms*.

The lucid-dream science is its own thing rather than a pre-modern tradition's clumsy first attempt at saying what the contemplative traditions had been saying for centuries. It uses different methods, asks different questions, produces different kinds of evidence, and is justifiable on its own intellectual standards.

The contemplative tradition is also its own thing. It uses different methods, asks different questions, produces different kinds of evidence, and is justifiable on its own intellectual standards.

**The convergence is that they are working on the same phenomenon. The divergence is that they are working on it for different reasons and with different equipment.**

A serious contemporary practitioner inherits both.

The science gives the practice its empirical foundation, its public legitimacy, its clinical applications, and its on-ramp methods that work without prior contemplative training.

The contemplative tradition gives the practice its aim, its frame, its soteriological orientation, and its centuries of refined first-person observation that the science is, even now, only beginning to verify in detail.

**To take only one and dismiss the other is to take a partial inheritance. To hold both seriously, on their own terms, is what the contemporary integration asks.**

Module 6 takes up the question of how that integration has actually been done, by whom, with what tensions, and what the next generation inherits.
