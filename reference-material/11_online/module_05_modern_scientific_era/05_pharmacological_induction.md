# Module 5, Lesson 5 — Pharmacological Induction: Galantamine (2018)

*The 2018 PLOS ONE paper that established a substantial dose-response effect for galantamine on lucid-dream frequency, layered onto a MILD + WBTB protocol. The result is large; the ethical complexity is real.*

---

> *A note before the science.* Galantamine is a prescription acetylcholinesterase inhibitor approved for Alzheimer's disease. Its sale and use are regulated in many jurisdictions; it interacts with beta-blockers, anti-cholinergics, and other medications, and is contraindicated in asthma, certain cardiac arrhythmias, and serious mental illness. The lesson below describes the published research and is not a recommendation to use the drug. Anyone considering it does so under the supervision of a physician. See the [medical disclaimer](/about/disclaimer) for the longer note.

## The 2018 paper

The pharmacological line of research has run alongside the cognitive-behavioral one since the late 1990s, with much of the early work conducted at the Lucidity Institute in Hawaii under LaBerge's direction.

The most rigorous published trial is the **LaBerge, LaMarca, and Baird** study of 2018, "Pre-sleep treatment with galantamine stimulates lucid dreaming: A double-blind, placebo-controlled, crossover study," published in *PLOS ONE*.

**The paper is short, the design is clean, and the result is large.**

It is worth setting out the abstract directly because the paper's claim is best made in its own compressed form:

> Lucid dreaming is a remarkable state of consciousness in which one is aware of the fact that one is dreaming while continuing to dream. *Based on the strong relationship between physiological activation during rapid eye-movement sleep and lucid dreaming, our pilot research investigated whether enhancing cortical activation via acetylcholinesterase inhibition (AChEI) would increase the frequency of lucid dreams* and found AChEI to be a promising method for lucid dream induction. In the current study we sought to quantify the size and reliability of the effect of AChEI on lucid dreaming. … Participants (N = 121) with high dream recall and an interest in lucid dreaming were randomly assigned counterbalanced orders of 3 doses of galantamine (0, 4 and 8 mg). On 3 consecutive nights, they awoke approximately 4.5 hours after lights out, recalled a dream, ingested the capsules and stayed out of bed for at least 30 minutes. Participants then returned to bed and practiced the Mnemonic Induction of Lucid Dreams technique while returning to sleep. *The percentage of participants who reported a lucid dream was significantly increased for both 4 mg (27%, odds ratio = 2.29) and 8 mg doses (42%, odds ratio = 4.46) compared to the active placebo procedure (14%).* (LaBerge, LaMarca, and Baird 2018)

The compressed form contains everything needed.

## The pharmacology

The drug is galantamine, an acetylcholinesterase inhibitor approved clinically for Alzheimer's disease. The acute pharmacology is that it slows the metabolic breakdown of acetylcholine in the central nervous system, increasing the amount of acetylcholine available at cholinergic synapses.

Acetylcholine is one of the principal neuromodulators of REM sleep. Brainstem cholinergic neurons are central to REM initiation; "*microinjection of the ACh agonist carbachol in the pons elicits extended periods of REM sleep, and many of the neurons critical to REM sleep are responsive to ACh*" (LaBerge 2018).

What galantamine does at the doses used in this study is to enhance phasic activation during REM — increased eye movement density, shorter REM latency, more vivid and remembered dream content.

On the working hypothesis that lucid dreams "tend to occur during periods of increased physiological activation during REM sleep," the prediction was that galantamine should increase the frequency of lucidity.

**The prediction was confirmed.**

## The integrated protocol

What is methodologically interesting about the 2018 study is that the galantamine intervention was not tested in isolation. It was layered onto an already-effective combination of MILD + WBTB:

> Each night, participants awoke after approximately 4.5 hours of sleep, recalled and memorized a dream, ingested the capsules, and then remained awake for the sleep interruption period while engaged in a quiet wakeful activity of their choice (e.g., reading about lucid dreaming or recording or reviewing their dreams). *Participants then returned to bed, practicing MILD until they fell asleep.* (LaBerge, LaMarca, and Baird 2018)

### The dose-response

The dose-response was clean.

- On the **0 mg (placebo)** condition, layered on top of MILD + WBTB, **14 percent** of nights produced a lucid dream — already a substantial increase over the 4 percent baseline rate the participants reported for the prior six months.
- On **4 mg** of galantamine, the rate doubled to **27 percent**.
- On **8 mg**, it tripled to **42 percent**.

**The effect was dose-related (linear trend, p < 0.000001) and was robust across age and gender.**

### First-time lucid dreamers

The study also demonstrated that the technique works for first-time lucid dreamers.

Ten participants reported never having experienced a lucid dream prior to the study, and four of them (40 percent) had their first lucid dream on the 8 mg condition — a success rate "*not different from the rest of the sample*."

### The dream-content analysis

The dream-content analysis in the paper deserves a separate mention.

Across all participants and all conditions, lucid dreams were associated with significantly higher levels of "*recall, cognitive clarity, control, positive emotion, sensory vividness and self-reflection on one's thoughts and feelings compared to non-lucid dreams*."

The Bayesian classification reported in the paper is striking: combining four content features — control, positive emotion, cognitive clarity, and sensory vividness — allowed lucid dreams to be classified with greater than 95 percent accuracy.

The result replicates what Voss, Schermelleh-Engel, Windt, Frenzel and Hobson had previously reported in their 2013 study using the LuCiD scale.

**Lucid dreams are not just labeled differently than non-lucid dreams; they have different phenomenology along a coherent set of dimensions, and the dimensions are stable across studies.**

## Limits, side-effect profile, ethical considerations

Galantamine is generally well tolerated at the doses tested.

The 2018 paper reports that 14 of 121 participants (12 percent) reported mild side-effects on an active dose — most commonly mild gastrointestinal upset, insomnia, or next-day fatigue — against 3 percent on the placebo condition. Five participants reported difficulty falling back to sleep; five reported mild gastrointestinal symptoms including nausea.

The paper notes that at higher doses, or in patients with cardiovascular sensitivity, more serious side-effects (bradycardia, in particular) are documented in the clinical Alzheimer's literature. It does not recommend doses higher than 8 mg without further study.

Two limitations of the 2018 design are worth flagging.

**The first** is that the study was conducted as part of an eight-day "Dreaming and Awakening" lucid-dream training workshop in Hawaii. The participants were a self-selected group of lucid-dream enthusiasts, with high baseline dream recall and high motivation. The protocol tested galantamine *in conjunction with* the workshop's intensive training.

The paper is explicit about this: "*It is again worth emphasizing that all of the statements regarding the effectiveness of galantamine in stimulating lucid dreams are in the specific context of a lucid dreaming training program in Hawaii*, which teaches participants to cultivate effective mental sets for lucid dream induction."

**The second** is that the experiment was conducted in a workshop setting, not in a sleep laboratory. There were no physiological recordings of lucid dreams or polysomnographic validation of participants' sleep stage. The lucidity classification is based on participant report, with the additional requirement that "dream reports had to include a reference to the current state as a dream."

## The ethical complexity

Galantamine is a prescription drug in many jurisdictions and an over-the-counter supplement in others (the 2018 study used Life Enhancement Products' "Galantamind").

Off-label use for dream enhancement in healthy populations is not approved by any regulatory body. The long-term safety profile in healthy adults using the drug periodically over years is not established. The drug interacts with beta-blockers, anti-cholinergics, and a range of other medications; the study's exclusion criteria included "asthma, taking beta-blockers, severe mental illness or cardiac arrhythmias."

The 2018 paper itself takes a measured tone: "*While more studies documenting the safety profile of this substance are needed, the potential of making lucid dreaming widely accessible also has implications for scientific research.*"

Most contemporary contemplative teachers — Holecek, Wallace, Tenzin Wangyal — are explicit that pharmacological induction is at best an adjunct and at worst a distraction from the contemplative work of cultivation.

Tenzin Wangyal in particular treats reliance on substances as something the practice must eventually move past.

## The science and the tradition emphasize different goods

The science and the practice tradition do not, on this point, contradict each other. **They emphasize different goods.**

Galantamine works. What it produces is a lucid dream that has been pharmacologically catalyzed. What the contemplative tradition aims at is a stable contemplative capacity that does not require the catalyst.

**Both are real, and a serious practitioner can decide where, if anywhere, the drug fits.**

The honest framing for a contemporary practitioner is something like this.

Galantamine is a real tool with a measurable effect and a manageable but not zero side-effect profile. It can produce lucid dreams in people who have never had them, and can substantially increase the rate in people who have.

It is not a substitute for the work of cultivation. The contemplative tradition's aim — stable awareness through the night, eventually through the deeper transitions, eventually through the death-process — is not a destination galantamine can deliver.

The galantamine-induced lucid dream is what it is: a real lucid dream, with the same phenomenological signature as any other lucid dream, available at the start of a practice arc that the practitioner then has to walk through with or without further pharmacological help.
