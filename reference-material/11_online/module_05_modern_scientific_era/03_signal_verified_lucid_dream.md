# Module 5, Lesson 3 — The Signal-Verified Lucid Dream (1978–1980)

*Two pre-arranged eye movements, executed from inside a confirmed REM episode. The contemplative tradition's central empirical claim, finally registered on an instrument outside the practitioner's body.*

---

## The state of the question, late 1970s

The contemplative tradition's claim that dream-awareness is real and trainable had not been tested in a laboratory because it was not, in the 1970s, generally believed in the laboratory.

LaBerge, beginning Ph.D. work at Stanford in the late 1970s, has described the climate plainly in *Exploring the World of Lucid Dreaming* (Stephen LaBerge and Howard Rheingold, Ballantine 1990): the experts of the period held that dreaming with the awareness that one was dreaming was a contradiction in terms and therefore impossible. Philosophical reasoning of that kind, LaBerge notes, could not convince a researcher who had himself had lucid dreams.

The objection — that consciousness within sleep is a contradiction in terms — was widely held in the dream-research community of the 1970s.

It had its sources both in the dominant Hobson-McCarley activation-synthesis framework, which held that REM dreaming was driven by random pontine activation that the cortex synthesized into apparent narrative without higher-order monitoring, and in the older clinical tradition that treated lucidity reports as confabulation, brief micro-awakenings, or pathology.

The objection was not stupid. It rested on a real difficulty.

A person reporting after waking that they had been aware of dreaming might be reporting a dream-content (a dreamed awareness, not an actual one), or a memory-error (the awareness arising during the report rather than during the dream), or a brief awakening that had been forgotten.

**Without independent evidence of the awareness's location *inside* a confirmed dream, the report was, scientifically, not strong enough to settle the question.**

What was needed was a way for the dreamer, while in the dream, to do something that could be detected on the polygraph trace and that no person not actually lucid in the dream could plausibly produce.

The trick was that the body in REM is paralyzed by descending inhibition from the brainstem. The sleeper cannot speak, cannot move arms or legs, cannot act in any of the ways that ordinary outside-the-dream signals would use.

**Two muscle groups, however, are not paralyzed: the muscles of respiration (the sleeper continues to breathe) and the extraocular muscles (which is what gives REM its name in the first place). The eye movements that define REM sleep are, in principle, available for voluntary use by a dreamer who is aware enough to use them.**

## Hearne and Worsley at Hull

The first laboratory verification of lucid dreaming was conducted by **Keith Hearne** at the University of Hull in 1975, working with the experienced lucid dreamer **Alan Worsley**.

The verification appeared in Hearne's 1978 PhD thesis ("Lucid Dreams: An Electro-Physiological and Psychological Study," University of Liverpool/Hull) and in subsequent journal publications.

The Hearne thesis itself is not in the project source bundle. What survives there is the discussion in LaBerge and Rheingold's book, which is generous and unambiguous.

LaBerge credits Worsley as "the first person to signal from a lucid dream in pioneering experiments carried out in collaboration with Keith Hearne" and describes Worsley as "*one of the world's most experienced lucid dreamers*," who "has been conducting personal lucid dream experiments since the age of five."

The Hearne-Worsley demonstration came in April 1975 in the Hull sleep laboratory. Worsley, while in confirmed REM sleep, executed a pre-arranged sequence of horizontal eye movements that had been agreed in waking, and the signal appeared on the polygraph trace.

Hearne's claim to scientific priority is firm. The fact that for several decades it was clouded by the earlier journal publication of LaBerge's parallel work is one of the small ironies of contemporary intellectual history.

**The present consensus is that Hearne and LaBerge did the same thing independently within a few years of each other, and both deserve the credit for the demonstration.**

## LaBerge at Stanford

LaBerge's own demonstration came in 1980 and is described in considerable detail in *Exploring the World of Lucid Dreaming*. The relevant passage opens with a poetic challenge — Coleridge's flower brought back from heaven — and then redescribes the challenge in operational terms. Reporting lucidity after waking, LaBerge writes, would not prove that the lucidity had occurred while the body was actually asleep:

> I had no doubt that lucid dreaming was a reality, but how could I prove it to anyone else? … Simply reporting I had been lucid in a dream after awakening wouldn't prove that the lucidity had occurred while I was actually asleep. I needed some way to mark the time of the lucid dream on a record showing that I had been asleep. … It occurred to me that I ought to be able to signal by moving my eyes in a pre-arranged, recognizable pattern. (LaBerge and Rheingold)

The insight rested on a fact already established in the literature: during ordinary REM, dreamers' physical eye movements were known to correspond, at least sometimes, with the direction in which the dreamer was looking within the dream. The extraocular muscles were not paralyzed by REM atonia. A practitioner aware enough to use them, and free to look anywhere within the dream, ought to be able to send a pre-arranged pattern out to the polygraph.

LaBerge spent the night at the Stanford Sleep Laboratory, wired with electrodes that recorded brain waves, eye movements, and muscle tone while his colleague Lynn Nagel monitored the polygraph. During that night he had a lucid dream in which, as agreed, he moved his eyes in a left-right-left-right sequence. The polygraph record, examined the next morning, contained the signals in the middle of a confirmed REM period.

The result was published as LaBerge, Nagel, Dement, and Zarcone, "Lucid Dreaming Verified by Volitional Communication during REM Sleep," *Perceptual and Motor Skills* 52 (1981): 727–732 — the first peer-reviewed publication of an objectively verified lucid dream in a major journal. LaBerge's 1980 Stanford dissertation, *Lucid Dreaming: An Exploratory Study of Consciousness during Sleep*, is the foundational scientific document.

## What was at stake in the demonstration

The demonstration's importance is more than the establishment of a single fact. Once the eye-signal paradigm was working, it became the methodological key to a research program.

The protocol's value, in LaBerge and Rheingold's account, is that lucid dreamers could remember to perform previously agreed-upon actions inside the dream and signal that performance to the waking world. With trained subjects, the eye-movement technique could be developed into a general methodology — a new approach to dream research that earlier work, lacking any from-inside marker, had not made available.

### Follow-on experiments the methodology made possible

**Time estimation in the dream** — subjects were asked to signal, count to ten by a standard protocol, then signal again — and "in all cases, we found time estimates made in lucid dreams were within a few seconds of estimates made in the waking state."

**The correspondence between dreamed gaze and physical eye movement**, confirmed with high precision.

**The physiology of dreamed action**: when lucid dreamers held their breath in the dream "they really do hold their breaths," and when they breathed fast in the dream "they really do pant."

**A series of studies on the physiological correlates of dreamed sexual activity** in lucid REM.

Each of these required the eye-signal protocol as the marking-method. The signal told the experimenter where in the polygraph trace the dream-event of interest was. Without it, no such fine-grained correlation between dream content and physiology could have been established.

## The philosophical implication

The wider implication, naturally, was philosophical as much as methodological. The experts had said that consciousness within dreaming was a contradiction in terms.

**The signal said it was not. After 1980 this was no longer in scientific dispute.**

It is, in retrospect, hard to overstate the significance of the moment. For two thousand years, the contemplative traditions had insisted that lucidity was real, trainable, and central to a developed practice. For perhaps a hundred years, the Western lucid-dream literature (Saint-Denys, van Eeden, Ouspensky, Fox) had reported the phenomenon in detail.

None of this had been received in the laboratory.

**Two pre-arranged eye movements changed that.**

The next lesson takes up the induction techniques the eye-signal demonstration enabled — MILD, WILD, WBTB — and the work LaBerge did through the 1980s and 1990s to establish that lucid dreaming was not just real but reliably trainable.
