# Module 3, Lesson 5 — Barzakh, Taʿbīr, and the Cosmic Dream

*Where dream sits in being; how dream is read; and why, on Ibn al-ʿArabī's deepest reading, the cosmos itself has the same mode of being that the dream has.*

---

## Barzakh: the structural place of dream

The technical name for the imaginal world's *position* — between spirit and body, between meaning and form — is *barzakh*, an Arabic word meaning isthmus or barrier. The Qur'an uses it in two principal places, and Ibn al-ʿArabī treats both usages as referring to the same structural reality: the boundary that both separates and joins two domains.

The most famous Qur'anic usage is in 25:53 (the meeting of two seas, "between them a barrier they do not overpass"), and the second is the eschatological *barzakh* — the intermediate state between death and resurrection.

Ibn al-ʿArabī, characteristically, treats these as continuous. The dream-world, the imaginal world, the post-mortem intermediate state are the same kind of ontological territory, accessed by different routes.

**Chittick gives the technical definition:**

> A barzakh is something that separates two other things while never going to one side, as for example the line that separates shadow from sunlight. ... The intelligible barrier is the barzakh. If it is perceived by the senses, it is one of the two things, not the barzakh. *Any two adjacent things are in need of a barzakh which is neither the one nor the other but which possesses the power of both.* The barzakh is something that separates a known from an unknown, an existent from a nonexistent, a negated from an affirmed, an intelligible from a non-intelligible. (*Futūḥāt* I 304.16, trans. Chittick, pp. 117–18)

This is the structural paragraph. The *barzakh* is a real third thing, a true intermediate; it has "the power of both" of the things it separates.

**The imaginal world *is* a *barzakh* — Ibn al-ʿArabī sometimes calls it *the* barzakh, *the* Supreme Barzakh — and dreams happen *in* it.**

The dreamer is in the imaginal world, accessed by the sleep-state's particular kind of attention, encountering imaginal beings who have their own mode of reality.

### The bardo / barzakh parallel

The structural parallel to the Tibetan *bardo* (Sanskrit *antarābhava*), which Module 2 covered, is striking.

The Sufi *barzakh* and the Tibetan *bardo* are not the same word and the traditions did not influence each other. They arrive, independently, at compatible accounts of an intermediate state with structural relations to both dream and the post-mortem condition.

This is one of the great unexplored comparative questions in contemplative studies, and the work can only mark it. **The convergence is real; what to make of it is open.**

## Sleep as transferal

Chittick continues, drawing on a chapter of the *Futūḥāt* (188) that Ibn al-ʿArabī devotes to *ru'yā* — the Arabic for "dream" or "vision":

> Dreams have a place, a locus, and a state. Their state is sleep (*nawm*), which is an absence from manifest sensory things that produces ease (*rāḥa*) because of the weariness (*taʿb*) which overcomes the soul in this plane in the state of wakefulness because of motion. ... God says, "*We appointed your sleep for a rest*" (Koran 78:9); in other words, We appointed sleep for you as an ease in which souls can relax. (*Futūḥāt* II 378.24, trans. Chittick, p. 119)

The register here is significant. The text could have begun with metaphysics; it begins instead with the practical theology of rest.

Sleep is, first of all, what God appointed for rest. Imagination is what happens *in* sleep, but the framing is mercy: the soul gets tired, sleep is the appointed remedy. The metaphysical superstructure is then built on this homely base.

**The text proceeds to distinguish two kinds of sleep — pure sleep, which is "only rest," and *transferal* (*intiqāl*), the kind of sleep within which dreams occur:**

> As for the sleep which is *transferal*, it is the kind within which there are dreams. *The instruments [of the soul] are transferred from the manifest side (ẓāhir) of sense perception to its nonmanifest side (bāṭin) in order to see what has become established in the Treasury of Imagination (khizānat al-khayāl)* — to which the senses have lifted up what they have acquired from sensory objects — and what has been formed by the form-giving faculty, which is one of the assistants of this Treasury. Thus the rational soul, to which God has given ownership of this city [of the human being], looks upon what has been placed in its Treasury, as is the habit of kings, who enter into their treasuries when they are alone to gain knowledge of what is in them. (*Futūḥāt* II 378.24, trans. Chittick, p. 119)

The Treasury of Imagination is among the most striking of Ibn al-ʿArabī's images. The senses by day deposit forms into a treasury; the form-giving faculty composes additional forms; the soul, asleep at night, enters the treasury alone, the way a king enters his treasury, and looks at what is there.

The kingly metaphor is not casual. **The soul's relation to its imaginal contents is one of *sovereignty*.** The residues are there, but the entry into the treasury is an act of governance.

## Dreams as taʿbīr — the science of crossing-over

The Sufi treatment of dream is, in the first place, a metaphysics of imagination of which dream-symbolism is one application. The symbolic register is also fully present, and Ibn al-ʿArabī treats it carefully.

**Chittick's gloss:**

> People know that dreams need interpretation (*taʿbīr*). *The word taʿbīr derives from the root ʿ.b.r., which signifies "crossing over," hence, to traverse, to ford, to pass.* The interpreter (*muʿabbir*) is he who passes from the sensory form of the dream to the meaning which has put on the clothing of form. From the same root we have "*ʿibāra*" or "[verbal] expression," which is a passage from understanding to exposition. (Chittick, p. 119)

**The verb of interpretation is the verb of crossing.**

The interpreter is a passer-over. To interpret a dream is to cross from the sensory form back to the meaning that took on the sensory form.

This is the same root that occurs in the Qur'anic *iʿtibār* — "take heed," literally "cross over" — and Ibn al-ʿArabī treats the connection as substantive. The whole Qur'anic call to take heed of natural and historical signs is, on his reading, an invitation to read the manifest world *as a dream*, crossing from its forms to the meanings they enclose.

The well-known *ḥadīth* of the Prophet — that he drank a cup of milk in dream until his fingertips were saturated, gave the rest to ʿUmar, and when asked to interpret the dream replied, "Knowledge" — sits inside this framework. Milk in dream is knowledge. The interpretation is the recovery of the meaning that put on the form.

## The cosmic dream

The deepest of Ibn al-ʿArabī's moves on dream — and the one most often quoted in the comparative literature — is his treatment of the cosmos itself as God's dream.

**Chittick brings this out by quoting a sequence of passages from the *Futūḥāt*:**

> The clearest access shared by all human beings to the nature of existence, which is "everything other than God," is our own imagination, especially dreams. The more deeply we delve into our own imagination, the more clearly we see that its characteristics coincide with those of existence itself. *Just as our imagination is the barzakh between our spirits and bodies, so also existence is the barzakh between Being and nothingness.* Everything that we observe in imagination on a microcosmic scale takes place on a macrocosmic scale in the Nondelimited World of Imagination, which is existence. Just as the world we observe in dreams is spiritual and corporeal, intelligible and sensory, meaning and form, so also the world that God observes in His "dream" is built of Being and nothingness. *When we wake up and want to understand our dreams, we try to interpret them or go to an interpreter to do this for us. So also, when we die and thereby "wake up" to the cosmic dream of God, we will find the interpretation of our dream* (even though that "waking up" is itself another stage in the cosmic dream). (Chittick, p. 113, drawing on *Futūḥāt* III 198.23)

The structural rhyme is exact. **As the dreamer is to the dream, so God is to the cosmos.**

The cosmos has the same imaginal-mode being as the dream. To die is to wake up *to* the cosmic dream — and not out of it. Even the post-mortem awakening is itself a stage in a more comprehensive imagining.

The line of argument runs all the way down without bottom; every "waking" turns out to be a more sober dream.

## "People are asleep, and when they die they awake"

The Prophet's saying ***People are asleep, and when they die they awake*** (*al-nās niyām fa-idhā mātū intabahū*) — among the most-cited *ḥadīth*s in the Sufi tradition's literature on dream — does the doctrinal work.

What is called waking is a kind of sleep relative to a more comprehensive awareness that becomes available at death. The Sufi practitioner's discipline is to *anticipate* that more comprehensive awareness within life — to cross over the manifest forms to the meanings they imply.

This is, structurally, the same move the Mahāyāna made in Module 1 with Śāntideva. *All form is like a dream*, the *Bodhicaryāvatāra* had said. The Sufi tradition arrives at a compatible claim by a different route, with a different vocabulary, and with a different metaphysical framing.

Ibn al-ʿArabī's *barzakh* is a positive ontological domain in a way the Madhyamaka's *śūnyatā* is not, but the practical orientation is recognizably continuous.
