# Module 3, Lesson 4 — Ibn al-ʿArabī and Khayāl: Imagination as Ontology

*The most philosophically developed treatment of dream in any pre-modern tradition.*

---

The most philosophically developed treatment of dream in any pre-modern tradition is the work of the Andalusian Sufi master **Muḥyī al-Dīn Ibn al-ʿArabī** (1165–1240), called *al-Shaykh al-Akbar* — "the Greatest Shaykh" — by the long tradition of his readers.

His vast corpus, principally the *Futūḥāt al-Makkiyya* ("Meccan Openings," running to thousands of pages in Arabic) and the more compact *Fuṣūṣ al-Ḥikam* ("Bezels of Wisdom"), develops a metaphysics of imagination that has, for the dream-practice tradition, no equivalent elsewhere.

The standard scholarly engagement with the *Futūḥāt* in English is **William Chittick**'s *The Sufi Path of Knowledge: Ibn al-ʿArabī's Metaphysics of Imagination* (SUNY, 1989). It is through Chittick's translations and analytical glosses that this lesson and the next will work.

## A note on the difficulty

A short word about the difficulty before the lesson begins. Ibn al-ʿArabī is, with Aquinas, with Maimonides, with Śaṅkara, with Tsongkhapa, one of the great systematic theologian-philosophers of the medieval world.

His vocabulary builds up across many texts, each term defined in relation to many others, and the central moves take many readings to come into view. Difficulty is part of the reading.

The reward — and there is one — is that the framework Ibn al-ʿArabī builds is *substantively different* from anything else the work has covered, and it dignifies the dream in a way the other traditions do not.

## Khayāl: imagination as ontological category

The core move of Ibn al-ʿArabī's metaphysics of dream is that *khayāl* — imagination — is an ontological category rather than a psychological faculty in the modern Western sense.

***Imagination is not a private mental event; imagination is what kind of being certain things have.***

There is a domain of reality — a *world* — whose mode of existence is imaginal: neither material in the gross sense, nor purely intellectual or spiritual, but something genuinely intermediate.

Things in this domain *are*; they are real; but they are not real the way a stone is real and not real the way a logical proposition is real. They are real the way the figures in a dream are real to the dreamer who is in the dream.

## The three levels of cosmos

Chittick frames the move structurally. The cosmos, on Ibn al-ʿArabī's account, is divided into three principal levels.

The **World of Spirits** (*ʿālam al-arwāḥ*) is the level of pure intelligibility, of meanings without sensory form, of what one might in another vocabulary call the noumenal.

The **World of Bodies** (*ʿālam al-ajsām*) is the level of gross materiality, of stones and trees and bodies, of what is ordinarily called physical reality.

Between them — and this is the move — is the **World of Imagination** (*ʿālam al-khayāl* or, in the more famous variant terminology, *ʿālam al-mithāl*, the World of Image-Likenesses).

The imaginal world has the same ontological dignity as the other two. It is a real domain at which spiritual realities take perceptible form.

**Chittick's gloss:**

> Those Muslim thinkers who deal with the imaginal world — and there are many, as Corbin's researches have helped to show — *love to point to dreams as our most direct and common experience of its ontological status*. In the dream world, the things we perceive share in the luminosity of our own consciousness, yet they are presented to us as corporeal and dense things, not as disembodied spirits. Since the World of Spirits manifests directly the unity of the divine, angels have no "parts," while the world of corporeal things appears to us as indefinite multiplicity. *But the world of dreams combines unity and multiplicity.* A single dreaming subject perceives a multiplicity of forms and things that in fact are nothing but his own single self. Their manyness is but the mode that the one consciousness assumes in displaying various facets of itself. (Chittick, *Sufi Path of Knowledge*, p. 116)

The dream is, for Ibn al-ʿArabī, the most common human experience of an imaginal-world ontology.

**Every human being has direct, repeatable, ordinary access to this kind of being every night. The argument can therefore proceed from a shared phenomenon, not from an esoteric premise.**

## The mirror analogy

The technical specification of imagination's status is given in a passage from the *Futūḥāt* (I 304.16) that Chittick translates as a self-standing definition. The passage is unusual for its relative simplicity, by the standards of the *Futūḥāt*, and is worth quoting in full:

> *Imagination is neither existent nor nonexistent, neither known nor unknown, neither negated nor affirmed.* For example, a person perceives his form in a mirror. He knows for certain that he has perceived his form in one respect and he knows for certain that he has not perceived his form in another respect. ... He cannot deny that he has seen his form, and he knows that his form is not in the mirror, nor between himself and the mirror. ... Hence he is neither a truth-teller nor a liar in his words, "I saw my form, I did not see my form." (*Futūḥāt* I 304.16, trans. Chittick, p. 117)

The mirror image is the cleanest analogy. The face in the mirror is real; it is also not real; it is real *as image*.

**To call it real is to overstate; to call it unreal is also to overstate.**

## He / not He

Ibn al-ʿArabī's whole vocabulary of imagination — including its dream-mode — is built to handle this kind of *both/and* ontology.

**Chittick's technical name for it in the *Futūḥāt* is *He/not He* (*huwa lā huwa*), and Ibn al-ʿArabī develops it as the basic structure of the cosmos itself:**

> According to Ibn al-ʿArabī, *the reality of "He/not He" finds its clearest expression in the cosmos through imagination (khayāl)*. In dreaming, for example, which is a function of imagination, a person sees corporeal things which are not corporeal things. The objects he sees possess corporeal forms, yet they dwell not in the world of corporeal bodies, but in that imaginal world which is the soul. *Imagination can take a "meaning" (maʿnā) — that is, a reality of the world of intelligible things without any outward form — and give to it a sensory form (ṣūra maḥsūsa).* ... This occurs in spite of the fact that in normal circumstances "meanings" and "sensory forms" are mutually exclusive. (Chittick, p. 116, summarizing *Futūḥāt* II 66.14)

The dream's metaphysical interest, then, is that it is a domain in which intelligibility and sensibility are joined. A meaning becomes an image; an angel becomes a person; God's name becomes a youth (the Prophet's reported saying, "I saw my Lord in the form of a youth," is one of the *ḥadīth*s Ibn al-ʿArabī cites repeatedly).

The dream is the standard exhibit of an imaginal mode of being, and the fact that everyone has direct and repeated experience of it is precisely what makes it serviceable for theology.

The next lesson takes up the *barzakh* — the structural place where the dream sits — and the practical attitudes toward dream that follow from this metaphysics: *taʿbīr*, the science of dream-interpretation as crossing-over; the dream as a kind of prophecy; and the cosmos itself as God's dream.
