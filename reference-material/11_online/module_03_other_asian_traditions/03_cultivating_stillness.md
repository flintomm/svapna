# Module 3, Lesson 3 — Cultivating Stillness: The Daoist Register

*The* Tài Shàng Qīngjìng Jīng *— Cultivating Stillness — and the inner-alchemy framework that the Daoist sleep-cultivation tradition presupposed.*

---

## The text and its dating

The second Daoist source the project bundle supports is Eva Wong's translation of the *Tài Shàng Qīngjìng Jīng* (*Cultivating Stillness*), a short canonical text from the Six Dynasties era (220–589 CE), printed with an illustrated commentary attributed to Shui-ch'ing Tzu and dated, by Wong's analysis, to the late Ming or early Ch'ing dynasty.

The text itself is short — twenty-four segments — and the commentary is the locus where the *neidan* (internal alchemy) framework comes into the open. What Wong's translation contributes to a comparative dream-yoga history is the philosophical and energetic background against which the Daoist sleep traditions developed and which any of those traditions presupposes.

The text's central concern is the cultivation of *stillness* — of body, mind, and the three inner substances *jīng* (essence), *qì* (vital energy), and *shén* (spirit) — and its claim is that the practitioner who attains stillness in waking has done the prior work that nighttime cultivation will then complete.

Wong's translator's introduction is unusually candid about the textual situation. *Cultivating Stillness* is "from the Taoist canon," composed in the literary form of the Warring States period to "highlight its philosophical closeness to the *Tao-te Ching*"; it shows "strong influence of internal alchemy characteristic of the Eastern Han Period (25–220 C.E.)" while predating the proliferation of esoteric symbolism that the Tang dynasty would bring.

Two things follow from this dating that matter for the contemporary reader.

**First**, the text is the philosophical-energetic foundation that the Tang and Song dynasty Daoist sleep traditions — the developed *neidan* synthesis associated with Chen Tuan and the Quanzhen school — would build on. It is therefore a reasonable starting point for understanding what the Daoist contemplative tradition was doing with sleep and dream, even though the specific sleep-practice literature is not in the bundle.

**Second**, the text places the question of the spirit (*shén*) at the center of contemplative practice, and treats waking life as the principal condition under which the spirit is steadily lost. This claim is what gives the Daoist treatment its distinctive register, against the Indian-Buddhist treatment.

## The spirit and its dissipation

**Here is the central passage on this in Wong's rendering:**

> The existence of the spirit is connected with the existence of the body. The spirit is born and dies with the human body. ... Knowledge is born when the infant utters a cry and falls to the ground. Here the spirit becomes attached to the body. Then knowledge enters the infant's body to dwell with the spirit in the mind. From then on, knowledge controls the mind and the spirit loses its place. The seven emotions and the six desires arise. Day and night the spirit dissipates until it disappears. *Even if you live to over one hundred, life is still a dream.* At death the ghosts will escort the spirit to hell. (Wong, *Cultivating Stillness*, ch. 19, "The Spirit")

The line that does the comparative work here is *life is still a dream* (*jīn shēng yě shì yī mèng* in the Chinese original). It is the figurative bridge to the broader Daoist treatment of waking life as itself dreamlike when the spirit has been allowed to dissipate.

The point operates in a *physiological-spiritual* register rather than a strictly metaphysical one (the way the Mahāyāna's "all form is like a dream" is metaphysical).

**A life lived without the cultivation of *shén* is a life lived in something close to dream, in the sense that the practitioner is not present to it; the same body acts, but no one is home.**

The cultivation of stillness — *qīngjìng* — is therefore, on this account, what the dreamlike condition of unrecollected human life is being cured of.

## The spirit's preference for purity

**The commentary's chapter on "the spirit tends toward purity" gives the operative claim:**

> *The spirit tends toward purity and stillness.* Knowledge demands the spirit's response and disturbs it. Therefore the spirit weakens and a hundred illnesses arise. ... If you do not stop craving, the spirit will depart. When the spirit departs the six thieves will disturb the heart.

The *Qingjing Jing*'s contribution to dream-and-sleep cultivation in this register is structural rather than technical. It tells the practitioner that the work of stillness is the work of holding the spirit in its place; that the spirit is constantly being drawn out by sensory and emotional engagement; that the recovery of stillness is what the inner-alchemical sequence (*jīng* into *qì*, *qì* into *shén*, *shén* into *xū* — emptiness) consists in.

Sleep is one stretch of the night and day across which the same cultivation is either preserved or lost.

## Stillness and movement

Wong renders the next stage with a directness that the more obscure *neidan* treatises rarely achieve:

> *Sitting quietly and not moving is stillness. Craving is movement.* If you are filled with desire and your senses are attached to objects, the heart is not still. Losing generative energy in sexual activity is movement. Storing generative energy is stillness. If you are free of cravings, in stillness you will see the mystery within. (Wong, *Cultivating Stillness*, ch. 2 "Wang-chi")

The Daoist sleep-cultivation tradition, in its classical form, presupposes this analysis. What Chen Tuan's twenty-four sleep exercises (*mián gōng èrshí-sì shì*) operationalize in postures and timed visualizations across the night is, in the *Qingjing Jing* register, the same cultivation the practitioner has been doing in waking, now carried through the body's transition into and out of sleep.

Sleep is the principal site at which the *jīng* (generative energy) is most easily lost — through ordinary nocturnal emission, through restlessness, through dreams that re-enact the sensory engagements of the day — and the principal site at which it can also be deliberately preserved and refined.

## Chen Tuan, named and located

A note on what is named and what is not. Wong's translator's introduction is the only place in the extracted file where Chen Tuan appears by name, and the appearance is consequential.

**Wong's introduction places Chen Tuan precisely where the comparative dream-yoga literature will be most interested in him:**

> Chen Hsi-i was a Sung dynasty Taoist hermit who resided on Huashan. *He was reputed to have originated unique forms of sleeping postures of chi-kung*, the internal martial art form known as *Hsin-i Liu He Pa Fa Ch'uan* (the Six Harmonies and Eight Methods Form), the celestial divination system *Tzu-wei Tu-su*, and to have written various treatises on Taoist cosmology and internal alchemy. (Wong, *Cultivating Stillness*, translator's introduction)

That sentence, more than any other in the extracted file, is the bridge between the *Qingjing Jing* and the developed dream-and-sleep practice tradition. Chen Tuan's "sleeping postures of chi-kung" — the pairing of bodily position with energetic cultivation across the night — is the most-cited Daoist contribution to a developed sleep yoga.

The bundle does not contain Chen Tuan's own writings; what it contains is the Quanzhen-lineage philosophical text that holds Chen Tuan's pedagogical position, and Wong's careful note locating him.

## What the Daoist tradition contributes

What the Daoist tradition contributes to the comparative picture, taken from these two sources together, is recognizable.

**The dream is the symptom; *stillness* is the central concern.**

The dream and the dreamlike condition of unrecollected waking life are symptoms of the same loss of *shén*; the cultivation of stillness recovers the spirit and, in doing so, reorganizes both.

Where the Tibetan tradition develops a dedicated dream yoga as one of the Six Yogas, the Daoist tradition develops dream-and-sleep practice as one application of a broader program of *neidan* whose center is elsewhere. The aim is the stable spirit.

The Daoist conviction that a practitioner who has not stabilized the spirit in waking will not stabilize it in sleep gives Daoist sleep cultivation its characteristic temporal structure. **The night, in this register, is the body's most demanding test of what the day has built.**

In this respect the Daoist orientation rhymes structurally with the Dzogchen "practice of natural light" treated in Module 2 — the conviction that what carries through the descent into sleep is the awareness already present in the waking practice.

The metaphysical framings (Daoist *shén* and *qì*; Dzogchen *rigpa*) are different in vocabulary, and the two traditions did not, so far as the surviving record suggests, talk to each other directly until the modern period. But the structural parallel is real.
