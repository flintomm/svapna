# Welcome

*An orientation lesson before the work begins.*

---

What follows is a survey of the science and history of dream yoga, lucid dreaming, and the cultivation of awareness across the states of consciousness. Before the first module begins, a few words on what the work is and how it is built.

The work is a long backstory of both kinds at once. The practice modules of the Svapna curriculum sit elsewhere and assume the historical and conceptual ground this work covers. What follows here, instead, is the two and a half millennia of contemplative thought and philosophical argument *and* the seven decades of laboratory science out of which the contemporary practice has finally emerged. The two halves are given their own weight. Module 5 is the science. Modules 1 through 4 are the history. Module 6 is where the two meet. The work tells that story in the order it actually happened: the Upaniṣadic seers in eighth-century-BCE India asking what the dream reveals about consciousness; the Indian Buddhist analysts taxonomizing the causes and conditions of dream-experience; the Mahāyāna teachers using dream as the working figure for emptiness; the Tibetan synthesis that became dream yoga proper; the Daoist Chinese and Sufi Islamic traditions developing parallel metaphysics of the imaginal; the long European nineteenth century rediscovering empirically what the Asian traditions had been working out for centuries; the twentieth-century laboratory verification of lucid dreaming; and the contemporary convergence in which the contemplative and scientific traditions are, finally, in sustained contact.

Six modules. Forty-eight short lessons. Each lesson is between roughly six hundred and fifteen hundred words, designed for a five-to-eight-minute read. The arc is cumulative — later lessons assume the vocabulary and arguments laid down by earlier ones — and the chronology is part of the argument. The Indian seers ask one question; the Greek philosophers ask a different one; the Buddhist analysts work out a third; the Tibetan tradition draws on the first three to produce something that none of them, on its own, could have produced.

The lessons are dense, written in the same sustained prose register as the source material the work is built on. The work takes pains to make the material accessible — by introducing technical vocabulary in context, by quoting the primary sources at length so the texts can be heard in their own voice, and by citing the underlying source wherever a passage has been compressed.

A note on names and languages. The work draws on five languages of source material: Sanskrit, Pāli, Tibetan, Greek, and Arabic. Where a technical term carries weight in the original, it is given in transliteration on first occurrence and in English thereafter. The terms are collected for reference in the [glossary](glossary.md). The lessons reintroduce them where they matter. Diacritics are preserved in the transliterations because the diacritics are part of how the words actually sound and how scholars distinguish them from related terms.

A note on translation. Where the work quotes a passage from the primary sources, it quotes the translator's actual wording. The translator is named on first occurrence in the lesson, and the citation appears at the end of the relevant paragraph. This is the work's most important technical commitment. The Mahāyāna tradition's "all form is like a dream" is a particular wording in the Padmakara translation of Śāntideva; Bṛhadāraṇyaka 4.3's claim that the dreamer "creates for himself" carriages and rivers is Olivelle's translation choice; van Eeden's definition of the lucid dream is van Eeden's own English. The work quotes the primary sources wherever quotation is possible.

A note on what the work is. It is the history of a conversation — the conversation that Buddhism, Hinduism, Daoism, Sufism, and contemplative neuroscience have been having about the dream — and of how that conversation assembled the contemporary practice landscape. The work asks for each tradition to be taken seriously on its own terms, in the order in which the traditions actually developed, and for the convergences — when they come — to be noticed for how unexpected some of them are.

A note on time. The work was written and is being read in 2026. Many of its central figures are still alive and still teaching. B. Alan Wallace, Andrew Holecek, Tenzin Wangyal Rinpoche, Khenchen Thrangu Rinpoche; Stephen LaBerge; Evan Thompson. The convergence the work's final module covers is a convergence still in progress. What follows, in those late lessons, is an honest account of an experiment that is roughly thirty-five years old and whose long-term outcome is not yet visible.
