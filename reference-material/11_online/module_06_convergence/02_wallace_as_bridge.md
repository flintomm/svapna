# Module 6, Lesson 2 — B. Alan Wallace as the Bridge Figure

*Who he is, what he set out to do, and why his Dreaming Yourself Awake is the right book to build the convergence around.*

---

## A two-decade collaboration

**Wallace introduces himself in the introduction to *Dreaming Yourself Awake* with characteristic plainness.** He met Stephen LaBerge at Stanford in 1992,

> "*when I was a graduate student in religious studies at Stanford. My research centered on the contemplative development of attention.* When Stephen and I talked about our research, *we both saw immediately that our work was complementary*. Beginning in the late 1990s I began collaborating with Stephen in ten-day public workshops that included training of the attention and dream practice."

The collaboration is the personal fact behind the book. The book is what twenty years of working alongside the leading laboratory researcher in the field, while training and teaching from the dream-yoga side of the lineage, finally produced.

The dream-yoga side of Wallace's training is the older of the two by some margin. It begins, by his own account, in 1978:

> My first encounter with dream yoga came in 1978, when I acted as a translator for Westerners attending teachings on dream yoga by Zong Rinpoche, an eminent Tibetan lama. He explained that dream yoga is one of a group of advanced practices called the Six Yogas of Naropa and that *it requires a strong foundation in meditation*. Following that advice, I engaged in foundational practices before attempting dream yoga. In 1990 I received dream yoga instruction from another revered Tibetan teacher, Gyatrul Rinpoche. Two years later a friend requested that I teach him dream yoga. *I asked Gyatrul Rinpoche if I should teach it, and he gave me his permission.* (Wallace)

Wallace had at that point been a Tibetan Buddhist monk for fourteen years (1971 through 1985), a translator for the Dalai Lama and other senior teachers, and a serious shamatha practitioner.

**The dream-yoga instruction sits inside that biography, not on top of it.**

## The book's stated purpose

The book's stated purpose is also plain. **Wallace writes early in the introduction:**

> "*Dreaming Yourself Awake* integrates the two most effective approaches to dream practice — lucid dreaming, as developed and enhanced by the science of psychology, and the dream yoga of Tibetan Buddhism. *Together they will bring you to a life-changing awakening.*"

The aim is not synthesis in the sense of dissolving the two practices into a third, hybrid technique; nor is it translation in the sense of recasting one in the other's vocabulary.

**It is integration in a specific sense: the two practices retain their distinctive aims and methods, but they are taught and practiced together, "the two complement[ing] one another."**

## The architecture as argument

The structural decision that follows from this aim is worth marking.

The book has three parts.

**Part One ("Lucid Dreaming")** gives a careful exposition of the science and method as Wallace understands them, including LaBerge's findings, the standard induction techniques, the architecture of REM sleep, and the dream signs / state checks / WBTB / mnemonic-induction repertoire.

**Part Two ("Dream Yoga")** moves to Padmasambhava's *Natural Liberation* terma cycle and develops the daytime and nighttime practices of Tibetan dream yoga proper.

**Part Three ("Bringing It All Together")** then asks what becomes possible when a practitioner has developed both sets of skills.

The architecture is itself the argument. Wallace is saying, in the form of the book, that the two practices belong in the same training arc — lucid dreaming first, because it is more accessible and the relevant infrastructure is simpler; dream yoga second, because it asks more of the practitioner and is fruitless without prior shamatha foundations; and then the integration, in which the lucid dream becomes the laboratory in which dream yoga's actual work gets done.

## The methodological move

Two further claims by Wallace are worth quoting, because they recur as load-bearing assumptions through the rest of the book and therefore through the rest of this module.

First, on the empirical character of the dream-yoga tradition itself. Wallace argues that dream yoga and lucid-dream science are not actually as far apart in epistemic register as they look from the outside.

Both are, on Wallace's reading, empirical disciplines that test hypotheses through replicable methods. They differ on what counts as a method and what counts as a result, but the spirit is the same:

> *Dream yoga has been passed down for a millennium within an empirically based tradition* where the student follows the guidance of an experienced and knowledgeable teacher in the transformation and exploration of states of consciousness. Although scientific instruments are not used to prove the experiences of dream yoga, *they are replicable and can be made public through the probing questions of the teacher and the detailed descriptions of the student*. Anyone who wishes to make the effort can then experience them for themselves. This, then, is an "objective" or "scientific" approach on a par with LaBerge's proof of lucid dreaming. *His experience was purely subjective — no one else could directly observe it.* His ingenious method of demonstrating he was both asleep and conscious is in the same class as the empirical methods used by dream yogis to verify their subjective experiences. (Wallace, ch. 2)

This passage is doing real work. Wallace is denying the cliché — common on both sides — that the contemplative tradition is "subjective" while the laboratory tradition is "objective."

Both, he argues, are first-person disciplines. LaBerge's eye-signal experiment did not directly observe LaBerge's lucid awareness; it observed an eye movement that LaBerge produced, in dream, on the basis of a pre-arranged plan. The eye movement is the public record. The awareness it points to is, like every contemplative claim, available only to the practitioner who develops the relevant capacity.

**The methodological difference between LaBerge in the lab and a Tibetan yogi in retreat is not the difference between objective and subjective; it is the difference between two ways of making private experience publicly accountable.**

This is, for Wallace, the methodological premise on which the whole convergence rests. It is also the most important single move the book makes, and the one most easily missed in a quick reading.

The contemplative tradition is not a less rigorous version of science. It is its own discipline of first-person observation, with its own methods of replicability (teacher-student dialogue, careful first-person description, comparison across practitioner reports), and its own forms of accountability.

## What awakening means

Second, on what awakening means.

**Wallace draws — early in chapter five, "The Universe of Dream Yoga" — the distinction that for him is the deepest difference between the two traditions:**

> *The most dramatic difference between lucid dreaming and dream yoga hinges on what it means to be lucid or awake.* The Western scientific tradition from which psychological theories and practices of lucid dreaming developed considers the normal waking state to be as awake as one can get: at night we go to sleep and enter into an "unreal" dreamworld. In the morning when we awaken we once again perceive reality more or less "as it is." That's all there is to it. We may become lucid while dreaming, but that awakening is not viewed as substantially different from the mental clarity of daytime. One is simply awake to the dreaming process.
>
> *Buddhism, however, considers normal waking consciousness itself to be a dream state relative to our deepest dimension of consciousness.* The Buddhist hypothesis here is that among three general states of consciousness — waking, sleeping, and dreaming — *the coarsest state of consciousness, the one with the least potential for spiritual development, with the least malleability, is, surprisingly, the ordinary waking state.* (Wallace, ch. 5)

This is the move that Module 1's reading of the *Bodhicaryāvatāra* set up, philosophically, twelve hundred years ago — Śāntideva's claim that the deceptiveness of dream-presence and dream-absence is structurally identical to the deceptiveness of waking-presence and waking-absence.

Wallace is now operationalizing that claim as a practice difference.

For the lucid-dream tradition, becoming lucid in dream means recognizing the present state as a dream rather than waking. The waking state remains the home base from which the dream is recognized.

For the dream-yoga tradition, becoming lucid in dream is the entry exercise for becoming lucid in waking — that is, for recognizing that the conviction one has of waking-as-real is itself a function of being in waking-state, and not a function of waking-state being more real.

**The dream is the working analogy. The whole life is what eventually has to be seen through.**

## Why Wallace is the right author for the bridge

The two passages together — the methodological one and the soteriological one — are what make Wallace the right author for the bridge.

He is willing to defend the empirical character of the contemplative tradition without softening its philosophical and ethical demands; and he is willing to take the science seriously as science without conceding to it the question of what kind of awakening matters in the end.

This is unusual. Many writers on the convergence — including, as Wallace gently flags, some Buddhist teachers writing in English — go soft on one side or the other. **Wallace does not.**
