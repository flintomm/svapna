# Module 6, Lesson 1 — What This Module Is For

*Where the contemplative and scientific traditions meet, and where they refuse to.*

---

## Two long arcs, finally in contact

The first five modules traced two long arcs that, for most of their history, ran in parallel without contact.

The Indian seers of the Upaniṣads, the Buddhist analysts of the Pāli canon, the Mahāyāna teachers of the *Bodhicaryāvatāra*, the Tibetan lineages of milam and the bardo — together produced, by the end of the second millennium, a contemplative discipline whose stated aim is the recognition of the illusory nature of all experience and the stabilization of awareness through every transition the mind undergoes.

The Western dream-science arc — Saint-Denys, van Eeden, Hearne, LaBerge, Tholey — produced, by the late twentieth century, an empirically verified picture of lucid dreaming as a trainable hybrid state of consciousness.

**The two arcs were developed independently by people who, for the most part, did not read each other's work.**

The convergence is recent. It is also, as of the mid-2020s, incomplete. This module is the module that asks what has actually happened when the two arcs meet, and what the practitioner of the next generation inherits from that meeting.

## Wallace as the bridge text

The module is built around one book — **B. Alan Wallace**'s *Dreaming Yourself Awake* (Shambhala, 2012) — because that book, more than any other in the source bundle, is the bridge text.

Wallace is unusually well placed to write it: a Stanford-trained scholar who spent fourteen years as a Tibetan Buddhist monk, the founder of the Santa Barbara Institute for Consciousness Studies, a translator of substantial Tibetan philosophical material, and — by his own account — a long-time interlocutor of Stephen LaBerge, with whom he has co-led ten-day workshops on attention and dream practice since the late 1990s.

The book gives serious shamatha instruction, serious lucid-dream technique, and serious dream-yoga teachings drawn from Padmasambhava's *Natural Liberation* cycle, in roughly that order — without simplification on either side.

The rest of this module reads with him and beside him, returning where useful to **Andrew Holecek** and **Tenzin Wangyal Rinpoche** — the two contemporary Western-facing teachers in the source bundle most directly engaged in the same dialogue.

## The module's structure

**Lesson 2** — Wallace as the bridge figure. Who he is, what he set out to do, and why the book is what the contemporary integration needed.

**Lesson 3** — What dream yoga adds to the lucid-dreaming program: an aim beyond lucidity itself. The first and most consequential of Wallace's four moves.

**Lesson 4** — The illusory body, sleep yoga, and the bardo framing. The remaining three moves, taken together. What the Tibetan tradition contributes that a methodology-only lucid-dream practice cannot reach by itself.

**Lesson 5** — What the science adds to the tradition. Empirical verification of lucidity; the architecture of REM sleep and the induction techniques; falsifiable physiological claims; the neurophenomenology of imagination, perception, and dream.

**Lesson 6** — Holecek and Wangyal in the same dialogue. Two other contemporary teachers working on the same convergence in different registers.

**Lesson 7** — The Mind and Life Institute and Evan Thompson. The institutional dialogue and the philosophical complement to Wallace's contemplative-practical treatment.

**Lesson 8** — Convergence, translation, synthesis. The work continues. What the next generation of practitioners inherits.

## What the source bundle does not support

A note on what the source bundle does not support.

The 2026-04-26 version of this material made substantial use of Francisco Varela's edited *Sleeping, Dreaming, and Dying* and of Evan Thompson's *Waking, Dreaming, Being*. No primary text from either source is in the current source bundle.

This module therefore handles Mind and Life only through Wallace's references and discusses Thompson only where Wallace himself does.

The institutional-dialogue side of the convergence is fully developed in Varela 1997/2002. The rigorous philosophical complement to Wallace is fully developed in Thompson 2015. Both are recommended; neither is in the bundle this work is built from.

## A note on register

A short word about register. Wallace is, by temperament, more philosophically argumentative than either Holecek or Wangyal.

The book contains long passages of explicit polemic — against neuroscientific reductionism, against the "Buddhist revisionists" who dismiss rebirth, against scientists who substitute "metaphysical assumptions for empirically validated hypotheses."

Some of this polemic will not age well; some of it will. Reading Wallace as a bridge text means inheriting the polemic too. It is part of the book's character, not a digression from it.

## The honest stance

**What the convergence asks of the contemporary practitioner is an honest stance, not a position.**

The contemplative tradition is to be taken seriously enough to do its practice, with its frame intact. The science is to be taken seriously enough that what it has empirically established is incorporated.

The points of convergence and the points of refusal both deserve careful notice. And the work of integration is, as of 2026, still in progress.

**The honest stance toward the convergence, in the present moment, is the stance of someone in the middle of a long project.** That is where this module ends.
