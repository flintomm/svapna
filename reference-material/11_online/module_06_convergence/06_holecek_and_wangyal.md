# Module 6, Lesson 6 — Holecek and Wangyal in the Same Dialogue

*Wallace is the central source. He is not the only one. Two other contemporary teachers in the source bundle — Andrew Holecek and Tenzin Wangyal Rinpoche — are working on the same convergence, in different registers, for different audiences.*

---

## Holecek: the on-ramp from the West

Holecek, writing more accessibly than Wallace, comes to the dialogue from a slightly different starting point.

Where Wallace's book moves from the lucid-dream science into the dream yoga and treats them as complementary practices, Holecek's *Dream Yoga* (Sounds True, 2016) treats the lucid-dream techniques as the contemporary on-ramp to dream yoga proper.

**The metaphor is his:** "*The classic practice texts are pithy and therefore steep. The great contribution of modern lucid dreaming is to provide a gradual on-ramp.*"

His framing of what makes dream yoga distinctive from lucid dreaming maps closely onto Wallace's, though in plainer language: "*Dream yoga unites you with deeper aspects of your being; it is more concerned with self-transcendence.*"

### The toolkit, taught without embarrassment

What Holecek brings to the convergence that Wallace does not is unselfconsciousness about teaching the lucid-dream methodology to a popular audience.

The article catalogs, with no apparent embarrassment, the entire toolkit: state checks, dreamsigns, prospective memory, MILD, WBTB, electronic aids (the NovaDreamer mask, the Goethe-University frontal-lobe stimulation findings), galantamine.

The article spends roughly half its length on these techniques. The other half is the dream-yoga reframing.

### Death signs as daytime practice

The reframing, when Holecek gets to it, is precise. The Tibetan death signs ("you cast no shadow... you walk on sand or snow and leave no footprints... you cannot see the sun or moon") are not given as metaphysics.

**They are given as a daytime practice that, by training the habit of checking for one's shadow, builds the cognitive infrastructure that will eventually become available in the bardo:**

> Working with dreamsigns also helps us prepare for death. *Tibetan Buddhists believe that one of the biggest problems we have after death is not realizing we're dead, and dream yoga developed as a preparation for death*... Create the habit of checking for these signs now, and that habit will carry into dream and death.

The pedagogical instinct is sound. The contemporary student approaching the practice for the first time is much more likely to be moved by the practical question — *am I dreaming right now? am I dead right now?* — than by an abstract presentation of the bardo doctrine.

**Holecek treats the practice as the door, with the doctrine as what is on the other side of the door.**

This is, in pedagogical terms, the inversion of the older monastic model, in which the doctrine was given first and the practice followed. The contemporary Western context does not have the monastic structure that made the older sequence stable; Holecek is implicitly designing for what is actually available.

What Holecek shares with Wallace, structurally, is the conviction that the lucid-dream methodology is genuinely valuable but not by itself the practice.

> *Lucid dreaming has much to offer for practitioners of dream yoga, and dream yoga has a great deal to contribute to lucid dreaming. Together they make fantastic sleeping partners.*

The sleeping-partners image is light; the underlying point is the same as Wallace's.

**Two practices, complementary, with the integration as the working assumption rather than the aspiration.**

## Wangyal: the lineage holder writing for Westerners

Tenzin Wangyal writes from a different position again. He is a Bön lineage holder, ordained at the main Bön monastery in Dolanji and trained in the *Zhang Zhung Nyan Gyud* and the *Mother Tantra*. He founded Ligmincha International in 1992 to bring those teachings to Western students.

*Tibetan Yogas of Dream and Sleep* (Snow Lion, 1998) is the most-read English-language treatment of the practice. Module 2 covered the structure of his dream and sleep yoga teaching in detail.

For the convergence specifically, Wangyal does not write as a bridge author. He is a teacher of the practice, transmitting a Bön lineage to Western students who, in many cases, do not have prior contact with the Tibetan or Bön traditions.

Where the convergence enters his work is at two specific points.

### First: on the question of dream control

**Wangyal addresses, with some directness, a pedagogical disagreement that has emerged between the Western lucid-dream tradition and the Tibetan teachings:**

> Although some Western psychologies believe that the dreamer should not control the dream, *according to Tibetan teachings this is a wrong view*. It is better for the lucid and aware dreamer to control the dream than for the dreamer to be dreamed. The same is true with thoughts: it is better for the thinker to control the thoughts than for the thoughts to control the thinker. (Wangyal, *Tibetan Yogas of Dream and Sleep*)

The "Western psychologies" Wangyal has in mind are the post-Jungian schools that treat the dream content as an autonomous communication from the unconscious, to be received rather than directed. This is the Hillman position from Module 4.

Wangyal's reply is doctrinal: in the Tibetan view, the dream content arises from karmic traces, and the practitioner's lucid engagement with it — including the active transformation of dream environments and dream figures — is part of the practice's transformation of the traces.

The position is the same as the position Wallace draws from Padmasambhava on the practices of emanation and transformation.

**Dream control, in the dream-yoga frame, is not the imposition of waking ego on dream content; it is the demonstration to the practitioner that the apparent solidity of the dream-world is plastic, and that the same plasticity will eventually be recognizable in waking experience too.**

### Wangyal versus Hillman

The disagreement between Wangyal's position and Hillman's is real.

- Hillman would say: leave the dream alone, do not pull it into the dayworld's purposes.
- Wangyal would say: the practitioner who is doing dream yoga is not pulling the dream into the dayworld's purposes; the practitioner is using the lucid dream to demonstrate the plasticity of phenomena, which is a piece of contemplative work that happens *to* the practitioner as much as the practitioner does it.

Whether the two positions can be reconciled in practice is a question each contemporary practitioner has to work out. Hillman's caution is real and worth holding. Wangyal's doctrinal grounding is also real and worth holding.

### Second: on visualization and Western medicine

**On the relation between visualization-based contemplative practice and Western medicine.** Wangyal makes, in passing, a remark that registers the convergence in a different domain:

> "*Visualization leads to changes in our bodies. Western research is demonstrating the truth of this statement, and Western medicine is now using the power of visualization even for serious illnesses such as cancer.*"

The remark is careful and modest. Wangyal does not claim the science is converging on the Bön healing tradition; he notes that Western research has empirically established something the contemplative tradition has known practically for a long time.

The framing — "*It is good also to avail ourselves of Western medicine when possible. Rather than limit ourselves to a particular system, it is better to use whatever is beneficial*" — is the framing of a teacher who has decided that the integration is the working assumption and that pluralism within the practice is appropriate, not threatening.

### What Wangyal brings to the convergence

What Wangyal brings to the convergence as it is taking shape in the source bundle is therefore, quietly, the lineage holder's perspective.

The integration, on Wangyal's framing, is real. The differences in emphasis between the Tibetan and Western frames are real. What is *not* at stake is the practice's lineage origin. A Bön student does not become a Western lucid dreamer by deploying state-checks; a Western lucid dreamer does not become a Bön student by visualizing the white *A* at the central channel.

**The practices are absorbable in both directions.**

What is not negotiable, on Wangyal's framing, is the practice's framing within bodhicitta orientation, ethical conduct, and the larger view of liberation. The framing the practice asks for is not optional. It is the practice's actual character.

This is the same edge Wallace's book holds. Holecek holds it more lightly. Wangyal holds it as a lineage holder. Wallace holds it as a polemicist.

**Three contemporary teachers, three pedagogical registers, one underlying conviction: the convergence is real, but the practice has a character the convergence cannot fully translate.**

## What the three voices share

Working through Wallace, Holecek, and Wangyal — the three contemporary teachers most directly engaged with the convergence in the project's source bundle — yields different registers but compatible commitments.

All three treat the lucid-dream methodology as genuinely valuable but not, by itself, the practice. All three treat the integration of contemplative and scientific traditions as the working assumption rather than the aspiration. All three insist, in different vocabularies, that the practice has a frame — bodhicitta, the bardo, the recognition of mind's nature — that is not optional and that the science cannot supply.

What differs is what each teacher prioritizes in the meeting between traditions.

**Wallace prioritizes the philosophical and methodological clarity of the integration.** He is the most argumentative, the most willing to identify and name what he calls "Buddhist revisionism," the most insistent on the empirical character of the contemplative tradition.

**Holecek prioritizes accessibility.** He is the most pedagogically generous, the most willing to teach the Western methodology in full and use it as the contemporary on-ramp to the contemplative practice.

**Wangyal prioritizes the lineage.** He is the most insistent that the practice has a character that survives the change of cultural context, and the most explicit that the framing is not optional.

A contemporary practitioner who reads all three, in roughly that order, will get most of what the contemporary integration has to offer.
