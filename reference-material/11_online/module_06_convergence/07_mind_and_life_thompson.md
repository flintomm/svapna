# Module 6, Lesson 7 — The Mind and Life Institute, and Evan Thompson

*A short lesson, by design. The institutional dialogue and the contemporary philosophical work that handles the same territory the practice is operating in — both flagged as essential reading, neither in the project source bundle.*

---

## The Mind and Life Institute

The **Mind and Life Institute** is, as the curriculum outline has noted, the principal institutional venue through which the contemplative-scientific dialogue on consciousness has been conducted since 1987.

It has produced, over thirty-five years, a series of edited volumes from multi-day private dialogues between the Dalai Lama and small groups of senior scientists and contemplatives.

The volume most directly relevant to the dream-yoga subject — Francisco Varela's edited *Sleeping, Dreaming, and Dying: An Exploration of Consciousness with the Dalai Lama* (Wisdom, 1997, second edition 2002) — is in the bibliography of *Dreaming Yourself Awake* but is not in the current source bundle. This module therefore handles Mind and Life only via Wallace.

### Wallace's relation to the Institute

Wallace's relation to the Institute is, by his own restraint about it, oblique. He does not name the Institute in the text of *Dreaming Yourself Awake*.

He attended the 1992 Mind and Life conference on sleeping, dreaming, and dying as a translator and intellectual interlocutor, and he draws on the broader contemplative-scientific engagement that the Institute helped to create as a kind of intellectual atmosphere throughout the book.

The Dalai Lama appears in *Dreaming Yourself Awake* as an authority on bodhicitta and on advanced Vajrayana dream-yoga practices. Varela appears in the single passage on imagination, perception, and dream that Lesson 5 quoted.

The book treats the dialogue between contemplative and scientific traditions as a settled feature of the intellectual landscape rather than as something to be argued for.

**This is, in itself, evidence of how far the Institute's work has reached. The dialogue is no longer the achievement; it is the floor.**

### What Mind and Life has accomplished

What Mind and Life has accomplished, on Wallace's implicit framing, is the establishment of the methodological premise this whole module has been working with: **that contemplative claims and scientific claims sit in the same epistemic register, that both are first-person disciplines made publicly accountable through method, and that serious contemplatives and serious scientists can engage in technically demanding discussion without either side dismissing the other.**

This premise was not available before the 1980s in any institutionally stable form. It is now. Wallace's book is one of the things that became possible because of it.

### Varela's volume as the primary text

The Mind and Life material in primary form is, on the strength of Wallace's bibliographic recommendation, **Varela's edited *Sleeping, Dreaming, and Dying*.**

That volume — a transcript of the 1992 dialogue with Wallace himself among the principal interlocutors — remains the foundational text for the contemplative-scientific dialogue specifically on the subject of dream and sleep, even thirty-plus years after its publication.

The participants included His Holiness the Dalai Lama, Francisco Varela himself, Jerome Engel, Joyce McDougall, Charles Taylor, and B. Alan Wallace. The Tibetan side of the dialogue was supplemented by Geshe Lhundub Sopa and others from the Geluk lineage's senior teachers.

The volume covers, across its multi-day discussion, the architecture of sleep and dream, the question of unconscious mental states, the bardo and the death-process, the relations between dreaming and post-mortem consciousness, the contemplative training of attention, and the scientific verification of meditation states.

**It is the closest thing the contemporary integration has to a foundational document.**

The current source bundle does not contain it. The institutional-dialogue side of the convergence, in primary form, lives in Varela's volume.

## Evan Thompson

Even briefer.

Wallace's *Dreaming Yourself Awake* does not cite Evan Thompson, and Thompson's *Waking, Dreaming, Being: Self and Consciousness in Neuroscience, Meditation, and Philosophy* (Columbia, 2015) is not in the source bundle.

**The honest statement, then: Evan Thompson is the contemporary philosopher of mind whose work is most directly relevant to this module's subject.**

*Waking, Dreaming, Being* is the natural philosophical complement to Wallace's contemplative-practical treatment, working the four states of consciousness through:

- **The Indian-Tibetan philosophical material** (the Māṇḍūkya tradition through Gauḍapāda; the Yogācāra; the Tibetan tradition's treatments of the four states).
- **The Western phenomenological tradition** (Husserl, Merleau-Ponty, the broader continental philosophy of mind).
- **The contemporary cognitive science** (the lucid-dream literature, the contemporary neuroscience of REM sleep, the work on the default mode network, the philosophical implications of the laboratory findings).

Thompson is the son of William Irwin Thompson, a long-time student of contemplative traditions, and a philosopher of cognitive science with substantial contemporary credibility. He is also one of the contributors to the Varela-edited *Sleeping, Dreaming, and Dying* discussions and a long-time interlocutor with the Mind and Life community.

After Wallace, the rigorous philosophical treatment of the same territory lives in Thompson. The current source bundle does not support a longer treatment of Thompson here, and rather than reconstruct him from secondary memory, this module registers the gap.

## Why this lesson is short

The honest answer is that the project's source bundle does not contain the primary texts that would support a fuller treatment.

The work's commitment, established in the welcome lesson, is to write from primary sources rather than from comparative memory. **When the bundle is thin, the lessons mark the gap honestly rather than producing summaries that look more authoritative than they are.**

What this lesson accomplishes is therefore limited but real.

It establishes that there is a contemporary institutional dialogue that has been conducting the contemplative-scientific engagement at a level higher than any individual book can sustain.

It establishes that there is a contemporary philosophical treatment of the same territory the practice is operating in, written with the rigor the academic philosophy of mind expects.

It marks where to go next.

## The working library, after this module

Working through this material with care leaves the following texts in hand:

- **The primary contemplative materials of the Indo-Tibetan tradition**, accessible through the reading list compiled by module.

- **The primary scientific materials**, accessible through the LaBerge-Rheingold book and the 2018 PLOS ONE paper.

- **The contemporary teacher voices** (Wallace, Holecek, Wangyal) accessible through the books cited in Module 6.

- **And, beyond what this work has covered**: Varela's *Sleeping, Dreaming, and Dying* for the institutional dialogue, and Thompson's *Waking, Dreaming, Being* for the rigorous contemporary philosophical treatment.

**Together, these constitute the working library a serious contemporary practitioner of dream practice has available in 2026. Each is doing different work. None is a substitute for the others.**
