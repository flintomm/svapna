# Module 6, Lesson 4 — Illusory Body, Sleep Yoga, and the Bardo

*Wallace's three further moves on what the Tibetan tradition contributes — taken together, because they build a single integrated picture of what the practice is finally for.*

---

## The illusory-body teaching and the recognition of mind's nature

The second move follows from the first. **If the aim is the recognition of pristine awareness, then the *content* of dream practice — what the practitioner is actually supposed to do in the lucid dream — has to be different from what the Western lucid-dream literature typically prescribes.**

The Western program, after lucidity has been achieved, generally moves toward dream control and dream applications. Spinning to keep the dream stable; rubbing the dream body to anchor it; focusing on a dream object to vivify it; using anticipation and expectation to direct dream content; deploying the lucid dream as a laboratory for psychological work, creative problem-solving, motor-skill rehearsal, or recreational adventure.

Wallace teaches all of this in Part One of his book. He is not against any of it.

### Padmasambhava's daytime instruction

The dream-yoga program, after lucidity has been achieved, moves in a different direction. Wallace's chapter on the daytime practices of dream yoga draws on Padmasambhava's instruction:

> Padmasambhava gives us the essential, specific instruction for daytime dream yoga practice: "*At this time, powerfully imagine that your environment, city, house, companions, conversation, and all activities are a dream; and even say out loud, 'This is a dream.' Continually imagine that this is just a dream*." Another Tibetan Buddhist teaching on daytime dream yoga elaborates on these instructions, encouraging the student to "*establish single-pointed meditative equipoise in the awareness, 'I have fallen asleep. This appearance is a dream. It is an illusion.'*"... This practice points directly to the key difference between the daytime practices of lucid dreaming and those of dream yoga. If in daytime you complete a dozen state checks, and ten times in a row your digital clock gives you the same answer when you glance away and look back — in terms of lucid dreaming you can be 99 percent sure that you are not dreaming. *And yet Padmasambhava suggests that even so, even though you are sure you are "awake," you should tell yourself you are dreaming.* Padmasambhava is not inviting us to practice a self-induced illusion. *He is asking us to pull the rug out from under our habitual perspective on what we are experiencing.* He is reframing what it means to be awake and making this the focus of daytime practice. (Wallace, ch. 6)

The state check, in the Western program, asks whether one is asleep or awake. The dream-yoga daytime practice answers the state check before it is asked: ***this is a dream***. The waking state is also a dream — slower, denser, more karmically stabilized, but a dream nonetheless.

The state-check tool the Western literature uses to enter lucidity in the night becomes, in the dream-yoga reframing, an instrument for entering lucidity in the day.

**As Wallace puts it directly:** "*Padmasambhava is asking you to view the dream not from the perspective of the deluded dream persona but, figuratively speaking, from that of the dreamer asleep in bed. He is suggesting that you view waking reality from the perspective of buddha-nature.*"

### Emanation and transformation in the dream

In the lucid dream itself, the dream-yoga instruction is correspondingly different. Wallace describes the core practices of "emanation and transformation" — the practitioner systematically transforms dream objects (one into many, many into one; small into big, big into small; one form into another), not to demonstrate dream control but to demonstrate, experientially, that the apparent solidity of the dream-world is a function of the dreamer's own consciousness:

> At first you will encounter certain emanations and transformations that are resistant to your will, difficult to perform. *In these instances you must exert yourself because there is something in the lucid dream that you are still reifying*; there appears to be some objective resistance, something is not malleable to the play of your own awareness. So you must work on it until you get it completely free. *You must realize not only conceptually but also experientially that dream phenomena are entirely fluid*... The tendency to reify stops not because you have suppressed it but because you know these phenomena are unreal through your own experience — that is the overarching reason you are practicing dream yoga. Although it can be a lot of fun to play with these things, *the point of these exercises is to thoroughly saturate your understanding with the knowledge that dream phenomena have no inherent existence from their own side*. (Wallace, ch. 7)

The same techniques the Western literature describes — flying, transforming dream objects, walking through walls — are, in the dream-yoga frame, doing entirely different work.

The Western practitioner deploys them for the experience. The dream-yoga practitioner deploys them as evidence — evidence the practitioner produces for himself, in a state where the evidence is unanswerable, that the apparent objectivity of phenomena is a function of consciousness rather than the other way around.

**The dream is the laboratory in which the doctrine of emptiness becomes operationalizable. *This is what the dream yogi means by lucid dreaming.***

## Sleep yoga and the clear light

The third move. The Western lucid-dream literature has, over the past two decades, begun to take seriously the question of awareness in dreamless sleep. The literature on this is small.

Wallace nonetheless treats sleep yoga as integral to the practice, not as an optional advanced extension. **It is the third leg of the architecture, and it is the leg the Western program has the least to offer on.**

### The substrate

Wallace's structural argument: dreamless sleep is normally entered "naturally and unconsciously and in which we remain with a very low level of awareness, if any at all."

But this state — what Wallace, drawing on Yogācāra-derived Tibetan terminology, calls the *substrate* (Sanskrit *ālaya*) and the awareness that knows it the *substrate consciousness* (*ālayavijñāna*) — is "*a state we can explore and benefit from*, if we take a keen interest in understanding the nature of consciousness and mental states. Lucid dreamless sleep brings us a relatively pure experience of the substrate" (Wallace, ch. 4).

Wallace gives two methods of entry. The first: closing one's eyes during a lucid dream "brings one immediately to the vacuity of the substrate." The second, drawn directly from the dream-yoga literature, is "to concentrate on a visualized image at the heart chakra." A third method, the practice of awareness of awareness (*shamatha without a sign*), is given as a way to "gently sink into dreamless sleep lucidly at bedtime."

### What is done once stabilized

What the practitioner does once awareness is stabilized in lucid dreamless sleep is the question on which the older Tibetan literature spends most of its attention.

**Wallace's pass at the answer:**

> One exercise you can perform in the state of lucid dreamless sleep is to address your attention to the vacuity (the substrate) and pose questions such as, "What is the nature of this vacuity? What is its size, shape? What are its characteristics?" Having observed the substrate, resting there, answers to these questions can bring you to a bigger one: "*What is the nature of being conscious?*" In this rarified atmosphere, devoid of distractions, you can examine consciousness in detail and perhaps find important answers to questions that have puzzled philosophers for millennia.
>
> Another advanced practice based on lucid dreamless sleep is open presence, which falls under the heading of Dzogchen... In brief, *if one is able to attain stability in lucid dreamless sleep, accessing the substrate, practicing open presence may enable one to break through to pristine awareness — one's buddha-nature*. For those on the spiritual path, this is a major accomplishment. (Wallace, ch. 4)

The "breakthrough" Wallace refers to is *trekchö* — the Dzogchen practice of cutting through the substrate consciousness to the pristine awareness it normally occludes.

The architecture is exactly the architecture Tenzin Wangyal sets out in *Tibetan Yogas of Dream and Sleep*. **The substrate is the screen; pristine awareness is what looks at the screen; sleep yoga is the practice of recognizing, in the deepest sleep state available to the practitioner, that what looks at the screen has been there all along.**

The science of dreamless awareness is at most thirty years old in any rigorous form. The contemplative tradition is a thousand years old on this point.

A practitioner who works only with lucid dreams is doing something real, but doing it in the shallowest of the night's available states. A practitioner who extends the work to sleep yoga has access — at least in principle — to a deeper one.

## The bardo and the death-process framing

The fourth move. The Tibetan tradition's foundational claim about why dream yoga matters in the way it matters is the bardo claim, which Module 2 covered in detail.

**Wallace's version, drawing the same parallel, is brief but explicit:**

> In more advanced practices, *the development of stability in lucid dreamless sleep can be a crucial factor in one's navigating the after-death bardo*. In Tibetan Buddhism it is believed that this after-death state is a factor determining the nature of one's rebirth. Normally the consciousness of the deceased is confused and frightened by the unfamiliar and dramatic appearances of this bardo — unable to either comprehend it or use it to effect a more positive rebirth. However, if one has attained stability in dreamless sleep, there is a better chance that one can remain calm in this situation and make meaningful choices — especially if one has prepared oneself by studying texts explaining the nature of this transitional state. (Wallace, ch. 4)

And, more bluntly: "*Dream yoga can prepare one for the after-death bardo*... If one has a thorough understanding of the dream state and can control one's dreams, this can be done in the after-death bardo as well."

This is the framing the Western lucid-dream tradition has the least equipment to engage. The bardo is, by hypothesis, an experience that begins after the brain processes available to scientific measurement have ceased.

There is, in the standard scientific apparatus, no way to evaluate the claim that dream practice prepares for the bardo.

### Wallace's polemical edge

But Wallace argues — and the argument is one of the most distinctive and most contested moves in the book — that *removing the bardo framing while keeping the practice* attenuates the practice in ways that take time to become visible.

**He is direct:**

> Such a high motivation brings with it some far-reaching questions. *Can you really dedicate yourself to achieving bodhichitta — with all that it implies — while maintaining the working assumption that when you die all bets are off?* I don't know how that can possibly be realistic unless you are almost there — almost awakened when you begin this practice — or unless you downgrade bodhichitta to the mere catch phrase, "may everybody be happy." It would seem that *the working hypothesis of reincarnation — that we can propel ourselves to continue this practice, this work, in future lifetimes if need be — would be very helpful if not essential for us to seriously make the assertion that Padmasambhava asks us to make*. (Wallace, ch. 7)

Wallace's argument is that the motivation that sustains the practice over decades is not, in the traditional view, the prospect of better lucid-dream frequency or improved sleep.

**It is the orientation toward the moment of death and what follows.**

This is the polemical edge of Wallace's bridge work. He is not arguing that the contemporary practitioner must commit to the bardo doctrine before beginning the practice. He notes elsewhere that "*this does not mean that one must accept Buddhist theories about consciousness before one can venture into Buddhist practice*" (ch. 10).

What he is arguing is that holding the bardo framework at a *purely metaphorical* register — using "samsara" as a way of talking about the everyday, "buddhahood" as a way of talking about psychological flourishing — drains the practice of its motivational substrate over time.

The reductive interpretation of Buddhism that Wallace identifies as common in some Western teaching — what he calls "Buddhist revisionism" — treats the bardo and rebirth as folk-cosmology to be set aside while the genuine psychological insights are retained.

**Wallace's view is that the setting-aside is not as separable from the psychological content as the revisionists assume.**

## Summary of the four moves

What the Tibetan tradition adds to the lucid-dreaming program, on Wallace's account, is therefore:

- **An aim beyond lucidity itself.**
- **A teaching on how lucidity intersects with the deeper aim**, including the illusory-body practices of emanation and transformation that turn the lucid dream into a laboratory for the recognition of emptiness.
- **The practices of sleep yoga and clear-light recognition** that extend the work into deep dreamless sleep, where the Western literature is thin.
- **The bardo / death-process framing** that gives the practice its existential and ethical weight.

None of these is, by itself, a methodology the Western lucid-dream literature lacks the technical apparatus to absorb.

**What the Western literature lacks is the *integration* — the holding-together of these elements within a single contemplative discipline whose stated aim is the awakening Wallace's title points at.**
