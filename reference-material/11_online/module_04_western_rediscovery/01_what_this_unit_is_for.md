# Module 4, Lesson 1 — What This Module Is For

*A century of European dream science worth taking seriously on its own terms — and the depth-psychological treatment that grew up alongside it.*

---

## A serious empirical phase, before Freud

The Asian contemplative dream traditions handed down a tradition of practice in which lucidity within the dream was a station along a longer path rather than the goal. The European nineteenth century knew almost none of that tradition.

What it had was a small, scattered body of empirical work — a handful of careful self-observers who kept dream journals, developed methods for waking the attention inside the dream, and published their findings in journals of psychology or in the proceedings of the Society for Psychical Research.

**The body of work was substantial enough that, taken in its entirety, it constitutes a serious empirical phase of dream studies preceding Freud's *The Interpretation of Dreams* (1900).**

It is the phase that the rest of the twentieth century has had to keep rediscovering. Partly because Freud's psychoanalytic framework so dominated the early twentieth century that everything before it was reabsorbed into pre-history.

And partly because the depth psychology that came after Freud — in particular Jung's — has had its own complicated relationship to the empirical-introspective tradition: respectful of its methods, but committed to a register of meaning the empirical work mostly set aside.

This module takes the empirical-introspective tradition first — Saint-Denys, the Carroy reframing of his place, van Eeden, Arnold-Forster — and then turns to Jung's mature theoretical statement and to the archetypal critique that James Hillman would later mount against the use of dreams as instruments of the dayworld self.

Each section quotes from the source texts directly. Where a passage has been compressed, the compression is marked. Where the source is in French, French is given alongside a conservative English rendering.

## The arc of the module

**The Marquis d'Hervey de Saint-Denys** (1822–1892) — *Les Rêves et les moyens de les diriger*, 1867 — is the founding text of the modern empirical study of dreaming.

A young French sinologist keeps a dream journal from his early teens, develops by patient self-observation a faculty he calls "having awareness, while asleep, of my true situation," and publishes the results anonymously after twenty years of work. The book is the foundation stone of the empirical-introspective tradition.

**Jacqueline Carroy**'s *Nuits savantes* (2012) is the contemporary historiographic correction that places Saint-Denys back at the center of the story.

Her argument is that there was a serious empirical science of dream in the nineteenth century, conducted before and alongside Freud, which the dominance of psychoanalysis in the twentieth century had obscured.

**Frederik van Eeden** (1860–1932) — Dutch psychiatrist, novelist, and reformer — presented "A Study of Dreams" to the Society for Psychical Research in 1913.

The paper is the document in which the term *lucid dream*, in its present meaning, enters the English-language scientific vocabulary. Van Eeden had collected 352 instances of his seventh type of dream over fifteen years.

**Mary Arnold-Forster** (1857–1951) — *Studies in Dreams*, 1921 — produced something the tradition had not yet had: a practical manual.

Her central contribution was a method — a *formula* — that the practitioner repeats during the day and at sleep onset, until it becomes the cue by which the dream mind orients itself within a recurring distressing dream. Sixty years before Stephen LaBerge would codify the method as MILD, Arnold-Forster had already worked out its structure.

**C. G. Jung**'s mature theory of the dream — given in "On the Nature of Dreams" (1945/1948) — treats the dream as compensatory: an autonomous statement from the unconscious that brings the relevant counterweight to whatever the conscious attitude has pushed too far.

Jung's reading is austere in its method (the practitioner takes up the context, makes no assumptions, attends to the dream's actual texture) and ambitious in its scope (the deepest dreams draw on a collective layer of the unconscious whose vocabulary is shared with the world's mythologies).

**James Hillman**'s *The Dream and the Underworld* (1979) is the in-house archetypal critique of the use to which depth psychology had put the dream by the late twentieth century.

Hillman holds that the entire procedure of dream interpretation aiming at more consciousness about living is "radically wrong" — that the dream belongs to the underworld and its gods, and that the practitioner should go down to meet the dream rather than calling the dream up to be interpreted on terms the dayworld already accepts.

## Four things this module hands forward

What this module hands forward to Module 5 — the modern scientific era — is fourfold.

**A vocabulary.** *Conscience en dormant* (Saint-Denys) becomes *lucid dream* (van Eeden) becomes the canonical term of the laboratory and contemporary contemplative literatures.

**A method.** Arnold-Forster's repeated formula, given in 1921 as a technique for the elimination of bad dreams, is the same formula that LaBerge would later codify as MILD.

**An empirical record.** Saint-Denys had decades of journal entries; van Eeden had 352 instances by 1912; Arnold-Forster had a journal extending across decades. The records were kept carefully enough that, by the 1970s, when Hearne and LaBerge began designing the eye-signal experiments, the prior empirical literature was already substantial.

**A depth-psychological frame.** Jung's compensatory theory and the Hillman correction together set the conceptual frame within which the contemporary practitioner works.

## A caution against an easy formula

A caution worth marking before the module begins.

**The easy formula — *the West rediscovered what Asia knew* — is wrong in two ways.**

First, the chronology is wrong: the European empirical-introspective tradition was up and running in 1867, three decades before any substantial Tibetan dream-yoga teaching reached the West (the Evans-Wentz publication of 1935 is the first to do so for any sizable readership).

Second, the framing is wrong: the European tradition was working out a phenomenology and a method for cultivating awareness within the dream; the Tibetan tradition was working out a soteriology in which the dream-state was one site within a much larger contemplative discipline whose aim was the recognition of the illusory nature of all experience.

The two arcs converge on a method (cultivating awareness in dream) but diverge on the aim (psychological integration vs. recognition of mind's nature). The convergence on method is genuine and worth marking. The divergence on aim is what Module 6 will eventually take up.
