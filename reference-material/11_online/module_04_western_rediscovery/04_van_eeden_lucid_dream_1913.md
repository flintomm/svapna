# Module 4, Lesson 4 — Frederik van Eeden and the Term "Lucid Dream" (1913)

*The 1913 paper to the Society for Psychical Research, and the document in which the term* lucid dream *enters the English-language scientific vocabulary in its present meaning.*

---

## A Dutch psychiatrist with a journal

**Frederik van Eeden** (1860–1932) was a Dutch psychiatrist, novelist, and reformer who, in 1913, presented a paper to the Society for Psychical Research titled simply "A Study of Dreams."

The paper is short enough to read in a sitting. It is the document in which the term *lucid dream*, with its present meaning, enters the English-language scientific vocabulary.

Van Eeden's project is empirical in the same Saint-Denys mode. He reports that since 1896 he had kept a journal of his own dreams; that since 1898 he had kept a separate record of one particular kind of dream, of which by 1913 he had collected 352 instances; and that the body of work he had to offer was "only a preliminary sketch, a short announcement of a greater work" he hoped to complete in later years (van Eeden 1913, opening).

What he then offers is a typology — a classification of dreams into nine kinds — and the bulk of the paper is given over to careful description of each.

## The canonical definition

The seventh kind is the kind he had been collecting for fifteen years.

**The seventh-type passage is the canonical definition of lucid dreaming in the modern Western literature:**

> The seventh type of dreams, which I call lucid dreams, *seems to me the most interesting and worthy of the most careful observation and study*. Of this type I experienced and wrote down 352 cases in the period between January 20, 1898, and December 26, 1912.
>
> In these lucid dreams *the reintegration of the psychic functions is so complete that the sleeper remembers day-life and his own condition, reaches a state of perfect awareness, and is able to direct his attention, and to attempt different acts of free volition*. Yet the sleep, as I am able confidently to state, is undisturbed, deep and refreshing. (van Eeden 1913)

Two features of it are worth marking.

**First**, van Eeden's emphasis on *reintegration*. The lucid dream is, in his account, a state in which the psychic functions, dissociated by ordinary sleep, recombine in a different mode rather than a state of partial sleep.

He resists Havelock Ellis's reading (that the lucid state is a sort of hypnagogic trance rather than a "real" dream) by pointing out that what he is reporting was observed during deep healthy sleep, with full recollection of day-life. Whatever it is called, this state is what he had been studying.

**Second**, his explicit naming of the empirical concept. The term *lucid dream* is offered as a stipulation for a phenomenon that can be verified against the data.

Van Eeden makes no metaphysical claim about a "dream-body" except in the experiential sense — the sense that, within the lucid dream, "*the sensation of having a body — having eyes, hands, a mouth that speaks, and so on — is perfectly distinct; yet I know at the same time that the physical body is sleeping and has quite a different position*."

The observation that the laboratory eye-signal experiments of the 1970s would, in their own register, eventually confirm.

## The first glimpse, June 1897

He dates his first glimpse of the state to June 1897, in a passage worth quoting because it exemplifies the empirical reserve of the tradition:

> *I obtained my first glimpse of this lucidity during sleep in June, 1897*, in the following way. I dreamt that I was floating through a landscape with bare trees, knowing that it was April, and I remarked that the perspective of the branches and twigs changed quite naturally. Then I made the reflection, during sleep, that *my fancy would never be able to invent or to make an image as intricate as the perspective movement of little twigs seen in floating by*. (van Eeden 1913)

**The recognition is the practice.** He notices, within the dream, the texture of the perceptual data, and the noticing of it carries the inference that this is something his ordinary fancy could not have constructed — and from that inference, awareness of the state.

He compares his own approach with a similar observation made independently by Ernst Mach, with the difference that "*Mach came to the conclusion that he was dreaming, but it was because he saw the movement of the twigs to be defective, while I had wondered at the naturalness which my fancy could never invent*."

## Repeating the observation

In the months following, van Eeden made a deliberate effort to repeat the observation. The second instance, dated to January 1898, has him "lying in the garden before the windows of my study, and saw the eyes of my dog through the glass pane. I was lying on my chest and observing the dog very keenly. At the same time, however, *I knew with perfect certainty that I was dreaming and lying on my back in my bed*."

He resolves to wake up slowly and to observe the transition from the dream-body to the waking body, and reports that "the transition — which I have since undergone many times — is most wonderful. *It is like the feeling of slipping from one body into another, and there is distinctly a double recollection of the two bodies.*"

**It is the same observation Saint-Denys had been making, expressed in a slightly different vocabulary, and arrived at independently.**

Two careful self-observers, working a generation apart in different countries, with different vocabularies and (initially at least) without contact with each other's work, find the same territory.

## The nine-type classification

Van Eeden's nine-type classification is detailed and not all of it bears directly on this module. The types are:

1. Initial dreams, the rare type that occurs at the boundary of waking and sleep
2. Pathological dreams, those caused by fever, indigestion, or other bodily disturbance
3. Ordinary dreaming, the absurd-and-confused type that for many people is the only type they know
4. Vivid dreaming, intense and well-remembered
5. Symbolic or "mocking" dreams
6. General dream-sensations, dreams without imagery in which the sleeping mind is occupied across the night by a single person or idea
7. Lucid dreams
8. Demon-dreams
9. The "wrong waking up" type, in which one dreams of waking and only slowly realizes one is still asleep — what later writers will call false awakening

The typology is useful here as evidence of the empirical mode in which van Eeden is working: the classifications are reports of what his journal contained rather than theoretical posits.

Most of his types appear under different names in the contemporary lucid-dream literature. The "wrong waking up" type is the false awakening of the present-day clinical literature; the lucid type is what LaBerge will test in 1980.

## What van Eeden hands forward

What van Eeden hands forward to the rest of this history is twofold: **a name for the central state**, and **a typology of contiguous states** that the practitioner can expect to encounter and need to distinguish.

The name is the more important contribution.

*Lucid dream*, by 1913, is the term the empirical-introspective tradition has settled on; by the 1970s, it is the term the laboratory work of Hearne and LaBerge will use; by the early twenty-first century, it is the term the integration with the contemplative traditions will adopt as the bridge term.

The Tibetan tradition has its own word — *milam* — for the practice it has been doing for a thousand years, but when the Tibetan teachers begin teaching in English in the late twentieth century, the word they use to translate it is the one van Eeden coined.
