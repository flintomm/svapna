# Module 4, Lesson 3 — The Carroy Reframing: There Was a Science Before Freud

*A short lesson, but a load-bearing one: the contemporary historiographic correction that places the empirical-introspective tradition back at the center of the nineteenth-century dream-science story.*

---

## A historiographic restoration

Jacqueline Carroy's *Nuits savantes: Une histoire des rêves (1800–1945)* (Paris: Éditions de l'EHESS, 2012) is the book that, in the contemporary French historiography of dream studies, has restored to the nineteenth century the empirical phase that Freud's hegemony had obscured.

A short notice in the source bundle reviews the volume's argument with care.

The review opens by noting that, although the use of *clefs des songes* (dream-keys, or dream-interpretation manuals) goes back to antiquity, "*c'est au XIXe siècle que des savants commencent à se pencher sur les rêves en tant qu'objets scientifiques à part entière*" — "it is in the nineteenth century that scholars begin to lean over dreams as scientific objects in their own right" (Carroy review).

This empirical phase, the review continues, was conducted largely by men who *se mettent en scène, s'exposent, se révèlent* — who put themselves on stage in their own dream-records, both protagonists and spectator-observers of their own nights.

**The journals were not private; they were "diffusés et publiés en tant que véritables documents scientifiques" (circulated and published as genuine scientific documents). This is the empirical-introspective tradition before Freud.**

## Saint-Denys, returned to the center

The review's account of Saint-Denys is worth quoting. Where Maury treats dreams as pathological, Saint-Denys

> Chez ce sinologue de renom, qui occupe à partir de 1874 la chaire de tartaro-mandarin au Collège de France, le réalisme des récits nocturnes fait place à une transposition fortement esthétisée. *Hervey apparaît ici comme un traducteur infidèle reconstruisant ses matériaux oniriques et prônant par le fait même un nouvel art de rêver* — art qui, de par son rejet des conceptions strictement organicistes, séduira notamment l'onirologue Sigmund Freud. (Carroy review)

In English: *In this renowned sinologist, who from 1874 held the chair of Tartar-Manchurian at the Collège de France, the realism of nocturnal accounts gives way to a strongly aestheticized transposition. Hervey appears here as an unfaithful translator reconstructing his dream materials and, by that fact, advocating a new art of dreaming — an art which, by its rejection of strictly organicist conceptions, will seduce the dream-theorist Sigmund Freud.*

**The review's claim is twofold:**

- **First**, that Saint-Denys's empirical work was already proposing a "new art of dreaming" — a *praxis*, not just an observational record.
- **Second**, that Freud was a more attentive reader of Saint-Denys than the standard historiography has acknowledged.

The point is sharpened later: Carroy holds, the reviewer reports, that "la formation française en psychopathologie [de Freud] a fait l'objet de nombreuses études, mais [l]es lectures françaises ou francophones sur les rêves ont été peu étudiées" — Freud's French training in psychopathology has been much studied, but his French-language reading on dreams has been little studied.

And: "*c'est en immergeant résolument [la Traumdeutung] dans la science des rêves de son temps que l'on peut mieux saisir au bout du compte son originalité*" — it is by resolutely immersing the *Interpretation of Dreams* in the dream-science of its time that one can finally better grasp its originality.

## The corrective

What follows from this reframing, for the purposes of the present module, is a corrective.

**The standard story — in which "the West rediscovered what Asia knew" by way of LaBerge in the 1980s, and in which Freud was the inaugurator of modern dream science — flattens the actual historical record on both ends.**

Saint-Denys, Maury, Tarde, and the broader mid-nineteenth-century European tradition were doing serious empirical dream science a generation before Freud. The lucid-dream practitioners who came after — van Eeden, Arnold-Forster, and (much later) the Tholey/Hearne/LaBerge tradition — were extending a project Freud had not started but had absorbed.

This is the correction worth holding through the rest of the module. The European nineteenth century had a substantial dream science whose center of gravity Freud's *Interpretation of Dreams* obscured but did not invent.

The lucid-dream tradition that runs through Saint-Denys, van Eeden, and Arnold-Forster — and that the laboratory work of the late twentieth century would eventually verify — is a continuous European empirical tradition that has been doing its own work for a hundred and sixty years.

## What this changes for the contemporary practitioner

The implication for a contemporary practitioner is a small but real one.

The lucid-dream methodology most likely to be encountered through LaBerge, through the contemporary popularizers, through the Western on-ramp to the integrated Wallace-Holecek-Wangyal teaching — is its own tradition rather than a derivation of the Asian ones.

**Its method (cultivation of awareness in dream by patient repetition of intention and observation) converges on what the Tibetan tradition does. Its aim (psychological integration, control of nightmare patterns, exploration of the dream-state) does not converge on what the Tibetan tradition is doing.**

The convergence on method and the divergence on aim are what Module 6 will eventually take up as the central question of the contemporary integration.

For now, what Carroy gives the work is the correction that restores Saint-Denys to his place: the European tradition was a generation ahead of where the popular history places it, and the practitioner who reads the empirical-introspective tradition in its own register will find a serious body of work to read.
