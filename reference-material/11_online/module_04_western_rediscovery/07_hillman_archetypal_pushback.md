# Module 4, Lesson 7 — Hillman and the Archetypal Pushback

*The in-house critique of the use to which depth psychology had put the dream by the late twentieth century. The dream belongs to the underworld; the dayworld's light is not where it ought to be pulled.*

---

## A pushback from inside the Jungian tradition

**James Hillman**'s *The Dream and the Underworld* (Harper & Row, 1979) is the in-house archetypal critique of depth psychology's treatment of dream.

Hillman writes from inside the Jungian tradition, but the book's purpose is to push back on the use to which that tradition had begun to put the dream by the late 1970s.

**The pushback is announced in the choice of title:**

> Among the possible titles for this work have been "The Dream Bridge," or "The Dream Between World and Underworld." Freud had already used a similar metaphor; he called the dream a royal road, the via regia to the unconscious. *But because this via regia, in most psychotherapy since his time, has become a straight one-way street of all morning traffic, moving out of the unconscious toward the ego's city, I have chosen to face the other way.* Hence my title, which is a directional signpost for a different one-way movement, let us say vesperal, into the dark. (Hillman, *The Dream and the Underworld*, "Bridge")

The complaint is that depth psychology, for all its talk of taking the dream seriously, has used the dream as a piece of evidence in service of the ego's own purposes — as a means of gaining "more consciousness about living."

## The argument in its strong form

**Hillman's pushback is that this is the wrong direction of travel:**

> This little book attempts a different view of the dream from those we are used to. Its thesis does not rely on ideas of repression (Freud) or of compensation (Jung), but imagines dreams in relation with soul and soul with death. *I have come to believe that the entire procedure of dream interpretation aiming at more consciousness about living is radically wrong.* And I mean "wrong" in all its fullness: harmful, twisted, deceptive, inadequate, mistaken, and exegetically insulting to its material, the dream. *When we wrong the dream, we wrong the soul, and if the soul has the intimate connection with death that tradition has always supposed, then mistaken dream interpretation deceives our dying.* (Hillman, "Bridge")

The argument is unusually emphatic.

Hillman is not saying dream interpretation is unhelpful or limited. He is saying the entire procedure of dream interpretation aiming at more consciousness about living is *radically wrong*.

**The dream is wronged when it is treated as a piece of evidence for the dayworld self's purposes; the soul is wronged when the dream is wronged; the practitioner's dying is wronged when the soul is wronged.** The argument runs vertically through the metaphor.

## The mythological grounding

The mythological grounding Hillman gives to the pushback is the imagery of the underworld. Dreams belong, in the older mythological vocabularies, to the underworld and its gods — Hades, Persephone, the realm of the shades — and an honest dream practice keeps the dream in its own register rather than pulling it up into the dayworld's light.

The practitioner descends to meet the dream, rather than calling the dream up to be interpreted on terms the dayworld already accepts:

> What is new in what follows is the attempt to re-vision the dream in the light of myth.... It is no secret that dreams belong to the underworld and its Gods, for our title announces this, and the short second chapter shows that a mythic base for dreams is an old story.... *We are saying a psychology of dreams that tries to keep a sense of the underworld always present in our work with them.* (Hillman, "Bridge")

## What Hillman is correcting against

For the purposes of the present module, what is worth recording is what Hillman is correcting against.

The empirical-introspective tradition (Saint-Denys, van Eeden, Arnold-Forster) had treated the dream as a phenomenon to be observed and, where possible, gently directed.

The depth-psychological tradition (Jung) had treated the dream as a compensatory function in service of the individuation process.

**Both traditions, in Hillman's reading, had assumed that the dream was *for* something — for self-knowledge, for therapeutic gain, for the integration of the personality.**

Hillman's correction is to say that the dream is not for anything in that sense. The dream belongs to its own mythic register and the practitioner's task is to learn to inhabit the register rather than to translate its contents into dayworld currency.

## The correction held in productive tension

The correction sits in productive tension with both prior traditions.

A patient empirical journal of the Saint-Denys kind can still be kept in good faith if the journaler understands that the dreams will not yield up an "interpretation" in the instrumental sense.

A Jungian compensatory reading can still be commenced if the analyst understands that the compensation is a conversation with a region of the psyche that has its own integrity and its own resistance to the dayworld's purposes.

**What Hillman supplies, looking forward to the rest of this history, is an intellectual brake against the bromide that any of this — empirical, depth-psychological, lucid-dream-experimental — is finally for the dayworld self's gain.**

The pushback is a useful one to keep in view as the next module (Modern Scientific Era) takes up LaBerge's laboratory work and the lucid-dream movement that became, in the 1990s, an applied practice.

The applied practice is at its best when it remembers that the dream still belongs, on Hillman's reading, to a register that is not the laboratory's.

## Hillman against the Tibetan tradition

It is also useful to read Hillman against the Tibetan tradition covered in Module 2.

Hillman's underworld dreams and the Tibetan tradition's *bardo* are working in different vocabularies — Hillman in a Greco-mythological one, the Tibetan tradition in a Buddhist-tantric one — but the structural complaint Hillman makes against ego-instrumental dream interpretation is one a Tibetan teacher would recognize.

The Tibetan tradition would not put it in Hillman's terms, but it would agree that lucid-dream methodology used for the dayworld self's purposes ("self-fulfillment" in Holecek's contrast with "self-transcendence") is a misuse of what the practice is for.

**The convergence is partial and the framings are different. But the suspicion of the dayworld self as the legitimate beneficiary of the dream is a point on which Hillman, the Mahāyāna tradition, and the Tibetan dream-yoga lineage broadly agree.**

## What the module hands forward

This module ends here. What it has handed forward, between Saint-Denys's 1867 notebook and Hillman's 1979 critique, is a Western tradition that did its own work without consulting the Asian tradition for most of its century.

The empirical-introspective half found, by patient self-observation, the phenomenon and the method. The depth-psychological half found a way of taking dream content seriously without reducing it to symptom. The archetypal half offered the in-house critique that prevented either from collapsing into instrumentalism.

The next module turns to the laboratory. The two-page paper, in 1953, that founded the modern science of dreaming. The eye-signal demonstration, in 1980, that put lucid dreaming on the polygraph trace. The induction methods that made the technique reliably trainable. And the pharmacological and neuroscientific extensions of the last two decades.
