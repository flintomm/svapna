# Module 4, Lesson 2 — Saint-Denys and the Dream Journal (1867)

*A young marquis takes notes on his dreams, and over twenty years develops by patient self-observation a faculty he calls "having awareness, while asleep, of my true situation." The foundation stone of the modern empirical study of dreaming.*

---

## A young marquis with a notebook

The book that begins the modern empirical study of dreaming is *Les Rêves et les moyens de les diriger : observations pratiques*, published anonymously in Paris in 1867 by **Hervey de Saint-Denys** (the Marquis d'Hervey de Saint-Denys, 1822–1892), a sinologist who held the chair of Tartar-Manchurian at the Collège de France from 1874.

The book reports the results of a private experiment. From his early teens, Saint-Denys kept a journal of his dreams. Over twenty years he developed by patient self-observation a faculty he called *avoir conscience en dormant de ma situation véritable* — "having awareness, while asleep, of my true situation."

The phrase is the foundation stone of the empirical-introspective tradition, and the conditions under which it was produced are worth taking seriously: not in a laboratory, not with instruments, but by a young man with a notebook beside his bed, willing to spend two decades on the discipline.

## The faculty as he describes it

**In the opening pages of the volume he describes both the discovery and the price of it:**

> Je voyais en même temps se développer chez moi, sous l'influence de l'habitude, une faculté à laquelle j'ai dû la plus grande partie des observations consignées plus loin, *celle d'avoir souvent conscience en dormant de ma situation véritable, de conserver alors, en songe, le sentiment de mes préoccupations de la veille, et de garder par suite assez d'empire sur mes idées pour en précipiter au besoin le cours* dans telle ou telle direction qu'il me convenait de leur imprimer. (Saint-Denys 1867, *Les Rêves*, p. 5–6)

In a conservative English rendering: *I saw at the same time developing in me, under the influence of habit, a faculty to which I owe the greater part of the observations set down below: that of often having awareness, while asleep, of my true situation, and of keeping then, within the dream, the feeling of my waking preoccupations, and thus retaining enough mastery over my ideas to direct, as needed, their course in whatever direction it suited me to give them.*

The terms here are the empirical tradition's central operating concepts.

*Conscience en dormant* — awareness in sleep — is an early phrasing of what van Eeden will later call lucidity. The persistence into the dream of waking memory and concern is the second piece. And what Saint-Denys calls *empire sur mes idées* — a measured kind of authority over the dream's course — is the third.

He is explicit that the faculty arose with practice and could in principle be cultivated by others, treating it as a phenomenon to be reported rather than as a metaphysical claim.

## The cost of the early phase

He is also honest about the cost. The early phase of the work brought on persistent headaches that forced him to suspend the experiments for a time:

> *Des maux de tête m'assaillirent, et je crus devoir interrompre mes élucubrations nocturnes* ; mais un repos d'esprit relatif m'ayant rendu la santé sans altérer cette faculté définitivement acquise de m'observer parfois en rêvant... (Saint-Denys 1867, p. 7)

(English: *Headaches assailed me, and I judged it necessary to interrupt my nocturnal lucubrations; but a relative rest of mind, having restored my health without altering the now definitively acquired faculty of observing myself within the dream...*)

The faculty, once consolidated, persisted for the next twenty years without further trouble, and the book is essentially the record of what he learned in that period: how the dream draws its raw material from waking impressions; how attention can be redirected within a dream; how recurring nightmares can be unlearned; how the apparent hard edge between waking thought and the dream's *marche spontanée des idées* (spontaneous procession of ideas) is in fact porous in both directions.

## The rhythm of the practice

A useful representative passage on the rhythm of the practice as he describes it elsewhere in the book:

> [...] *la conscience de ma situation me revient en rêve à peu près une nuit sur deux*. Si je m'y attache, pour analyser ou diriger les illusions du songe, je puis la retenir assez longtemps. (Saint-Denys 1867)

(English: *The awareness of my situation returns to me in dream about one night in two. If I attach myself to it, in order to analyze or direct the illusions of the dream, I can hold it for a fair while.*)

The frequency is unusual. The patience is the kind that produces the data.

**The willingness to attach the attention is the part that the Tibetan tradition (Module 2) and the LaBerge laboratory tradition (Module 5) would each independently come to recognize as the practical pivot.**

## Empirical reserve

Saint-Denys is at pains to refuse a metaphysical reading of his own work. The book's full title — *Les Rêves et les moyens de les diriger : observations pratiques* (*Dreams and the Means of Directing Them: Practical Observations*) — announces both its empirical reserve and its practical aim.

Where his contemporary Alfred Maury (1817–1892), who had published *Le Sommeil et les Rêves* in 1861, treated dreams as essentially pathological phenomena (a symptom-language of the body and brain), Saint-Denys treats them as observable phenomena of a healthy attention turned inward in a different mode.

The contrast — the medical-pathological versus the practical-introspective — is the contrast that the rest of this module's history sits within.

## A curious afterlife

The book had a curious afterlife. It was rare enough by the early twentieth century that, as the title page of the 1867 edition records, "*ouvrage devenu assez rare pour que ni Freud ni Havelock Ellis n'aient réussi à en prendre connaissance*" — "a work become rare enough that neither Freud nor Havelock Ellis succeeded in getting acquainted with it" (printed material on the source file's title page).

André Breton, in *Les Vases communicants*, would later note Saint-Denys as *le premier homme à penser qu'il n'était pas impossible... de vaincre à son profit les résistances* within the dream — the first to think that one might, within the dream, work with what otherwise simply happens to one.

The Surrealists rediscovered him in the 1920s, the Society for Psychical Research circles via van Eeden, and, much later, the contemporary lucid-dreaming movement; but for most of his own century after publication he was a marginal figure.

The Carroy reframing — which the next lesson takes up — is the historiographic correction that returns him to the center of the story.

## What carries forward

What is worth carrying forward from Saint-Denys is the simple fact that the practice he describes is recognizable.

*Conscience en dormant* is not what the Tibetan tradition is doing — Saint-Denys is not reading Sanskrit, not training shamatha, not holding the dream-state inside a soteriological frame — but the phenomenon he is describing is the same phenomenon.

**The recognition, within the dream, that one is dreaming. The persistence of waking memory across the threshold of sleep. The measured authority over the dream's course.**

These are the operating concepts of every dream-practice tradition the work will encounter.

A young Frenchman, working alone with a notebook, found the territory. The notebook is the empirical-introspective tradition's founding document.
