# Module 4, Lesson 6 — Jung on the Dream

*Jung's mature theoretical position: the dream as compensation, taking up the context, big and little dreams, and the two dreams Jung tells about himself.*

---

Jung's relation to the empirical-introspective tradition is more complicated than the relation Saint-Denys, van Eeden, and Arnold-Forster had to one another.

Jung is interested in the dream not as an empirical object but as a psychological statement about the dreamer's situation: a function of the unconscious, with specifiable structural relations to the conscious mind.

His mature theoretical position on dream is given in the essay "On the Nature of Dreams" (first published 1945; revised 1948; collected in *The Structure and Dynamics of the Psyche*). Read alongside the dream-narrative passages of *Memories, Dreams, Reflections* (1961), the essay gives a clean statement of how Jung's theoretical and practical positions had settled by the last phase of his life.

## The dream as compensation

Jung's central thesis is that the dream is "a fragment of involuntary psychic activity, just conscious enough to be reproducible in the waking state," and that its function in relation to the conscious mind is *compensatory*.

**He develops the notion against Freud's wish-fulfillment model, but with care to distinguish his concept of compensation from Freud's:**

> Since the meaning of most dreams is not in accord with the tendencies of the conscious mind but shows peculiar deviations, *we must assume that the unconscious, the matrix of dreams, has an independent function. This is what I call the autonomy of the unconscious.* The dream not only fails to obey our will but very often stands in flagrant opposition to our conscious intentions. The opposition need not always be so marked; sometimes the dream deviates only a little from the conscious attitude and introduces only slight modifications; occasionally it may even coincide with conscious contents and tendencies. When I attempted to express this behaviour in a formula, the concept of compensation seemed to me the only adequate one, for it alone is capable of summing up all the various ways in which a dream behaves. *Compensation, on the other hand, as the term implies, means balancing and comparing different data or points of view so as to produce an adjustment or a rectification.* (Jung, "On the Nature of Dreams")

Jung's compensation is not a balancing in the mechanical sense — the dream is not the symmetrical opposite of the waking attitude. **It is a balancing in an evaluative sense: the dream brings the relevant counterweight to whatever the conscious attitude has pushed too far.**

Jung lays out three cases.

- If the conscious attitude is "in large degree one-sided," the dream takes the opposite side.
- If the conscious attitude is "fairly near the middle," the dream offers slight variations.
- If the conscious attitude is "correct," the dream coincides with and emphasizes the tendency, "though without forfeiting its peculiar autonomy."

The third case is methodologically important. It is what prevents the compensatory theory from collapsing into the bromide that the dream just says the opposite of what the dreamer thinks.

## Taking up the context

The dream-interpretation method Jung commends — *taking up the context* — follows from the theory. The dream is not interpreted by reference to a fixed dream-key (as in the older tradition of *clefs des songes*) but by patient inquiry into what each salient feature of the dream meant for the dreamer:

> On the basis of these conclusions and for the purpose of ascertaining the meaning of the dream, I have developed a procedure which I call "*taking up the context*." This consists in making sure that every shade of meaning which each salient feature of the dream has for the dreamer is determined by the associations of the dreamer himself. *I therefore proceed in the same way as I would in deciphering a difficult text.* (Jung, "On the Nature of Dreams")

The practice is austere.

**Jung writes that, when a patient tells him a dream and asks his opinion, he has made it a rule "*to say first of all to myself: 'I have no idea what this dream means.' After that I can begin to examine the dream*."**

The dream is to be approached without theory and without preconception; the theory comes only in the second pass, after the context has been taken up.

The methodological humility is the part of Jung's practice that is most easily overlooked by readers familiar only with the popular caricature of Jungian dream-interpretation as a key-and-lock matching of dream-symbols to archetypes.

## "Big" and "little" dreams

The compensatory theory accommodates routine dreams — the "little" dreams Jung says even primitive cultures have distinguished from "big" or "significant" dreams.

Big dreams "are often remembered for a lifetime, and not infrequently prove to be the richest jewel in the treasure-house of psychic experience." They tend to occur "during the critical phases of life, in early youth, puberty, at the onset of middle age (thirty-six to forty), and within sight of death."

The category is what allows Jung to do the work he does in the alchemical and individuation-oriented later writings. The big dream is a dream in which the contents come not from the personal unconscious only but from a deeper layer Jung calls the collective unconscious, and these contents arrange themselves in long dream-series into what he names *the individuation process*.

**The collective layer is what makes the big dream "archetypal" in Jung's sense:**

> Significant dreams, on the other hand, are often remembered for a lifetime... I have examined many such dreams, and often found in them a peculiarity which distinguishes them from other dreams; *they contain symbolical images which we also come across in the mental history of mankind*. It is worth noting that the dreamer does not need to have any inkling of the existence of such parallels. *This peculiarity is characteristic of dreams of the individuation process*, where we find the mythological motifs or mythologems I have designated as archetypes. (Jung, "On the Nature of Dreams")

The methodological move that follows is the controversial one. Jung holds that to interpret a "big" dream, one must have access to the materials of mythology, folklore, and comparative religion, because the dream's symbolic vocabulary is shared with those traditions and not invented privately by the dreamer.

This is what gave Jungian dream interpretation its later academic status as a hybrid practice — half clinical, half mythographic.

## Two dreams Jung tells about himself

The theoretical positions are more vivid when read alongside the dream-narratives Jung gives of himself in *Memories, Dreams, Reflections*. Two are worth pulling forward.

### The crusader in the noonday rush

The first comes after Jung has had a dream in which Freud appears as "a peevish official of the Imperial Austrian monarchy, as a defunct and still walking ghost of a customs inspector" — a figure Jung reads as a compensatory image for his own too-deferential conscious attitude toward Freud at the time.

**The second part of the same dream is the part with the knight:**

> Suddenly to see in a modern city, during the noonday rush hour, a crusader coming toward me. What struck me as particularly odd was that none of the many persons walking about seemed to notice him. *No one turned his head or gazed after him. It was as though he were completely invisible to everyone but me.* I asked myself what this apparition meant, and then it was as if someone answered me but there was no one there to speak: "Yes, this is a regular apparition. The knight always passes by here between twelve and one o'clock, and has been doing so for a very long time [for centuries, I gathered] and everyone knows about it." (*Memories, Dreams, Reflections*, "Sigmund Freud," p. 164)

Jung's reading of the dream, given some pages later, is that the knight belonged to the twelfth century — the period when alchemy was beginning and when the Grail-quest was first being told — and that the dream was therefore announcing his "*own world, which had scarcely anything to do with Freud's*."

### The Liverpool dream

The Liverpool dream, much later, is the dream Jung treats as the climactic statement of his individuation process:

> I was in Liverpool. With a number of Swiss — say, half a dozen — I walked through the dark streets. I had the feeling that there we were coming from the harbor, and that the real city was actually up above, on the cliffs. We climbed up there.... When we reached the plateau, we found a broad square dimly illuminated by street lights, into which many streets converged. The various quarters of the city were arranged radially around the square. *In the center was a round pool, and in the middle of it a small island.* While everything round about was obscured by rain, fog, smoke, and dimly lit darkness, the little island blazed with sunlight. On it stood a single tree, a magnolia, in a shower of reddish blossoms. *It was as though the tree stood in the sunlight and were at the same time the source of light.* (*Memories, Dreams, Reflections*, "Confrontation with the Unconscious," p. 198)

**He gives the reading himself:**

> "Liverpool is the 'pool of life.' The 'liver,' according to an old view, is the seat of life — that which 'makes to live.' This dream brought with it a sense of finality. I saw that here the goal had been revealed. *One could not go beyond the center. The center is the goal, and everything is directed toward that center.* Through this dream I understood that the self is the principle and archetype of orientation and meaning."

## What the two dreams demonstrate

The two dreams together exemplify Jung's compensatory theory in operation. The knight dream supplies the figure that Jung's ordinary professional consciousness was not yet entertaining (his vocation lay outside the territory Freud occupied). The Liverpool dream supplies the figure that organizes the work he had been doing for two decades into a single image.

Neither dream is read as a wish-fulfillment in Freud's sense, and neither is treated as a private code to be unlocked by a fixed dictionary.

**Both are taken seriously as autonomous productions of an unconscious psyche that, on Jung's account, has its own purposes and its own structural commitments.**

The point worth carrying forward to Hillman, in the next lesson, is that even Jung — for whom the dream is finally the *via regia* to the unconscious in service of the dayworld self's individuation — is taking the dream seriously *on something like its own terms*. Whether the dream's terms are really compatible with the individuation framework is the question Hillman would put.
