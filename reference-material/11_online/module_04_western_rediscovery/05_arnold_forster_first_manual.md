# Module 4, Lesson 5 — Mary Arnold-Forster: The First Instructional Manual (1921)

*Where Saint-Denys produced an introspective record and van Eeden a typology, Arnold-Forster produced something the tradition had not yet had: a practical manual. And the kernel of what LaBerge would, sixty years later, codify as MILD.*

---

## A serious self-observer

**Mary Arnold-Forster** (1857–1951) — wife of the British politician Hugh Oakeley Arnold-Forster — published *Studies in Dreams* with George Allen & Unwin in 1921.

The book opens with a foreword by Morton Prince, the American psychologist who had earlier written *The Dissociation of a Personality*. Prince says of her that "*the accurate recording of observations accurately made is the basis of [science] and requires the development and possession of no common talent*" — a measured endorsement that places the project squarely in the empirical register.

**Her preface is unguarded about the book's aim:**

> By rights, perhaps, the task of an unscientific recorder of dreams should be simply to explore — to record his experiences in the dream state; leaving it to science to explain the whys and wherefores of what has been observed. *But it is very difficult for one who has dreamed much, and thought much, and read much, about dreaming not to stray a little beyond the proper province of the recorder*, and not to attempt some partial explanation of some of the riddles that are met with. (Arnold-Forster 1921, Preface)

What follows is a chapter-by-chapter exposition of methods. The chapter titles alone are diagnostic: *Introductory*; *Dream Control*; *Flying Dreams*; *Dream Recording*; and onward through more specialized topics.

## The chapter on dream control

The book's central methodological contribution sits in chapter II, "Dream Control," and in chapter IV, "Dream Recording."

**The ambition of the chapter on dream control is announced plainly:**

> *I believe, in short, that we can at will stop the recurrence of "bad" dreams*, or of dreams that we dislike or dread, and that we can, to a considerable extent, alter the very nature of our dreams by using in our sleep the same faculty of rational selection and rejection that we use with regard to our thoughts and to our wandering fancies by day. (Arnold-Forster 1921, Chapter II)

The proposed method is straightforward, and is given in the form of a *formula* — a sentence or two, repeated during the day and on going to bed, that becomes (with practice) the cue by which the dream mind orients itself within a recurring distressing dream.

**Her own formula she gives in full: *Remember this is a dream. You are to dream no longer.***

The mechanism, as she describes it, is simple recurrence:

> *I tried repeating this formula to myself from time to time, during the day and on going to bed, always in the same words*... until, I suppose, the suggestion that I wanted to imprint upon the dream mind became more definite and more powerful than the impression of any dream; so that when a dream of distress begins to trouble me, the oft-repeated formula is automatically suggested. (Arnold-Forster 1921, Chapter II)

## The kernel of MILD

**In this 1921 passage sits the kernel of what LaBerge would later formalize as MILD — the Mnemonic Induction of Lucid Dreams.**

Arnold-Forster's formula functions as exactly what MILD would later prescribe: a pre-sleep intention, embedded in a memorable phrase, whose repeated rehearsal gradually trains the sleeping mind to recognize the dream state as a dream state.

She is not framing her method as an induction technique for lucid dreaming. Her stated aim is the elimination of bad dreams. But the mechanism she has discovered is general, and her own dream records show the same.

The chapter on "voluntary dreaming" extends the same formula in the positive direction: setting a pre-sleep intention to fly, or to revisit a particular landscape, or to find oneself in a particular dream-room.

## Three independent discoveries of the same mechanism

Set this against what Module 5 will eventually cover.

LaBerge, in 1980, in a Stanford laboratory, codified what he called MILD as a structured five-step procedure: set up dream recall; recall a dream; focus the intent on remembering the dream state; visualize the recognition; repeat.

**The structural mechanism — pre-sleep intention, embedded in memorable language, internalized through patient repetition — is the mechanism Arnold-Forster had already worked out fifty-nine years earlier.**

The Tholey reflection technique that came out of Frankfurt in the late 1950s is the third independent discovery of essentially the same mechanism.

Three independent traditions arrive at compatible accounts of what works: the British amateur, the German experimental psychologist, and the American sleep researcher. The Tibetan tradition had been working with a structurally similar method — *the power of resolution* — for a thousand years.

**The convergence is real. The mechanism is robust enough that it shows up wherever serious people work on the question.**

## The voice of the book

A sustained passage from Arnold-Forster's chapter on dream recording is also worth marking — both for what it shares with the present-day dream-journaling literature and for the unguarded interest in dream cultivation as a practice of attention worth cultivating for its own sake:

> Sleep which brings us our dreams fulfils the eternal need within us, the need of romance, the need of adventure; for *sleep is the gate which lets us slip through into the enchanted country that lies beyond*. We give too little heed to the nightly miracle of our dreams. Long hours are spent by us in dreaming sleep, during which we are absorbed in a life which is for the most part forgotten or ignored by our waking consciousness. (Arnold-Forster 1921, Preface)

The voice here is the voice of a serious self-observer who is not pretending to be a scientist and who is not trying to interpret the dream into anything it is not.

**The empirical-introspective tradition reaches its practical maturity in this book.**

## A historical note

A short historical note. Arnold-Forster presented her work in the diffident register that women writing as amateurs in the early twentieth century were expected to adopt.

The diffidence has the secondary effect, in the present period, of having concealed how directly her work prefigures both Tholey's reflection technique (1959 onward) and LaBerge's MILD (1980 onward).

The Carroy reframing of Saint-Denys applies, by extension, to Arnold-Forster: there was a serious empirical tradition before Freud's Vienna and after his ascent, and the work of recovering it is still in progress.

## What Arnold-Forster hands forward

What Arnold-Forster hands forward to the contemporary practitioner is therefore:

- **A method** — the formula
- **A frame for that method** — pre-sleep intention as the active ingredient
- **An empirical record** — her own decade-spanning journal
- **A tone** — serious without being self-important, practical without being technocratic, attentive to dream as a real object of cultivation without being mystified by it

The next two lessons turn from the empirical-introspective tradition to the depth-psychological one. Jung's mature theory of the dream as compensation, and Hillman's archetypal pushback against the use of dreams as instruments of the dayworld self.
