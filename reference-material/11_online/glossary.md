# Glossary

*Key terms drawn from the work's source material in five languages — Sanskrit, Pāli, Tibetan, Greek, Arabic — with the language of origin in parentheses, the lesson where the term is introduced in brackets, and a working definition. The lessons reintroduce the terms where they matter.*

---

## Sanskrit

**ātman** (Skt) [Module 1, Lesson 2] — The deepest layer of the self, identified in the Upaniṣads with *brahman*, the ultimate ground. The awareness that runs through waking, dream, and deep sleep. The Māṇḍūkya identifies the fourth quarter (*turīya*) with *ātman*.

**brahman** (Skt) [Module 1, Lesson 2] — The ultimate ground; in the principal Upaniṣads, identified with *ātman* in the practitioner.

**catuṣ-pāt** (Skt) [Module 1, Lesson 2] — "Four-quartered." The four quarters of the self in the Māṇḍūkya: *vaiśvānara*, *taijasa*, *prājña*, *turīya*.

**jāgrat** (Skt) [Module 1, Lesson 2] — The waking state.

**māyā** (Skt) [Module 2, Lesson 4] — Illusion. The character of dream phenomena, and (in the Mahāyāna and Tibetan view) of all unanalyzed experience.

**prājña** (Skt) [Module 1, Lesson 2] — "The knower." The awareness as it appears in deep dreamless sleep — undifferentiated, "consisting of bliss and feeding on bliss."

**prapañca** (Skt) [Module 1, Lesson 2] — The manifest manifold of objects; the world of multiplicity that comes to rest in *turīya*.

**prāṇa** (Skt) [Module 1, Lesson 3; Module 2, Lesson 6] — Vital function, breath, life-energy. In the Tibetan tradition, *prāṇa* (or Tibetan *lung*) is the energetic correlate of mind that the dream-yoga and sleep-yoga practices work with.

**sṛj** (Skt) [Module 1, Lesson 3] — The verb "to create" used in Bṛhadāraṇyaka 4.3 of the dreamer; the same verb used elsewhere for divine creation. The dreamer creates the dream-world the way a god creates a cosmos.

**suṣupti** (Skt) [Module 1, Lesson 2] — Deep dreamless sleep.

**svapna** (Skt) [Module 1, Lesson 2] — Sleep, dream, the dream state. The Tibetan equivalent is *milam* (Tib *rmi lam*).

**śūnyatā** (Skt) [Module 1, Lesson 6] — Emptiness; the dependently-arisen, illusion-like character of all phenomena. The central concept of the Madhyamaka school. The Mahāyāna's "all form is like a dream" is a statement about *śūnyatā*.

**taijasa** (Skt) [Module 1, Lesson 2] — "The radiant one." The awareness as it appears in the dream state.

**turīya** (Skt) [Module 1, Lesson 2] — "The fourth." The awareness running through all three states (waking, dream, deep sleep) and described positively as *prapañca-upaśama* (the coming to rest of all multiplicity), *śānta* (peaceful), *śiva* (auspicious), *advaita* (non-dual).

**vaiśvānara** (Skt) [Module 1, Lesson 2] — "Universal in waking form." The awareness as it appears in the waking state, "outward-turned," "enjoying gross objects."

**ālaya** / **ālayavijñāna** (Skt, Yogācāra) [Module 6, Lesson 4] — The substrate; the substrate consciousness. In Wallace's contemporary register, the deep-sleep "substrate" is what sleep yoga works with, and the recognition of pristine awareness through the substrate is what the practice points at.

## Pāli

**bhavaṅga** (Pāli) [Module 1, Lesson 4] — The life-continuum; "the passive stream of the life-continuum, below the threshold of awareness" (Mendis). In the Abhidhamma analysis, the unconscious continuity of mind that is briefly disturbed by mental contents during dream.

**kapi-niddā** (Pāli) [Module 1, Lesson 5] — "Monkey-sleep." The half-asleep, half-awake liminal phase in which Nāgasena, in the Milindapañha, locates dream-experience.

**kiriya** (Pāli) [Module 1, Lesson 4] — "Functional." A class of mental activity in the Abhidhamma that arises and passes without leaving karmic traces of the kind waking thought leaves. Dream-consciousness is, on this analysis, *kiriya*.

**supina** (Pāli) [Module 1, Lesson 4] — The Pāli word for dream. (Sanskrit: *svapna*.)

## Tibetan

**bardo** (Tib *bar do*) [Module 2, Lesson 7] — Intermediate state. The Bardo Thödol enumerates six bardos: birth, dreams, samādhi-meditation, the moment before death, dharmatā, and becoming. Dream practice is, on the tradition's account, training for the after-death bardos.

**Bön** (Tib) [Module 2, Lesson 9] — The institutional contemplative tradition of Tibet that traces itself to Tonpa Shenrab Miwoche, predates Buddhist Tibet, and runs in parallel with the Buddhist lineages. Tenzin Wangyal Rinpoche is the principal contemporary Western-facing Bön teacher.

**Dzogchen** (Tib *rdzogs chen*) [Module 2, Lesson 5] — "Great perfection." A contemplative tradition transmitted in the Nyingma school of Tibetan Buddhism and in the Bön. Centers the recognition of *rigpa* — pristine awareness — and treats dream and sleep yoga as applications of that prior recognition.

**gyulü** (Tib *sgyu lus*) [Module 2, Lesson 2] — Illusory body. One of the Six Yogas of Naropa. In Tsongkhapa's structure, dream yoga is one of three illusory-body practices.

**Mahāmudrā** (Skt; Tib *phyag rgya chen po*) [Module 2, Lesson 8] — "Great seal." A contemplative tradition transmitted in the Kagyu school. Centers the recognition of mind's nature through the four yogas (one-pointedness, simplicity, one taste, non-meditation).

**milam** (Tib *rmi lam*) [Module 2, Lesson 1] — Dream. The dream yoga of the Six Yogas of Naropa; the practice this work centers.

**ösel** (Tib *'od gsal*) [Module 2, Lesson 2; Lesson 8] — Clear light. The recognition of the awareness that runs through all states; the central event toward which the entire architecture of practice is bent.

**phowa** (Tib *'pho ba*) [Module 2, Lesson 2] — Transference of consciousness. One of the Six Yogas of Naropa; the practice that takes the work into the death-process if liberation has not been attained in life.

**rigpa** (Tib *rig pa*) [Module 2, Lesson 8] — Pristine innate awareness. The Dzogchen term for what the Māṇḍūkya called *turīya* — the awareness that runs through all states.

**Six Yogas of Naropa** (Tib *Nā-ro chos drug*) [Module 2, Lesson 2] — *Tummo* (inner heat), *gyulü* (illusory body), *milam* (dream), *ösel* (clear light), *bardo* (intermediate state), *phowa* (transference). The system of six interlocking practices that codifies the Tibetan dream-yoga tradition's institutional core.

**trekchö** (Tib *khregs chod*) [Module 6, Lesson 4] — "Cutting through." The Dzogchen practice of cutting through the substrate consciousness to the pristine awareness it normally occludes.

**tsa** (Tib *rtsa*) [Module 6, Lesson 5] — Channels. The energy-body model that the practice rests on, alongside *lung* (vital energies) and *thig le* (vital essences).

**tummo** (Tib *gtum mo*) [Module 2, Lesson 2] — Inner heat. The foundational practice of the Six Yogas; the technology by which the practitioner draws subtle life-sustaining energies into the central channel.

**Zhang Zhung Nyan Gyud** (Tib) [Module 2, Lesson 9] — "The Oral Transmission of Zhang Zhung." The principal Bön transmission of dream practice, together with the *Mother Tantra*.

## Chinese

**jīng** (Ch 精) [Module 3, Lesson 3] — Essence; the most refined of the three inner substances of Daoist inner alchemy. The generative energy that the *neidan* sequence transforms into *qì*, then into *shén*, then into *xū* (emptiness).

**neidan** (Ch 內丹) [Module 3, Lesson 3] — Internal alchemy. The Daoist contemplative-energetic tradition associated with the Quanzhen school and earlier. The framework against which the Daoist sleep-cultivation tradition developed.

**qì** (Ch 氣) [Module 3, Lesson 3] — Vital energy. The second of the three inner substances of Daoist inner alchemy.

**qīngjìng** (Ch 清靜) [Module 3, Lesson 3] — Stillness, purity. The central concern of the *Tài Shàng Qīngjìng Jīng* (*Cultivating Stillness*) and the prerequisite for the Daoist sleep-cultivation traditions.

**shén** (Ch 神) [Module 3, Lesson 3] — Spirit. The third and most refined of the three inner substances. The cultivation of *shén* is, for the Daoist tradition, what waking life is steadily losing and what contemplative practice recovers.

**wù huà** (Ch 物化) [Module 3, Lesson 2] — "The transformation of things." The Zhuangzian doctrine in three syllables: the boundaries between things are themselves transforming, not fixed.

## Greek

**daimonia** (Gk) [Module 1, Lesson 8] — "Daemonic." Aristotle's concession on dreams: dreams are *daimonia* but not divine. Their cause is divinely planned, though not itself divine.

**kinēsis** (Gk) [Module 1, Lesson 7] — Movement. Aristotle's term for the residual sense-impressions that produce dream-images: just as a thrown projectile continues to move after the hand has released it, sense-impressions continue to move after the perceived object is gone.

**phantastikon** (Gk) [Module 1, Lesson 7] — The presentative or imaginative faculty. Aristotle's third option (against intellect or simple sense) for the faculty that produces dream-experience: dream "belongs to this faculty *qua* presentative."

## Arabic

**barzakh** (Ar) [Module 3, Lesson 5] — Isthmus, barrier. The Qur'anic word for an intermediate domain. Ibn al-ʿArabī treats the dream-state, the imaginal world, and the eschatological intermediate state as the same kind of *barzakh* — a real third thing, neither of the two domains it separates, but possessing the power of both.

**huwa lā huwa** (Ar) [Module 3, Lesson 4] — "He / not He." Ibn al-ʿArabī's technical name for the *both/and* ontology of imagination. The face in the mirror is real; the face in the mirror is not real; the face in the mirror is real *as image*.

**iʿtibār** (Ar) [Module 3, Lesson 5] — "Crossing over"; "taking heed." The Qur'anic call to read the manifest world as a dream, crossing from its forms to the meanings they imply. From the same root as *taʿbīr*.

**khayāl** (Ar) [Module 3, Lesson 4] — Imagination. For Ibn al-ʿArabī, not a psychological faculty but an ontological category. A real domain of being whose mode of existence is imaginal — neither material nor purely spiritual.

**maʿnā** (Ar) [Module 3, Lesson 4] — Meaning. A reality of the world of intelligible things without outward form. Imagination takes a *maʿnā* and gives it sensory form (*ṣūra maḥsūsa*).

**ʿālam al-khayāl** / **ʿālam al-mithāl** (Ar) [Module 3, Lesson 4] — World of Imagination / World of Image-Likenesses. The intermediate level between the World of Spirits (*ʿālam al-arwāḥ*) and the World of Bodies (*ʿālam al-ajsām*) in Ibn al-ʿArabī's cosmology.

**ruʾyā** (Ar) [Module 3, Lesson 5] — Dream, dream-vision. The Arabic word for dream specifically as the phenomenon Ibn al-ʿArabī treats philosophically.

**taʿbīr** (Ar) [Module 3, Lesson 5] — Dream-interpretation; literally "crossing over." The science of crossing from the sensory form of the dream back to the meaning that wore the form. From the root *ʿ.b.r.* (cross, traverse, ford).

## French

**conscience en dormant** (Fr) [Module 4, Lesson 2] — "Awareness in sleep." Saint-Denys's term for the recognition, while asleep, of one's true situation. The empirical-introspective tradition's earliest phrasing of what van Eeden would later call lucidity.

**marche spontanée des idées** (Fr) [Module 4, Lesson 2] — "The spontaneous procession of ideas." Saint-Denys's term for the dream's apparent autonomy; the fact that the dream's content seems to arrive on its own.

## English (technical)

**dreamsign** [Module 5, Lesson 4] — A recurrent oddity of one's own dream content (e.g., the impossibility of reading numbers consistently, the appearance of a particular figure, the dreamlike quality of a particular setting) that the practitioner trains as a trigger for state-checking and lucidity.

**DILD** (dream-initiated lucid dream) [Module 5, Lesson 4] — A lucid dream in which the practitioner becomes aware of the dream-state from inside an already-arising dream, typically triggered by a dreamsign. The most common form of laboratory-verified lucid dream.

**lucid dream** [Module 4, Lesson 4] — The state in which "the reintegration of the psychic functions is so complete that the sleeper remembers day-life and his own condition, reaches a state of perfect awareness, and is able to direct his attention, and to attempt different acts of free volition" (van Eeden 1913).

**MILD** (Mnemonic Induction of Lucid Dreams) [Module 5, Lesson 4] — LaBerge's structured induction technique built on prospective memory. Pre-sleep intention, embedded in memorable language, internalized through patient repetition.

**REM** (rapid eye movement) [Module 5, Lesson 2] — The phase of sleep characterized by rapid, jerky, binocularly symmetrical eye movements, low-amplitude irregular EEG, mild elevation in respiratory rate, descending muscle atonia, and the concentration of vivid dreaming. Discovered by Aserinsky and Kleitman, *Science*, September 4, 1953.

**state check** [Module 5, Lesson 4] — A waking-life practice (e.g., looking at a digital clock, glancing away, looking back) that builds the meta-cognitive habit of asking "Am I dreaming?" In the Tholey reflection technique, this is the upstream practice on which MILD's prospective-memory rehearsal depends.

**WBTB** (Wake Back to Bed) [Module 5, Lesson 4] — An induction protocol that exploits the late-night concentration of REM. The practitioner sleeps several hours, wakes, stays awake for thirty to ninety minutes, then returns to sleep — typically into a long REM episode unusually likely to contain a lucid dream.

**WILD** (Wake-Initiated Lucid Dream) [Module 5, Lesson 4] — A lucid dream in which the practitioner falls asleep without losing consciousness, with awareness present in the resulting dream by continuity. The Tibetan tradition's "practice of natural light" is structurally a WILD technique.
