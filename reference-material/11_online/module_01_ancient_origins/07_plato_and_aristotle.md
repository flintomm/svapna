# Module 1, Lesson 7 — Plato and Aristotle on Dream

*The Greco-Roman dream tradition is parallel to the Indian, not derivative of it. The Indian tradition asked, in the first instance, what dream is. The Greek tradition asked what dream means — and, as the philosophical schools developed, what dream is in a strictly naturalistic register.*

---

## Plato: the dream as diagnostic

Plato uses dream in the *Republic* mostly as an illustrative figure. Two passages matter most.

In Book V, the dreamer is the figure for the lover of sights and sounds who mistakes the copy for the original:

> "And he who, having a sense of beautiful things has no sense of absolute beauty, or who, if another lead him to a knowledge of that beauty is unable to follow — of such an one I ask, Is he awake or in a dream only? Reflect: *is not the dreamer, sleeping or waking, one who likens dissimilar things, who puts the copy in the place of the real object?*" (*Republic* V, Jowett trans.)

The dreamer here is the figure for the man without philosophical training — for whom the world of appearance is taken for the world of being.

This is the Platonic operation that will be parallel-mirrored, with very different consequences, in the Mahāyāna's use of dream as the figure for *all* unanalyzed experience. Plato's dreamer is the man who has not yet awoken to the Forms; the Mahāyāna's dreamer is the one who has not yet recognized emptiness. The figures rhyme; the metaphysics behind them does not.

### The tyrant's dream

The famous passage on the dream and the tyrant is in Book IX. Plato distinguishes necessary from unnecessary appetites and asks which appetites are unlawful. His answer is that the unlawful ones are

> "those which are awake when the reasoning and human and ruling power is asleep; *then the wild beast within us, gorged with meat or drink, starts up and having shaken off sleep, goes forth to satisfy his desires*; and there is no conceivable folly or crime — not excepting incest or any other unnatural union, or parricide, or the eating of forbidden food — which at such a time, when he has parted company with all shame and sense, a man may not be ready to commit." (*Republic* IX, Jowett trans.)

The diagnostic value of this for Plato is clear. The dream reveals what the rational part of the soul holds in check during waking.

**By contrast, the philosopher cultivates a different kind of sleep:**

> "But when a man's pulse is healthy and temperate, and when before going to sleep he has awakened his rational powers, and fed them on noble thoughts and enquiries, collecting himself in meditation; after having first indulged his appetites neither too much nor too little, but just enough to lay them to sleep, and prevent them and their enjoyments and pains from interfering with the higher principle … *then, as you know, he attains truth most nearly, and is least likely to be the sport of fantastic and lawless visions*." (*Republic* IX, Jowett)

This is, in its way, an early piece of pre-sleep contemplative hygiene — the kind of evening composition that appears, in different vocabularies, in the Tibetan preparatory practices for dream yoga and in the Daoist sleep cultivations.

Plato is not prescribing a dream practice; he is prescribing a waking discipline that is intended to produce a certain quality of dream. The diagnostic move (what the dream reveals of the soul) and the cultivatory move (what the soul reveals in dream when properly prepared) are present here. They are present sketchily, and they are not developed. But they are present.

## Aristotle: the founding empirical-skeptical treatment

If Plato uses the dream as a philosophical figure, Aristotle takes the dream as a phenomenon to be explained.

The *Parva Naturalia* contains three short treatises on sleep and dream: *De Somno et Vigilia* (*On Sleep and Waking*), *De Insomniis* (*On Dreams*), and *De Divinatione per Somnum* (*On Divination in Sleep*). The Oxford translation by J.I. Beare preserves the patient analytical voice that has more in common with the Pāli Abhidhamma than with the Upaniṣadic register.

### Dream as residual sense-impression

**Aristotle's *De Insomniis* opens by asking which faculty of the soul perceives a dream — sense or intellect — and arrives, after careful argument, at a third option:**

> "It is plain therefore that this affection, which we name 'dreaming', is no mere exercise of opinion or intelligence, but yet is not an affection of the faculty of perception in the simple sense. … *dreaming is an activity of the faculty of sense-perception, but belongs to this faculty qua presentative.*" (Aristotle, *De Insomniis* I, 459a, trans. Beare)

The technical claim is that dream belongs to the *phantastikon* — the imagination or presentative faculty — which is not a separate faculty from sense but is sense's residual or representative activity.

In ordinary perception, the senses present what is now there. In dream, they present residual movements: traces of perception that persist when the prior perception itself is gone. Aristotle's term is *kinēsis* — movement — and he develops the account through analogy.

Just as a thrown projectile continues to move after the hand has released it, so a sense-impression continues to move within the sensory organs after the perceived object is gone. Dream is what happens when these residual movements are accessible, in sleep, without being overridden by present perception.

### The phenomenology

The text is full of acute observations on the phenomenology. The afterimage that persists when one turns from sunlight into darkness; the apparent movement that persists when one looks away from a flowing river; the way "persons become very deaf after hearing loud noises, and after smelling very strong odours their power of smelling is impaired" (*De Insomniis* II, 459b–460a).

These observations are deployed as evidence for the residual-movement account: the same phenomenon that produces afterimages in waking produces dream-images in sleep.

What Aristotle then does — and this is the move that will define the Western philosophical-scientific treatment of dream until at least the seventeenth century — is provide a careful account of how the residual movements get deformed in sleep. When the sleeper is in disturbed states (illness, fever, intoxication, the "atrabilious"), the residual movements are violently agitated. In healthier conditions, the dream-presentations are clearer; the brain is, in effect, performing the same perceptual work without external input.

Aristotle is, in other words, giving a humoral-physiological account of dream that rhymes structurally with the Indian medical tradition's *vāta-pitta-kapha* analysis without any direct contact between them.

### The earliest surviving account of lucid dreaming

**The most striking observation in *De Insomniis*, for readers coming to Aristotle from the dream-yoga tradition, comes when the text addresses what could now be called lucid dreaming:**

> "exactly so it is in states of sleep: *if the sleeper perceives that he is asleep, and is conscious of the sleeping state during which the perception comes before his mind, it presents itself still, but something within him speaks to this effect: 'the image of Koriskos presents itself, but the real Koriskos is not present'*; for often, when one is asleep, there is something in consciousness which declares that what then presents itself is but a dream. If, however, he is not aware of being asleep, there is nothing which will contradict the testimony of the bare presentation." (*De Insomniis* III, 462a, Beare)

This is, as far as can be told, the earliest surviving treatment in any tradition of what twentieth-century researchers will call lucidity within the dream — the recognition, in dream, that one is dreaming.

Aristotle treats it as an ordinary psychological possibility ("often, when one is asleep, there is something in consciousness which declares that what then presents itself is but a dream"), not as a special achievement. He does not develop it as a technique; he does not seem to think it requires development. But he sees it.

That observation — Aristotle, fourth century BCE, on the recognition of dreaming-while-dreaming — is part of why this lesson matters. Twenty-three hundred years before Frederik van Eeden coined the term *lucid dream* and twenty-four hundred years before Stephen LaBerge's eye-signal experiment at Stanford, the phenomenon is sitting on the page of one of the founding texts of Western natural philosophy.

**The pattern recurs across traditions: each one recognized something the others did not get around to until much later.**
