# Module 1, Lesson 4 — Indian Buddhist Treatments: The Four (or Six) Causes

*The Buddhist tradition that emerged from the same intellectual environment as the Upaniṣads developed its own treatment of dream — different in vocabulary, different in emphasis, often different in conclusions, but in close conversation with the Vedic background.*

---

The Buddhist analyses of dream that the Tibetan tradition will eventually inherit run through two principal Indian Buddhist sources. The first is the Pāli Abhidhamma analysis exemplified by Buddhaghosa's fifth-century *Visuddhimagga*. The second is the dialogue on dream in the *Milindapañha*, which the next lesson takes up. Together they give the early Buddhist tradition its working orientation.

The Pāli word for dream is *supina*; the Sanskrit, *svapna*. The Buddhist tradition takes the term over from the broader Indian background but works it inside a different analytical apparatus.

## The Visuddhimagga and the four-cause analysis

The classical Buddhist taxonomy of dreams is referenced in Buddhaghosa's *Visuddhimagga*, translated by Bhikkhu Ñāṇamoli as *The Path of Purification*. The text itself, in chapter IX (on the divine abidings), enumerates a meditator's "evil dreams" only by example — "as though being surrounded by bandits, as though being threatened by wild beasts, as though falling into chasms" (Vism IX.62, Ñāṇamoli).

**The footnote at this point points to where the four-cause analysis actually lives:**

> "For the commentarial description of dream consciousness and kamma effected during dreams, see Vibh-a (commentary to Ñāṇa-Vibhaṅga, Ekaka) and A-a, (commentary to AN 5:196) which largely but not entirely overlap. *Vism-mhṭ says here: 'The seeing of dreams is done with consciousness consisting only of the functional.'*" (Ñāṇamoli, *Visuddhimagga* XIV.114 n.45)

The tradition that Buddhaghosa is summarizing — and that Bhikkhu Ñāṇamoli's footnote points toward — assigns dreams to four (or, in the Milinda redaction, six) causes: disturbance of the wind humour (*vāta*), disturbance of bile (*pitta*), disturbance of phlegm (*kapha*), and the activity of a deity who brings a true portent.

The classification is medical-cosmological in origin, drawn from the same Indian humoral framework that also informs Ayurveda. The Milinda dialogue, which the next lesson will quote in detail, expands this fourfold list into a sixfold one by adding two further sources: dreams arising from one's own habitual mental tendencies, and dreams that are genuine premonitions.

## A register-shift from the Upaniṣads

What is striking about this analysis, set against the Upaniṣadic register, is its register-shift. The Upaniṣadic seers ask what the dreamer is. The Buddhist analysts ask what produces the dream.

The first question is metaphysical; the second is causal. The Buddhist move is, in its way, the more conservative one. It keeps the dream as a phenomenon in the natural-medical-cosmological order, and refuses the Upaniṣadic move of treating the dreamer as a creator-god in miniature.

## Dream-consciousness as kiriya

What the Visuddhimagga frame does most usefully — and what the Pāli tradition is firm on — is locate dream-consciousness within the technical Abhidhamma map of mind. Dream is what happens when the *bhavaṅga* — the life-continuum, "the passive stream of the life-continuum, below the threshold of awareness" (Mendis's gloss) — is briefly disturbed by mental contents that are, in the technical sense, *kiriya* or *functional* rather than karmically productive in the same way as waking thought.

That last clause is doing real work. *Kiriya* mental activity, in the Abhidhamma, is mental activity that arises and passes without leaving a karmic trace of the kind that waking thought leaves.

The mind is moving in dream — that is not in dispute — but the mind is not, by the strict commentarial reading, accumulating new kamma in the same register. This is the analysis the Milinda dialogue will develop dramatically in its handling of King Milinda's dilemmas about dream and moral responsibility, and it is the position the Vinaya (the monastic rule) is famously casuistic about: nocturnal emissions, dream-actions, dream-intentions are not, on this reading, equivalent to their waking counterparts.

## Two consequences

Two consequences follow that are worth holding.

**First**, the Buddhist analytical tradition admits dream as evidence — about what the mind does when freed from external sense-input, about what habitual tendencies surface in unguarded conditions, about what the body's humoral state is — without inflating it into prophecy.

This is closer in epistemic register to the Greek-naturalistic tradition (which the next lessons cover) than to the Upaniṣadic-metaphysical one. The Buddhist analyst and the Aristotelian natural philosopher would have found more to talk about with each other, on the question of dream, than either would have found with Yājñavalkya.

**Second**, by tying dream-consciousness to the *kiriya* or *functional* register, the Buddhist tradition produces a particular stance toward what the practitioner does with dream content. Take it seriously as data; do not take it as a private theatre for moral self-reproach.

A dream-intention is not a waking intention. A dream-action is not a waking action. The dreamer is not the agent in the same operative sense.

## What this hands forward

This is the framework the Tibetan dream-yoga literature, much later, will inherit, develop, and finally invert. In the Tibetan synthesis, the recognition that one is dreaming is the entry condition for a deliberate, agentive engagement with the dream content — flying, transforming dream objects, walking through dream walls — that is precisely *not* the unguarded, half-functional engagement the early Pāli tradition assumed.

But that inversion comes much later. What the early Indian Buddhist tradition gives is the prior analytical frame: dreams have causes, those causes are classifiable, dream-consciousness is functional rather than fully agentive, and the moral weight of dream-content is correspondingly lighter than the moral weight of waking thought.

The Milinda dialogue — the next lesson — pushes this analysis further than the Visuddhimagga does. It also asks the question the Visuddhimagga does not quite ask: *who* is dreaming, and *when*?
