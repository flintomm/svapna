# Module 1, Lesson 6 — Śāntideva: Dream as the Figure for Emptiness

*The Mahāyāna Buddhist tradition that emerged in the early centuries of the Common Era did something the early schools did not: it made dream not merely an object of analysis but the central illustrative figure for its philosophical claims about the nature of waking experience itself.*

---

The Yogācāra school is most associated with this move. Vasubandhu's *Viṃśatikā* uses dream as the paradigm case for showing that what is taken to be experience of an external world is experience of cognitive appearances.

But the figure is most lyrically deployed in the work that the Tibetan tradition will most absorb: Śāntideva's *Bodhicaryāvatāra*, the "Way of the Bodhisattva."

Śāntideva's poem (eighth century, Nālandā) is in ten chapters. Chapter 9, the *Prajñāpāramitā* chapter on wisdom, is the one in which the dream-figure does its hardest philosophical work. The Padmakara translation rendered here gives Śāntideva's argument with a directness that survives the verse form.

The chapter is built around an extended dialogue with hypothetical opponents — Yogācārins, Mādhyamikas, common-sense realists — and the dream reappears at every turn as the figure that says what the argument cannot say analytically.

Three uses are worth marking.

## Dream as the figure for impermanence

**First, dream as the figure for the equality of long and short lives, which is also the equality of long and short dreams:**

> "One man dreams he lives a hundred years
> Of happiness, but then he wakes.
> Another dreams an instant's joy,
> But then he likewise wakes.
>
> And when they wake, the happiness of both
> Is finished, never to return.
> *Likewise, when the hour of death comes round,
> Our lives are over, whether brief or long.*" (BCA 7.57–58, Padmakara)

This is the dream as the working figure for impermanence — not a metaphor laid on top of an argument, but the way the argument moves. The dream is the most familiar instance of a vivid duration that ends with no remainder. The poem treats the entire embodied life as continuous with this familiar fact.

## Dream as the figure for the body's emptiness

**Second, dream as the figure for the body's lack of self-identity:**

> "Thus there is no 'body.' It is through illusion,
> With regard to hands and other parts, that 'body' as a notion is conceived —
> Just as on account of its specific shape
> A pile of stones is taken for a man.
>
> As long as the conditions are assembled,
> The body will appear to be a man. …
>
> *All form, therefore, is like a dream,*
> And who will be attached to it, who thus investigates?" (BCA 9.83–87, Padmakara)

The argument here is the standard Madhyamaka analysis — the body, on careful inspection, is a heap of particles that themselves dissolve under analysis — and the dream is the figure that closes the argument.

To say "all form is like a dream" is not, for Śāntideva, to assert a metaphysical thesis that forms are merely mental. It is to say that the *attachment* to forms, once they have been investigated carefully, is as poorly grounded as attachment to dream-content.

The phenomenology of recognition is the same. The dreamer recognizes, on waking, that he has been dreaming; on analysis, the practitioner recognizes that the body to which he was attached has the same provisional ontology.

## Dream as the figure for the equal deceptiveness of presence and absence

**Third, and most consequentially for the dream-yoga tradition:**

> "When therefore in one's dream a child has died,
> The state of mind that thinks it is no more
> Supplants the thought that it is living still.
> *And yet both thoughts are equally deceptive.*" (BCA 9.140, Padmakara)

This single verse is the philosophical engine of dream practice in the later Tibetan tradition. The dream-thought "the child has died" feels devastating in the dream; the dream-thought "the child is living still" feels comforting; both are dream-thoughts.

Recognition of the dream-status of *both* thoughts dissolves the asymmetry. The verse is the Mādhyamika argument for the emptiness of grief and for the emptiness of consolation, in two lines, with the dream as the demonstration.

Tibetan dream practice will operationalize exactly this: in the dream, the practitioner notices the dream-status of pleasant and frightening contents alike, and what is recognized is not a particular content but the equality of contents in their dependent arising.

## The chapter's closing verse

The chapter ends with a verse that has become — through Tibetan transmission — one of the lines of poetry the tradition's later teachers most often cite:

> "Wandering beings, thus, resemble dreams,
> And also the banana tree, if you examine well.
> *In ultimate reality there's no distinguishing
> Between the states of sorrow and beyond all sorrow.*" (BCA 9.150, Padmakara)

The banana tree is a stock Indian image for what looks substantial from outside but on examination is found to be sheath upon sheath with nothing solid at the center. The dream is its companion figure.

The verse compresses the entire chapter's argument into a couplet and bequeaths it to the meditative traditions that will read the *Bodhicaryāvatāra* for the next twelve hundred years.

## What the Mahāyāna adds

What Śāntideva — and the Yogācāra-Madhyamaka synthesis behind him — gives the dream-practice tradition is the philosophical permission to treat the dream-state as continuous with the waking state, not as its inferior shadow. Bṛhadāraṇyaka 4.3 had already put the move on the table. The Māṇḍūkya had given the structural framework.

**What the Mahāyāna adds is the soteriological frame: recognition of the dream-status of waking experience is *itself the path*, not a stage prior to the path.**

When the Tibetan teachers, working in the eighth through eleventh centuries, build the practice now called dream yoga, they are operating on Śāntideva's premise. The dream is the analogy, and the analogy is the argument, and the argument is the practice.

It is worth noting — although the connection is famously contested — that Śāntideva's chapter 9 contains arguments that are almost word-for-word continuous with arguments in Gauḍapāda's seventh-century *Māṇḍūkya-kārikā*, the verse commentary on the Māṇḍūkya that Śaṅkara would later treat as foundational for Advaita Vedānta.

The argument for the parity of dream and waking — the move that says the conviction one has of waking-reality is a function of one's being in waking-state, not a function of waking-state being more real — is recognizably the same argument in Gauḍapāda and in Śāntideva. Whether Gauḍapāda was in dialogue with Buddhist sources, or whether the early Yogācārins absorbed Vedāntic moves, is among the most debated questions in early-medieval Indian intellectual history.

What is clear is that by the time Tibetan Buddhism receives the Mahāyāna inheritance in the eighth century, the dream-as-emptiness figure is fully developed and ready to be operationalized. Module 2 takes up that operationalization.
