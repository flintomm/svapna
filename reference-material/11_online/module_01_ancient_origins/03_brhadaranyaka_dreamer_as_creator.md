# Module 1, Lesson 3 — Bṛhadāraṇyaka 4.3: The Dreamer as Creator

*If the Māṇḍūkya gives the structure, the Bṛhadāraṇyaka gives the great phenomenological description.*

---

Book 4.3 of the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad — older than the Māṇḍūkya, possibly seventh century BCE — is the locus classicus of Indian thought on dream. The passage is worth quoting at some length. It runs as a dialogue between the sage Yājñavalkya and king Janaka of Videha, who has paid for a teaching with a thousand cows.

## The light within the heart

Janaka asks what the source of light is for a person here. Yājñavalkya answers, in turn: the sun, the moon, fire, the voice. When all of those are gone — when sun, moon, fire have set and the voice is stilled — the source of light is the self.

Janaka presses further. *Which* self? Yājñavalkya replies:

> "It is this person — the one that consists of perception among the vital functions (prāṇa), the one that is the inner light within the heart. He travels across both worlds, being common to both. Sometimes he reflects, sometimes he flutters, *for when he falls asleep he transcends this world, these visible forms of death*." (BU 4.3.7, Olivelle 1998)

## The dreamer as creator

**Then comes the description of dream proper — the locus classicus of the Indian dreamer-as-maker:**

> "Now, this person has just two places — this world and the other world. And there is a third, the place of dream where the two meet. Standing there in the place where the two meet, he sees both those places — this world and the other world. … This is how he dreams. *He takes materials from the entire world and, taking them apart on his own and then on his own putting them back together, he dreams with his own radiance, with his own light.* In that place this person becomes his own light. In that place there are no carriages, there are no tandems, and there are no roads; but he creates for himself carriages, tandems, and roads. In that place there are no joys, pleasures, or delights; but he creates for himself joys, pleasures, and delights. In that place there are no pools, ponds, or rivers; but he creates for himself pools, ponds, and rivers — *for he is a creator*." (BU 4.3.9–10, Olivelle 1998)

The italics on *for he is a creator* are Olivelle's translation choice. The Sanskrit verb behind "creates for himself" is *sṛj* — the verb used elsewhere for divine creation, the emission of the cosmos.

The dreamer, in the Bṛhadāraṇyaka, is not receiving a dream; he is *making* one.

## The golden person, the single goose

The Vedic poetic register that Olivelle preserves makes the point as strikingly as the philosophical one. The dreamer is described, in three verses that survive almost intact across centuries of recension, as "the golden person, the single goose," the soul moving with its own light through a world of its own making:

> "Subduing by sleep the bodily realm, / Remaining awake, he contemplates / the sleeping senses. / Taking the light, he returns to his place — / *The golden person! / The single goose!* … / Traveling in sleep to places high and low, / The god creates many a visible form — / now dallying with women, / now laughing, / now seeing frightful things." (BU 4.3.11, 13, Olivelle 1998)

## Deep sleep as the self embracing itself

The passage continues into a remarkable account of what could now be called the phenomenology of dreaming. The dreamer moves between two realms "as a large fish moves between both banks" (4.3.18); when he passes into the deep sleep beyond dream, he is "as a hawk or an eagle, after flying around in the sky and getting tired, folds its wings and swoops down into its nest" (4.3.19).

And in deep dreamless sleep:

> "It is like this. *As a man embraced by a woman he loves is oblivious to everything within or without, so this person embraced by the self (ātman) consisting of knowledge is oblivious to everything within or without.* … Here a father is not a father, a mother is not a mother, worlds are not worlds, gods are not gods, and Vedas are not Vedas. Here a thief is not a thief, an abortionist is not an abortionist, an outcaste is not an outcaste, a pariah is not a pariah, a recluse is not a recluse, and an ascetic is not an ascetic. Neither the good nor the bad follows him, for he has now passed beyond all sorrows of the heart." (BU 4.3.21–22, Olivelle 1998)

## What the dreamer is

What Yājñavalkya offers Janaka — and what he gives the entire later tradition — is a sustained argument that dream is *evidentially significant*. The dreamer takes the materials of waking and recombines them into a world. The deepness of dreamless sleep is the embrace of the self by itself.

Doniger's gloss on this passage is unusually careful:

> "This text has not yet reached the extreme idealism of certain later schools (particularly Mahāyāna Buddhism) that suggest that all perception is the result of projection; rather, *in one particular liminal state the dreamer is able to understand the relationship between the two worlds, both of them equally real and unreal*" (Doniger 1984).

The dreamer is the bricoleur of the dream world, taking apart the elements of the outside world and rebuilding them inside, "without affecting their reality status."

This is the move that Gauḍapāda will systematize seven or eight centuries later in his *Māṇḍūkya-kārikā*, and that the Tibetan illusory-body practitioners will operationalize in the second millennium. But the move is here first, in this passage. The dreamer creates, and the dream-world's apparent solidity is a function of the dreamer's own activity.

It is worth pausing to feel the force of this. In the seventh century BCE, in an India that did not yet have writing in the form now recognized, a sage on the banks of the Ganges has worked out — by what must have been patient introspection over years — that the dream is a domain of *agency*. He is the one constructing it. The materials are familiar (carriages, ponds, women, Vedas); the construction is his.

**Every later Indian dream-tradition either accepts this claim, modifies it, or argues against it. None ignores it.**

Two hundred and fifty generations later, when Padmasambhava in eighth-century Tibet writes practice instructions on the illusory body that ask the student to recognize that the apparent solidity of the dream-world is a function of consciousness, he is operating on Yājñavalkya's premise. The dreamer creates. The recognition that the dream is one's own creation is the entry point for the recognition that the waking world has the same character.
