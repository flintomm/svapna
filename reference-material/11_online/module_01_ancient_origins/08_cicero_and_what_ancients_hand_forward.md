## Aristotle on divination

The companion treatise to Aristotle's *De Insomniis*, *De Divinatione per Somnum*, takes up the question whether dreams reliably foretell the future.

Aristotle's tone is balanced and skeptical. He cannot, he writes, "lightly either dismiss it with contempt or give it implicit confidence" (462b, Beare). The popular conviction has weight; the philosophical reasons for skepticism also have weight. He arrives, in the end, at a position more skeptical than not.

Three of his moves have lasted.

**First**, the principled refusal to accept divine sending of dreams as a sufficient explanation:

> "*it is absurd to combine the idea that the sender of such dreams should be God with the fact that those to whom he sends them are not the best and wisest, but merely commonplace persons.*" (462b, Beare)

**Second**, the proposal that some dreams are *causes* of subsequent waking action — a thought that has come up in the night creates a path that the day's action then follows — rather than tokens of a fixed future. The dream and the day are continuous; an inclination first felt in dream may become an action by day. There is no need to invoke the future for this; the present mind is already at work.

**Third**, the famous observation that most apparent dream-prophecies are coincidence:

> "Most [so-called prophetic] dreams are, however, to be classed as mere coincidences, especially all such as are extravagant, and those in the fulfilment of which the dreamers have no initiative, such as in the case of a sea-fight, or of things taking place far away. … *the probability is, rather, that many such things should happen.* As, then, one's mentioning a particular person is neither token nor cause of this person's presenting himself, so, in the parallel instance, the dream is, to him who has seen it, neither token nor cause of its [so-called] fulfilment, but a mere coincidence." (463b, Beare)

The combinatorics of human life are such that *some* dreams will appear to come true. The philosopher does not treat this as evidence of prophecy; he treats it as evidence of the lawfulness of coincidence.

Aristotle ends with a famous concession: dreams are *daemonic* — *daimonia* — but not divine. "Nature their cause is divinely planned, though not itself divine" (463b).

Most dreams are not sent. Some, by analysis of internal states the dreamer cannot quite bring to consciousness, may genuinely give early warning of approaching disease. But the wholesale prophetic apparatus — the divinely-sent dream, the oracle in the night — is not philosophically defensible.

## Cicero: the Roman culmination of dream-skepticism

Cicero's *De Divinatione* (44 BCE) is the most rigorous classical critique of dream-prophecy that survives. Book 1 is given to Cicero's brother Quintus, defending the Stoic position that divination through dreams is real and that the gods send signs. Book 2 is Cicero's own response, dismantling Quintus's argument step by step.

### The Stoic syllogism dismantled

The skeptical argument develops in stages. Cicero first concedes the rhetorical force of the popular conviction; he then dissects it.

**The Stoic syllogism — *if gods exist, they care for us; if they care for us, they send signs; therefore divination exists* — proceeds, he argues, from premises none of which is established:**

> *"Si sunt di neque ante declarant hominibus quae futura sint, aut non diligunt homines, aut quid eventurum sit ignorant…"*
>
> "If there are gods and they do not declare in advance to men what will happen, then either they do not love men, or they do not know what will happen, or they think it of no concern to men to know what is to come, or they think it beneath their majesty to signal these things to men, *or even the gods themselves cannot signal them*." (Cicero, *De Div.* II.49–50)

Cicero will go on to deny each of the assumptions in turn. The gods may indeed not love men in the operative sense — Epicurus thought they did not, and his arguments are not refuted by mere assertion. The gods may indeed be ignorant of the future, if the future is genuinely contingent.

The gods may indeed think it of no concern to men to know what is to come; Dicaearchus, Cicero notes, had argued that *not knowing* such things is better than knowing them. The Stoic argument, on each of these points, simply assumes what is in dispute.

### The argument from coincidence

The next phase is methodological. Most apparent divinations through dream, Cicero argues, are coincidences, and the random scatter of dream-content over a lifetime guarantees that some dreams will appear to come true. The argument is unusually clean for an ancient text, and reads — as Mary Beard among others has noted — almost like a piece of Hume.

The decisive move — the one that distinguishes Cicero from a merely empirical skeptic — is his observation that accepting the Stoic argument from the occasional truth of dreams leaves no principled basis for rejecting any of the other arts of divination.

The same combinatorial accident that makes some dreams come true also makes some haruspices, some augurs, some lot-casters seem prescient; accepting the dream evidence requires accepting the rest, and rejecting the rest leaves no good reason to accept the dream.

**What Cicero does *not* do, importantly, is deny that dreams happen, or that some dreams reflect significant inner states. He denies their evidential value as predictions of an external future. The line is drawn precisely: the dream is real; its prophetic interpretation is not.**

### What Aristotle and Cicero hand forward

What Aristotle and Cicero, between them, give the Western tradition is a stance toward dream that is broadly naturalistic, broadly skeptical of supernatural interpretation, attentive to the body's role in producing dream-content, and willing to allow the dream a small but non-zero place in the diagnostic toolkit of medicine.

This is a different inheritance from the Indian one. Aristotle does not treat the dream as a window on the structure of consciousness. Cicero does not treat the dream as a path to recognition. They treat the dream as a phenomenon to be explained and a class of claims to be evaluated.

## What the ancient world hands forward

By the close of the first millennium of the Common Era — the period at which the Tibetan dream-yoga lineage proper begins to take shape — the inheritance from the ancient world is layered and large.

**From Vedic and Upaniṣadic Hinduism**: the four-state model, the analogy between dream and waking, the recognition that the dreamer is the maker of the dream-world, the foundational sense that the structure of dream is a structure of mind itself, and the vocabulary (*svapna*, *suṣupti*, *taijasa*, *prājña*, *prapañca*, *turīya*, *ātman*) in which every later Indian dream-tradition will think.

**From Indian Buddhism**: a careful phenomenological taxonomy of dreams (the four-or-six causes, drawn from Indian humoral medicine and supplemented with the categories of "habit" and "portent"); a sophisticated Abhidhamma analysis of dream-consciousness as functional rather than karmically agentive in the same register as waking thought; a working position on moral responsibility for dream actions that neither inflates the dream into a theatre of moral failure nor dismisses it as mere noise.

**From the Mahāyāna**: dream as the working analogy for the empty, dependently-arisen, illusion-like character of all experience. Śāntideva's verses on the equality of long and short lives, on the body as a heap of conditions, on the equal deceptiveness of dream-presence and dream-absence, are the verses the Tibetan tradition will inherit, internalize, and operationalize. Dream yoga, when it emerges in its developed Tibetan form, is operating on the Mahāyāna's premise: recognition of the dream-status of waking experience is itself the path.

**From the Greco-Roman tradition** (which the Indian-Tibetan synthesis does not directly inherit, but which shapes the Western tradition that will much later rejoin the Asian conversation): the framing of dream as a natural phenomenon to be explained; Aristotle's residual-movement account, with its surprisingly modern observations on the persistence of sense-impressions and on the recognition of dreaming-while-dreaming; Cicero's principled argument that the apparent prophetic value of dreams is statistical accident in clothing.

### Resisting the temptation to over-converge

It is worth resisting the temptation to over-converge these inheritances. They are not saying the same thing in different vocabularies.

The Indian seers ask what *the dreamer* is; the Greek philosophers ask what *the dream* is. The Mahāyāna treats dream as the figure for liberation; Aristotle treats dream as the figure for nothing in particular, just one more phenomenon for the natural philosopher to explain.

The Tibetan synthesis that begins in the second millennium of the Common Era will draw almost entirely on the Indian inheritance and will have to be told about Aristotle by Europeans much later. The Western lucid-dream literature of the late nineteenth and twentieth centuries, when it picks up the question, will start from Aristotle and Cicero and find itself, somewhat to its surprise, discovering moves that the Indian tradition had already developed two thousand years earlier.

What unites them — what makes it possible to call this a single module at all — is the seriousness with which each tradition takes the dream as something worth thinking about.

**None of these thinkers thought the dream was nothing.**

That conviction — that the dream is something worth thinking about carefully — is the most important inheritance the ancient world hands forward. Module 2 takes up the Tibetan synthesis that operationalized the Indian half of the inheritance into the practice now called dream yoga.
