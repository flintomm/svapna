# Module 1, Lesson 2 — The Māṇḍūkya: Four States, One Syllable

*Twelve verses, no narrative frame, no dialogue. The shortest of the principal Upaniṣads, and the philosophical bedrock of nearly every later Asian discussion of consciousness across states.*

---

Composed sometime between roughly 800 and 500 BCE, the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad does in twenty-five lines of Sanskrit what the longer Upaniṣads spread across hundreds of verses. After the Māṇḍūkya, the four-state framework — waking, dreaming, deep sleep, and a fourth — is not one model among others. It is *the* model.

The text begins with a claim that everything is the syllable Om: "What has become, what is becoming, what will become — verily, all of this is OM. And what is beyond these three states of the world of time — that too, verily, is OM" (verse 1, Mandukya_Upanishad.txt). The next verse identifies the self with brahman and announces that the self has four quarters (*catuṣ-pāt*). The four are then enumerated.

## The four quarters

The first quarter is *Vaiśvānara*, the universal in waking form. "Its field is the waking state. Its consciousness is outward-turned. It is seven-limbed and nineteen-mouthed. It enjoys gross objects" (verse 3).

**The second quarter, *taijasa*, names the awareness present in dream:**

> "Its field is the dream state. Its consciousness is *inward-turned*. It is seven-limbed and nineteen-mouthed. It enjoys subtle objects." (verse 4, Mandukya_Upanishad.txt)

What the verse establishes — and what the entire later tradition will build on — is that the dream state has the same architecture as the waking state. Many limbs, many openings, a world of objects to engage with. The difference is what kind of object: *gross* in waking, *subtle* in dream.

The dream is a full state of consciousness with its own constitution.

The third quarter is *prājña*, deep sleep, "where one asleep neither desires anything nor beholds any dream … one becomes undivided, an undifferentiated mass of consciousness, consisting of bliss and feeding on bliss. His mouth is consciousness" (verse 5).

The Sanskrit name means "the knower"; it is given here even though there is, in deep sleep, no particular thing being known. Something is doing the work of knowing even when nothing in particular is being known. This is the move that licenses everything in the later sleep-yoga literature.

## The fourth: turīya

**The fourth quarter — the verse to which the entire text builds — is the most carefully written:**

> "That is known as the fourth quarter: neither inward-turned nor outward-turned consciousness, nor the two together; not an undifferentiated mass of consciousness; neither knowing, nor unknowing; invisible, ineffable, intangible, devoid of characteristics, inconceivable, indefinable, its sole essence being the consciousness of its own Self; the coming to rest of all relative existence; utterly quiet; peaceful; blissful: without a second: *this is the Ātman, the Self; this is to be realised*." (verse 7)

The Sanskrit is a sequence of negations: *na antaḥ-prajñam, na bahiṣ-prajñam, nobhayataḥ-prajñam …* The negations are not technicalities. Each cancels a possible misreading.

The fourth — *caturtham*, later standardized as *turīya* — is not aware of an inner world (so it is not the dream state); not aware of an outer world (so it is not waking); not the unified condensed awareness (so it is not deep sleep); not all of these together. The text refuses to put it alongside the others. It is the awareness *in which* the other three appear.

And it is described positively, in the end, with four words that recur through later Vedānta: *prapañca-upaśama, śānta, śiva, advaita* — the coming to rest of all multiplicity, peaceful, auspicious, non-dual.

## Om as the structure of consciousness

Verses 8–12 then do something that looks ornamental but is not: they map the four states onto the syllable Om. *A* is Vaiśvānara; *U* is taijasa; *M* is prājña; the silence after the spoken syllable is the fourth. "The fourth is soundless: unutterable, a quieting down of all relative manifestations, blissful, peaceful, non-dual" (verse 12).

The Māṇḍūkya is, in this sense, both a metaphysical text and a contemplative manual. The structure of Om is the structure of consciousness; sounding Om and attending to its sequence — open vowel, closing vowel, closed nasal, silence — is a graded attention to the four states.

Most translations emphasize the metaphysics. The practice is in the structure.

## The same awareness through all four

What makes the Māṇḍūkya foundational for everything that follows is not just its terminology — *svapna* for the dream state, *taijasa* for its character, *suṣupti* and *prājña* for deep sleep, *prapañca* for the manifest manifold, *turīya* for the fourth — but its claim that the same single awareness runs through all four conditions.

**Wendy Doniger states the central claim plainly in *Dreams, Illusion, and Other Realities*:**

> "These texts speak of four states of being: waking, dreaming, dreamless sleep (all natural states), and the supernatural, transcendent fourth state, the identity with Godhead. … the Upaniṣadic fourth stage added to the triad is the whole point of the original analysis, called simply *turīya*, the fourth: it is, in a sense, 'the first three all in all,' *the true state toward which the other three point*" (Doniger 1984).

The Mahāyāna's "all form is like a dream," the Tibetan tradition's clear-light-of-sleep, Wallace's "pristine awareness through the working analogy of dream," even Evan Thompson's contemporary philosophical treatment of *Waking, Dreaming, Being* — all of them are working within the structure the Māṇḍūkya laid down twenty-five hundred years before any of them was written.

The same single awareness, running through the four conditions. Every later Indian dream-tradition either accepts this framework, modifies it, or argues against it. None ignores it.
