# Module 1, Lesson 5 — The Milindapañha: Who Is Dreaming, and Is He Asleep?

*A Pāli dialogue between the Bactrian Greek king Menander and the monk Nāgasena. The most sustained early Buddhist treatment of dream that survives, and the place where the question becomes precisely philosophical.*

---

The *Milindapañha* — composed in the first century BCE — preserves a remarkable encounter. Menander (Pāli: Milinda) is a Bactrian Greek king who ruled territories in what is now Pakistan and Afghanistan in the second century BCE; Nāgasena is a Buddhist monk; the dialogue between them is the most sustained early Buddhist treatment of dream that the tradition preserves.

N.K.G. Mendis's translation, in *The Questions of King Milinda*, keeps the dialogue's careful pace.

## The king's question

The relevant passage is in the dilemmas, Book IV, where Milinda asks:

> "Revered Nāgasena, men and women in this world see dreams pleasant and evil, things they have seen before and things they have not seen before, things they have done before and things they have not done before, dreams peaceful and terrible, dreams of matters near to them and distant from them, full of many shapes and innumerable colours. *What is this that men call a dream, and who is it who dreams it?*" (Mil IV, Mendis 1993)

## The six causes

**Nāgasena's answer is the locus classicus of the Buddhist taxonomy:**

> "This that is called a dream, sire, is a suggestion that comes into the focus of the mind. There are six kinds of people who see dreams: the person who suffers from wind, the bilious person, the phlegmatic person, the person possessed of a deva, the person influenced by his own habits, and the person who sees a dream as a portent. *Among these, sire, only the last kind is true, the rest are false.*"

The Pesala translation gives the same enumeration in a slightly leaner register: "There are six kinds of dreams. A person affected by wind sees a dream, a person affected by bile, by phlegm, by a deity, by their own habits, by a premonition. It is only the last of these that is true, all the others are false" (*Debate of King Milinda* §75).

The Milinda's six causes are an expansion of the Visuddhimagga commentary's four — the first three humoral, the fourth divine — by adding two further sources. Dreams from one's own habitual mental tendencies have a clear phenomenological warrant: most dreams are continuations of waking preoccupations.

Dreams as genuine premonitions are admitted, but the passage carefully distances itself from any simple endorsement of the prophetic dream: only the *last* kind is true, the rest are false. The taxonomy admits the prophetic dream and refuses to inflate it.

## The mirror analogy

What follows in Milinda is what gives the dialogue its philosophical weight. The king asks whether the dream-mind, on its own, knows that what it sees is a portent. Nāgasena's reply uses the Buddhist analogy of the mirror:

> "It is not, sire, that his mind, going along of its own accord, seeks for that portent, nor does anyone else come and tell him of it, but that very portent comes into the focus of his mind. *It is like a mirror that does not go anywhere to seek for a reflection, nor does anyone else bringing a reflection put it on the mirror, but from wherever the reflection comes it appears in the mirror.*" (Mil IV, Mendis)

The mirror is a recurrent image throughout the dream literature, Buddhist and Hindu alike — Aristotle will use a closely related figure in his account of residual sense-impressions, as the next lessons show.

For the Milinda, the figure does specific work: it locates the dream-image not in the dreamer's intention but in a passive reception, and so it cleans the moral attribution. The dreamer does not *will* the prophetic content; it simply appears.

## Neither awake nor asleep

**The most philosophically penetrating part of the Milinda exchange comes next:**

> "Revered Nāgasena, does he who sees a dream see it when he is awake or asleep?"
>
> "Neither the one, sire, nor yet the other. *It is in the interval between being drowsy and not having yet reached the unconscious state that one sees a dream.* When a man is drowsy his mind is entering the unconscious state; a mind that is entering the unconscious state does not function; a mind that is not functioning is not susceptible to happiness or suffering. There is no dream for one who is not susceptible. When the mind is functioning it sees a dream. As, sire, in dense darkness where there is no light, no shadow can be seen on even a well-burnished mirror, even so, sire, a mind that has entered the unconscious state is not functioning though it is in the physical frame. … *As the mirror, sire, so should the physical frame be understood, as the darkness sleep, as the light the mind.*" (Mil IV, Mendis)

Nāgasena's answer is technical Abhidhamma cast in dialogue form. The dream is not seen in waking; it is not seen in the *bhavaṅga* proper (the unconscious life-continuum); it is seen in what he calls the *kapi-niddā* — the "monkey-sleep," a half-asleep, half-awake liminal phase.

## Two consequences

The framing has two consequences worth marking.

**First**, it locates the dream phenomenologically rather than ontologically: dream is a particular *condition* of mind, not a separate world.

This puts Nāgasena at some distance from the Upaniṣadic Yājñavalkya (Lesson 3), who treated dream as a third place between this world and the next, with the dreamer moving between them as a fish between two banks. For Nāgasena, the dream is not a place. It is a condition.

**Second**, by tying dream to a half-conscious liminal phase rather than to the deep unconscious, the Milinda quietly resolves the Buddhist problem of *moral responsibility for dream actions*. The Vinaya, the monastic rule, is famously casuistic about nocturnal emissions and other dream-occurrences.

The Milinda's framing is consistent with the Vinaya consensus that mental acts in dream are not karmically equivalent to waking acts because the dream-mind is not fully functional in the way the waking mind is. Dream activity is real activity. It is not, on this reading, fully agentive activity.

What follows from this is a particular kind of contemplative stance toward dream: it is taken seriously as data, but not as a private theatre for moral self-reproach. The dream is mind functioning at half-capacity in the gap between waking and unconscious.

The mind that is "neither drowsy nor unconscious" is the mind that dreams. And what it dreams reflects the conditions that brought it to that liminal state — humoral, habitual, occasionally portentous, occasionally divine.

## What the dialogue hands forward

The Milinda therefore gives the early Buddhist tradition its working orientation toward dream: a phenomenology that is humble about the dream's metaphysical pretensions, careful about its moral implications, and confident enough in its analytical apparatus to admit a class of true premonitory dreams without reducing the rest to noise.

This is the framework the Tibetan dream-yoga literature will inherit, develop, and finally invert. The Tibetan tradition will keep the analytical apparatus and the careful moral framing. It will reject the conclusion that the dream-mind is necessarily half-functional.

**The lucid dreamer in the Tibetan tradition is fully conscious, fully agentive, and fully responsible for what he does in the dream — but, as the Milinda has already established, this is not the *ordinary* condition of dream.** The Tibetan tradition will work to produce, by training, a state Nāgasena did not know was producible by training.
